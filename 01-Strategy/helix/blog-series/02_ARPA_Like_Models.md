# Pasteur's Quadrant and the Heilmeier Catechism

## What ARPA-like Models Got Right, and Where Even the Best Ones Run Out

If you asked a hundred people in metascience which institutional model has produced the most per-dollar scientific impact in the last 50 years, the plurality answer would be DARPA. The internet, GPS, stealth, drone autonomy, mRNA vaccine platforms: an unreasonable fraction of the technologies we now take for granted trace back through a DARPA program somewhere. So it is not surprising that the post-COVID wave of institutional experimentation has produced several ARPA-shaped attempts to replicate the magic elsewhere: ARPA-H (health), Wellcome Leap (global health philanthropy), ARIA (UK general-purpose), and an emerging federal push toward funding independent research organizations through NSF Tech Labs.

This post is about what these organizations share, where they differ, and why, for all their strengths, even the best ARPA-like structure leaves most of a healthcare moonshot unaddressed.

## Two ideas that do the work

ARPA-like organizations rest on two ideas that sound unrelated but are actually deeply connected.

**Pasteur's Quadrant.** Donald Stokes' 1997 book *Pasteur's Quadrant* argued that the traditional division of research into "basic" (curiosity-driven) and "applied" (problem-driven) missed the most consequential category: work that is *simultaneously* curiosity-driven and problem-driven. Pasteur studied microbes because he was curious about microbes. He also studied them because French wine was spoiling. Both motives drove the science forward. Stokes called the combination "use-inspired basic research," and argued that the highest-yield scientific work consistently lives in this quadrant.

ARPA-like organizations operate in Pasteur's Quadrant by charter. They do not fund pure basic research that has no theory of use; they do not fund applied engineering that has no fundamental advance. They look for programs that do both at once, because those are the ones that historically produce outsized impact.

**The Heilmeier Catechism.** George Heilmeier, DARPA director from 1975 to 1977, developed an eight-question discipline that every proposed program had to answer before receiving funding. The questions are deceptively simple:

1. What are you trying to do, in absolutely no jargon?
2. How is it done today, and what are the limits of current practice?
3. What is new in your approach, and why do you think it will be successful?
4. Who cares? If you are successful, what difference will it make?
5. What are the risks?
6. How much will it cost?
7. How long will it take?
8. What are the mid-term and final "exams" to check for success?

The power of the catechism is not in the questions themselves but in what they force. Question 1 is the highest-information. "Articulate your objectives using absolutely no jargon" is a filter; work that cannot be explained clearly has usually not been *thought about* clearly. Question 4 is the second highest-information. "Who cares?" is the filter that removes technically interesting but practically irrelevant proposals. Questions 5 through 8 force the quantitative discipline that distinguishes ambition from aspiration.

Together, Pasteur's Quadrant tells you *where* to work; the Heilmeier Catechism tells you *whether* the work is real.

## Five structural features, shared across all ARPA-like orgs

Beyond the philosophical backbone, all the ARPA-inheritors share five structural features:

1. **Empowered program directors on term limits.** Three-to-five-year tours, recruited from the research community, with authority to start, redirect, and terminate projects.
2. **Active management, not fire-and-forget.** Program directors stay in close contact with performers. If a project isn't working, resources get redirected rather than spent to exhaustion.
3. **Portfolio logic.** Individual projects are expected to fail at high rates. The portfolio succeeds if even a fraction of its bets produce outsized impact.
4. **Time-bounded urgency.** Programs have end dates. "We will figure this out eventually" is not an acceptable status.
5. **Jargon-free articulation.** Every proposal passes Heilmeier Question 1 or it does not get funded.

## Four implementations, four distinct trade-offs

Here is where the organizations diverge, and where the lessons live.

### ARPA-H: can the model work at civilian health scale?

ARPA-H was the most ambitious ARPA adaptation to date. Formally established in 2022 with $1B+ initial appropriation, now at several billion cumulative, it operates through a distributed "ARPANET-H" network (hubs in Boston, Cambridge, Durham, Dallas) and four mission offices. Individual programs run from $50M to $500M over three to five years, which is genuinely platform-scale.

What ARPA-H has done that DARPA did not: it has explicitly embedded equity (the Proactive Health and Scalable Solutions offices are structured around underserved-population access from the start, not as a retrofit), it has invested in structured patient and community input into program design, and it has embedded commercialization and deployment planning into program design itself, reflecting lessons from DARPA-funded technologies that reached defense applications but struggled to reach civilian markets.

As of 2026, ARPA-H is still too early for definitive output evaluation. The more interesting question, for our purposes, is what ARPA-H *cannot* do. Programs end on schedule. The organization does not continue operating the platforms it funds. Technology transition planning is in every program, but there is no standing ARPA-H body that owns the post-program trajectory of a given technology.

### Wellcome Leap: what happens when you strip the government constraints?

Wellcome Leap, launched in 2020, took the ARPA model and removed the government overhead. Its most durable contribution is probably institutional: the Health Breakthrough Network (HBNet), a standing network of academic and commercial performers, supported by two pre-negotiated standardized agreements called MARFA (Master Academic Research Funding Agreement) and CORFA (Commercial Research Funding Agreement).

Why this matters: typical ARPA-H or NIH contracts take 6 to 18 months of back-and-forth to negotiate IP ownership, publication rights, data sharing, and indirect-cost structures. HBNet collapses that to days or weeks because the terms are pre-negotiated. This is a real institutional innovation with broad applicability beyond Leap itself. Any mission-driven healthcare organization should be thinking about how to adopt or extend this model for its own collaboration infrastructure.

Leap's honest limits: it does not operate laboratories, which limits its ability to build physical platform infrastructure. It operates globally, which is a strength for comparative work and a weakness when regulatory pathways differ across jurisdictions.

### ARIA: legal tolerance of failure

ARIA (the UK Advanced Research and Invention Agency) was set up with a lean charter, an £800M over-five-years budget, and a deliberate legal exemption from standard UK procurement rules. ARIA's most distinctive contribution is that it makes open tolerance of failure an explicit *legal* feature, not just a cultural one. Program directors are indemnified. Failure does not trigger the public-sector accountability mechanisms that would otherwise make genuine risk-taking career-ending.

The lesson, which generalizes: risk tolerance is easier to write about than to legally operationalize. Most organizations that claim to tolerate failure have HR and procurement systems that punish it. ARIA's achievement is aligning the legal structure with the cultural aspiration.

### NSF Tech Labs: the emerging federal answer

The NSF Tech Labs initiative, rolling out in 2026, is the first federal mechanism explicitly designed to fund Independent Research Organizations, including FROs, with capital to *operate* rather than run project-by-project grants. This is significant. For the first time, a federal agency is treating "fund a new kind of research organization" as a first-class activity rather than only "fund a project inside an existing one." The downstream implications, for FROs, for PARPA-style independent agencies, and potentially for healthcare moonshot organizations, are still being worked out.

## Where even the best ARPA-like structure runs out

Here is the structural limitation that motivates the rest of the series. Every ARPA-like program, however well-designed, ends on schedule. That is the whole point. Three to seven years of funding, a defined set of milestones, and then the program closes and the performers continue independently.

For the kinds of scientific infrastructure a Focused Research Organization ships (an open dataset, an open platform, a methodology) this is fine. The output has been delivered; the community can pick it up.

For a healthcare technology that must be validated in a clinical trial, approved by a regulator, manufactured under Good Manufacturing Practice, sold through a reimbursement pathway, and deployed equitably across populations, "the program is ending, and the performers continue independently" is not an answer. Each of those downstream steps requires institutional structure, staffing, and capital that no ARPA-like program carries.

The question the next two posts examine is whether the Focused Research Organization and the hybrid nonprofit-plus-PBC models fill this gap. They partially do. They also do not, in ways that matter.
