### Strategic objectives that strong proposals should address (majority, not all)

1. Build Sector-Defining Platforms (VLSI/Internet/PCR/LED-class)
2. Generate Outcomes Beyond Papers (platform adoption, TRL progression, startup emergence, investment interest incl. from NSF Tech Accelerator)
3. De-risk Emerging Technologies (bridge not-yet-investable to investable)
4. Identify and Address Market Needs (industry/philanthropy partnerships)
5. Lead and Leverage Novel Types of Partnerships

### Phase 0 selection criteria (informative, not part of RFI response)

1. Team Qualifications and Structure (a: technical/leadership/creative expertise; b: legal & organizational structure appropriate, autonomy & flexibility)
2. Mission Clarity and Outcomes (a: addresses challenge/gap/bottleneck in a specified topic; b: aligned with strategic objectives; c: activities/outcomes well-defined and appropriately ambitious)

### Phase 1 / 2 selection criteria (add over Phase 0)

- Team: previously identified gaps being filled; funding amount aligns with scope/size/infrastructure/growth trajectory
- Mission: technology landscape justifies dedicated team; milestones well-defined and ambitious; meaningful progress during prior award; funding alignment

### RFI prompts to answer (Section II "Questions")

Format: no stated page limit, no word limit. Submission is a single email/document. All questions are "optional" in the sense that the RFI is voluntary, but any respondent is expected to answer the set coherently.

1. **Organization overview (required)** — Briefly describe your organization, capabilities, services offered, ownership model, and size.
2. **Comparative advantage of Tech Labs model (required)** — What, if any, substantive comparative advantage (vs. standard grants and other existing NSF programs) could the NSF Tech Labs program model provide in efforts to accelerate and advance U.S. competitiveness — either across various key technologies or within a specific technology focus area?
3. **Could your team become an NSF Tech Lab? (Y/N)** — If yes, answer 3a.i–v; if no, answer 3b.i–ii.
   - 3a.i. What is your vision for substantially advancing the state of practice in your chosen topic of focus, and why is the Tech Labs funding mechanism a superior approach to pursuing that vision?
   - 3a.ii. Describe your team's experience, including technology translation from early-stage academic research to industry/use.
   - 3a.iii. Describe the ideal management and structure of your potential Tech Lab.
   - 3a.iv. Has your team engaged with nonprofit and philanthropic organizations? (Y/N) If yes, to what end or for what purpose?
   - 3a.v. Has your team engaged with industry organizations, including venture capital firms, venture studios, incubators, accelerators, startups, mid-size and large organizations? (Y/N) If yes, to what end or for what purpose?
   - 3b.i. What existing technology (translation) team(s) exist(s) that could become an NSF Tech Lab?
   - 3b.ii. Describe the ideal scope, experience, management, and structure of a Tech Lab team.
4. **Program design (required)** — What program design choices would contribute to the success of the Tech Labs mechanism? What program design choices would present potential barriers?
   - 4a. Which types of teams and organizations should be considered eligible to apply? What restrictions on team eligibility should be in place to maximize speed and ensure novel impact?
   - 4b. Is the proposed timeline for Phase 0 (9 months), Phase 1 (24 months), and Phase 2 (24+ months) well-calibrated? If not, what adjustments should be made and why?
   - 4c. How should IP rights be structured to support maximum success and impact?
   - 4d. What degree of independence is optimal to ensure the flexibility, freedom, and speed required for the Tech Labs initiative? How should NSF define team independence?
   - 4e. How should funding be allotted to each proposed Tech Labs? What factors — team size, team expertise, infrastructure needs, growth trajectory — should NSF consider?
5. **Synergy with industry/philanthropy (required)** — What opportunities do you see for synergy with R&D efforts that are or could be funded by industry or philanthropic organizations? What partnership structure would allow Tech Labs to leverage federal and private support for maximum benefit?
6. **Translational problems addressable in 3–7 years (required)** — What translational problems, challenges and/or bottlenecks could be addressed within 3–7 years with this program design? Answers can be broad or specific.
7. **Feedback (Section III, optional)** — Any feedback, questions, areas of concern, suggestions, or other information to be considered as part of this RFI.

### Anticipated future full-proposal data to pre-stage (from Section I)

### PHO interest areas (ARPA-H-SOL-24-106)

1. Novel prevention, detection, and prophylactic treatment methods for disease:
   - i. Prophylactic approaches to prevention of diseases and harmful disease outcomes.
   - ii. Methods for continuous and widespread sensing of health-state and early disease indicators deployable at population scale.
   - iii. Novel and scalable methods for early detection of disease/illness, including low/no-cost sensing modalities.
2. Population-level approaches to increase adoption of prevention- and wellness behaviors:
   - i. Methods to inform and educate individuals about healthy behaviors (lifestyle, preventive medicine).
   - ii. Methods to incentivize individuals to adopt and maintain healthy behaviors.
   - iii. Novel approaches to increasing individual health spans and independence in absence of disease.
   - iv. Early indicators of disease- and pre-disease states; inexpensive/effective proactive-health surrogates; low-cost, high-uptake mental-health resiliency and mindfulness-building methods.
3. System innovation for the delivery of proactive health outcomes:
   - i. Novel, robust, predictive surrogates for long-term health outcomes with associated epidemiological models.
   - ii. Valuation models for long-term treatment effects (vaccination, screening, public-health interventions).
   - iii. New funding and delivery models for preventative interventions.
4. (Note clause) Other revolutionary submissions meeting PHO goals considered even if outside listed areas.

### HSF interest areas (ARPA-H-SOL-24-104)

1. **Breakthrough Technologies** — paradigm-shifting diagnostics/treatments: maternal-fetal medicine; genetic/epigenetic/cellular/tissue/organ replacement therapies; interventions that target and reverse disease pathogenesis or enhance plasticity; definitive diagnosis/cure of chronic diseases (diabetes, autoimmune, neurodegenerative, cancer); brain-circuit-mapping at precision/scale/accessibility.
2. **Transformative Tools** — tools that counter idiosyncratic/off-target/chronic drug effects; bionic/biohybrid integration devices; site-selective neuromodulation; synthetic biology with novel logic/control/targeting; imaging/tech from physics/chemistry frontier (cost, size, portability, pediatric); integrated sensing + therapy delivery for chronic / mental-health / SUD.
3. **Platform Systems** — novel molecular platforms (targeting, spatiotemporal precision, off-target mitigation); routinized mammalian/microbial cellular engineering; AI × HPC (incl. quantum) × biology including de novo biomolecule design; revolutionary omics platforms with unprecedented spatial/temporal resolution.
4. (Note clause) Other high-quality revolutionary submissions meeting HSF goals still considered.

### Evaluation criteria (both ISOs, §5.2)

1. **Overall Scientific and Technical Merit** — innovative, feasible, complete; tasks/technical elements in logical sequence; deliverables clearly defined; major risks and mitigations clearly defined and feasible.
2. **Potential Contribution and Relevance to ARPA-H Mission** — future R&D / commercial / clinical applications; unmet-need addressing; multidisciplinary; IP structure + software-component commercialization potential and open-source alignment; commercialization & transition strategy if applicable.
3. **Proposer's Capabilities and/or Related Experience** — expertise + experience; ability to deliver on performance/budget/schedule; similar completed/ongoing efforts fully described (government + commercial).
4. **Cost/Price/Budget Assessment** — cost aligns with technical solution; reflects understanding of resources/schedule/risks/effort; sufficient documentation for efficient evaluation.

### Phase A — Solution Summary (MANDATORY, gating; submitted first)

**Formatting limits (from Appendix A):** 6-page limit; English; sans-serif ≥11pt (smaller allowed in figures/tables/charts); Calibri/Arial/Avenir Next Pro Light readability. **Citations do not count toward the 6-page limit.** Submission via ARPA-H Solution Submission Portal.

#### Cover-page metadata (Appendix A cover block) — required

All fields required unless noted.

1. Innovative Solutions Opening Title (ISO name; e.g., PHO or HSF MO ISO)
2. Solution Summary Title
3. Submitter Organization
4. Type of Organization — checkboxes: ☐ For-Profit · ☐ Non-profit · ☐ Academia (choose all that apply)
5. Technical Point of Contact (POC) — Name, Mailing Address, Telephone, Email
6. Administrative POC — Name, Mailing Address, Telephone, Email
7. Estimated Project Duration — ___ months
8. Total Basis of Estimate — Total: $______
9. Resource Sharing (if applicable) — Gov't % ___ $______; Performer % ___ $______
10. Place(s) of Performance
11. Sub-Awardee and Consultant Team Members — per member: Organization Name; Technical POC Name; Organization Type(s) (For-Profit/Non-profit/Academia checkboxes). Rows repeated as needed (template shows 4 slots).

#### Solution Summary body sections (Appendix A) — required, counted against 6-page limit

1. **Concept Summary** — Describe the solution summary concept with minimal jargon and explain how it addresses the ISO interest areas.
2. **Innovation and Impact** — Clearly identify outcomes sought and/or problems to be solved. Describe how the proposed effort represents an innovative and potentially revolutionary solution to the ISO interest areas. Explain disruptive potential vs. existing or emerging technologies and how approach goes far beyond current capabilities. Provide a quantitative metrics table that compares the proposed concept to current and emerging technologies, which may include:
   - A progression of increasingly complex technical challenges.
   - State of the art / emerging technology "baseline."
   - Aggressive metrics for each year of the proposed project.
   - Summary of specific outcomes from the proposed project or concept.
3. **Proposed Work** — Describe final deliverable(s), key interim milestones, and overall technical approach. Discuss alternative approaches considered (if any) and why the proposed is most appropriate. Describe background / theory / simulation / modeling / experimental data / sound engineering & scientific practices that support the approach. Provide specific examples of supporting data and/or appropriate citations. Identify adoption challenges. Describe why the effort is a significant technical challenge and the key technical risks. At a minimum address:
   - Does the approach require one or more entirely new technical developments to succeed?
   - How will technical risk be mitigated?
   - What use cases, capabilities, or demonstrations will be featured?
4. **Team Organization and Capabilities**
   - 4.1 Roles and responsibilities of the organizations and key personnel in the Project Team.
   - 4.2 Name, position, and institution of each key team member with 1–2 sentences describing skills and experience. Key team members = individuals expected to contribute to the scientific development or execution in a substantive, measurable way.
5. **Basis of Estimate (BOE)**
   - 5.1 BOE covering all costs and resources for the full team (direct labor, materials, supplies, equipment, travel, profit, and any other relevant costs).
   - 5.2 Identify whether resource sharing applies. Table: BOE Amount (Total; Resource Sharing if applicable).

### Phase B — Full Proposal Bundle (submitted only after encouraged or authorized)

The full proposal consists of five artifacts. Proposers should use the provided templates.

#### B.1 Technical & Management Proposal — **20-page limit**

- Page limit includes: technical figures, tables, charts; Sections 1–4 below.
- Page limit does NOT include: Cover Sheet; Table of Contents; Bibliography (optional); Task Description Document (TDD); Cost Proposal and Cost Proposal Workbook.
- Formatting: English; 8.5x11; 1" margins; sans-serif ≥11pt (8–10pt for figures/tables/charts).
- Additional materials not called for must NOT be submitted (may appear only as bibliography links).

Cover metadata (not counted): Proposal Title; Proposer Organization Name; Type of Organization (For-Profit/Non-profit/Academia); SAM.gov UEI; Proposer's Internal Reference Number (if any); TPOC (name/mailing address/phone/email); Administrative POC (same); Award Instrument Requested = Other Transaction; Total Proposed Cost $; Resource Sharing Proposed (Government %/$ + Performer %/$); Place(s) of Performance; Date of Proposal Submission; Proposal's Validity Period (≥120 days); Sub-awardee & Consultant Team Members (org name, TPOC name/phone/email, org type).

Body sections (each required):

1. **Proposal Summary**
   - **A. Discussion** — synopsis of proposed project, answering the following questions (required, all must be addressed):
     1. What is the proposed work attempting to accomplish or do?
     2. How is the proposed work leading to improved health outcomes or improvements in the reliability of health systems?
     3. How is it done today, and what are the limitations of present approaches?
     4. What is new in your approach? Why do you think it will be successful at this time?
     5. What are the key technical, programmatic, or translational risks in your approach, and how do you plan to mitigate or overcome these?
     6. Who or what will be affected and what will be the impact if the work is successful?
     7. What are specific applications of your project?
     8. To ensure equitable access to all people, how will cost, accessibility, and user experience be addressed?
     9. How might this effort be misperceived or misused (and how can you prevent that from happening)?
     - Summary must include description of key technical challenges, concise review of the technologies proposed to overcome them, and a clear statement of the novelty and uniqueness of the proposed work.
   - **B. Innovative Claims Table** — required table linking technical goals, technical challenges, and proposed innovation/evidence. Columns: **ISO pages and paragraph(s) | Technical Challenge | Innovation and Evidence | Proposal pages, and paragraph(s).** Add rows as necessary.
2. **Goals and Impact** — Describe qualitative and quantitative difference the proposed effort will make. Describe innovative aspects in the context of existing capabilities and approaches; delineate relationship to past/present projects. Address plans and risks to achieve accessibility and adoption of technology. Include summary of specific outcomes from proposed research.
3. **Technical Plan**
   1. Credible plan to achieve the project's goal (high-risk, high-reward welcomed).
   2. Outline & address technical challenges and possible solutions for overcoming potential problems.
   3. Measurable milestones (quantitative where possible) at intermediate stages; clearly articulated and defined in time relative to project start.
   4. Schedule showing milestones, tasks, and interrelationships; task structure must be consistent with the TDD.
4. **Capabilities / Management Plan**
   1. Summary of expertise of proposed team, including sub-awardees/consultants and key personnel executing the work.
   2. Identify a Principal Investigator (PI) and provide a clear description of the team's organization including roles and responsibilities.
5. **Bibliography (Optional)** — brief bibliography with each key personnel's qualifications (resume), links to relevant papers, references, reports, etc.

#### B.2 Task Description Document (TDD) — **no page limit, submitted but not evaluated**

Instructions:
1. Provide the general objective of the proposal and a list of tasks/subtasks.
2. Complete per-task tables; add tables as needed; no proprietary information.
3. For each task/subtask, provide:
   - General description of its objective.
   - Detailed description of approach.
   - Location where it will be performed (commercial lab / non-profit site / academic campus / etc.).
   - Primary organization responsible (prime, sub-awardee, consultant) — by name.
   - All deliverables (data, reports, software) to be provided to the government.
   - Whether it involves human- and/or animal-subjects research (☐ Yes / ☐ No; if yes, describe).
   - Measurable milestones (demonstration, event/activity marking progress or completion).
4. Ensure tasks/subtasks are also listed in the corresponding "Ph # Task $" tabs of the Cost Workbook. Separate spreadsheet per Phase.

Per-task and per-subtask table (format required): Phase # | Task/Subtask # (hierarchical, e.g., 1.1.0, 1.1.1) | Title | Task Objective | Task Description | Location | Organization Responsible | Deliverable(s) with associated IP assertion if less than Unlimited Rights | Human Subjects/Animal Subjects Research (Y/N + description) | Associated Milestones (observable/measurable events; rows 1,2,3+ as needed).

#### B.3 Cost Proposal — no page limit

Cover metadata: Proposal Title; Proposer Organization; Type of Organization; Proposer's Internal Reference Number; Technical POC (Name/Address/Phone/Email); Administrative POC; Award Instrument = Other Transaction; Place(s) of Performance; Period(s) of Performance; Other Team Members (sub-awardees and consultants) incl. Technical POC Name / Organization / Organization Type; Total Proposed Price by Technical Area (TA) with per-TA breakdown; SAM.gov UEI; Taxpayer Identification Number (TIN); Date of Proposal Submission; Proposal Validity Period (≥120 days).

Required body sections (each must be addressed; supporting documentation expected):

1. **Level of Effort and Labor Costs** — levels of effort and associated personnel costs. Substantiate via key labor categories aligned to fully burdened rates × labor hours (or % effort). Explain outliers; document fair-and-reasonable rationale. Use only fully burdened labor rates; describe indirect rates only where applied to other cost elements.
2. **Materials** — itemized list: quantities, unit prices, proposed vendors (if known), basis of estimate (quotes, prior purchases, catalog prices, etc.). Any item > $5,000 must have back-up documentation. Note any assumed government-furnished materials.
3. **Equipment Purchases** — itemized list: quantities, unit prices, vendors, BOE. >$5,000 items require back-up docs. Note any government-furnished equipment assumed.
4. **Travel** — Table with columns: Purpose | # of Trips | # of Days | # of People | Departure/Destination | Cost. Add rows as needed.
5. **Other Direct Costs (ODCs)** — breakdown (e.g., graduate student tuition, computer maintenance/user fees) with anticipated costs; explanatory factors and derivation/application.
6. **Consultant Costs** — fully loaded daily/hourly rates, labor hours, any other anticipated consultant costs (e.g., travel).
7. **Sub-awardee Costs** — for each sub-awardee, provide the same Sections 1–7 level of detail.
8. **Resource Sharing** — source(s), nature, and amount(s) of proposed resource sharing.
9. **Potential Cost Reductions (Optional)** — written explanation of reductions that will NOT substantially impact intended results.

### Focus Area 2 (relevant to Cytognosis) — "Scaling the Biotechnology Revolution"

Participating Offices: SC-BER, CMEI-AFFO, SC-ASCR. Phase I applications must address a single focus area; the Cytognosis fit is Focus Area 2 with sub-area to be selected. Phase II may list primary + secondary focus areas.

Sub-focus areas (Phase I must select exactly one; Phase II primary):

- **2-A Biomolecular Science (BER)** — AI tools for protein structure prediction; integrate genomics / 'omics with biomolecular structure to elucidate function; address gaps in metabolic pathway analyses for microorganisms and plants; overcome current limitations in connection between biomolecular structure and function governing molecular interactions and molecular dynamics for biosystems design.
- **2-B Genotype to Phenotype (BER)** — advanced AI models linking genomic, biomolecular, metabolic, and environmental factors to emergent phenotypic outcomes in microbes and plants (non-linear, stochastic networks); reduce data requirements; efficient training; predict phenotypic outcomes directly in field settings.
- **2-C Predictive Engineering of Microbial Communities (BER)** — AI tools for genetic dark matter, novel metabolic-pathway prediction, interspecies metabolic networks, microbiome gene-expression prediction across phylogenetic spectrum for biomanufacturing / resource extraction / plant health.
- **2-D Bio Design (BER)** — DBTL → "Design-Build" paradigm; generate/analyze high-throughput multi-omics for AI-ready data on DOE-relevant plant or microbial systems; genome engineering tools.

**Project Narrative body — strongly encouraged structure (fits into 5 pages):**

2. **Project Objectives** (~0.5 page) — Clear, concise statement of specific project objectives; address how they align with the chosen focus topic and lead to an AI advantage.
3. **Proposed Research and Methods** (~1.5 pages) — Clear research plan for a 9-month project; describe activities and methods; enough technical detail to evaluate impact; for each activity, indicate responsible key investigator(s) and associated budget; if building on existing DOE funding, describe how the submission leverages and is distinct.
4. **Milestones in the Nine Months** (~1 page) — Clearly defined and measurable milestones.
5. **Data Sources and Models** (~0.5 page) — Existing or new data sources; team's access to the data; which AI models or frameworks used. Do NOT include a description of computing resources here — that belongs in Appendix 2.
6. **Decision Gate Metrics** (~0.5 page) — Specific metrics for Phase I go/no-go. Metrics may include AI-advantage scaling behavior; quantitative statements enabling transformative science/engineering preferred.

### Review Criteria (descending importance)

1. **Scientific and/or Technical Merit and Impact** — for fundamental research: scientific innovation; likelihood of achieving AI advantage; likelihood of valuable results; impact on direction/progress/thinking in the field; originality; articulation of contribution. For applied tech development: TRL level and advancement; KPI improvement path (cost, safety, efficiency); alignment with focus-area objectives; measurable, achievable, impactful goals.
2. **Technical Approach, Methods, and Feasibility** — methodology sound, well-justified; innovative concepts or methods; robust experimental/theoretical/computational design; challenges/risks identified + mitigation; realistic timeline + milestones. Applied additions: scalability; rigorous testing/validation; environmental impact + safety.
3. **Team, Resources, and Management** — team qualification; necessary expertise across disciplines; collaborative coordination + balanced responsibilities; adequate facilities/equipment/data/AI-model access.
4. **Commercialization Potential for Energy Applications** — Applied tech only. Technology transfer/commercialization strategy; market opportunities + preliminary techno-economic/market assessment; industry adoption potential; barriers addressed.
5. **Budget and Cost-Effectiveness** — reasonable and well-justified; cost share meets requirements and demonstrates commitment; efficient use of resources; collaboration with DOE/NNSA Labs / academia / industry.

Program Policy Factors the Selection Official may also weigh: availability of funds; relevance to DOE priorities; etc.
