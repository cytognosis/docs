# Framework Catalog: Research, Grant, and Moonshot Proposal Rubrics

A catalog of structured frameworks that complement the DARPA Heilmeier Catechism, assembled to inform a universal grant-application template for a bio + AI nonprofit (Cytognosis). Each entry is concise (roughly 150 words) and cites sources.

---

## 1. Heilmeier Catechism (DARPA)
- **Origin/owner:** George H. Heilmeier, DARPA Director 1975–1977; codified by DARPA.
- **Used by:** DARPA, IARPA, ARPA-E, ARPA-H, Wellcome Leap, most ARPA-style funders; widely adopted as a baseline "good proposal" rubric.
- **Core questions/sections (verbatim, 8 questions):**
  1. What are you trying to do? Articulate your objectives using absolutely no jargon.
  2. How is it done today, and what are the limits of current practice?
  3. What is new in your approach and why do you think it will be successful?
  4. Who cares? If you are successful, what difference will it make?
  5. What are the risks?
  6. How much will it cost?
  7. How long will it take?
  8. What are the mid-term and final "exams" to check for success?
- **Strengths:** Compressive, jargon-free, forces accountability (cost, time, exams). Universally recognized vocabulary among program managers.
- **Gaps vs. Heilmeier (what it lacks):** Silent on team fit, openness/IP, equity, safety/dual-use, why-not-academia/startup, regulatory path, org-model fit.
- **Fit for Cytognosis:** The baseline spine. Every Cytognosis proposal should answer these 8 questions in plain language, then extend with the categories Heilmeier omits.
- **Source URLs:**
  - https://www.darpa.mil/about/heilmeier-catechism
  - https://arpa-h.gov/sites/default/files/2024-11/Qs_behind_the_HQs.pdf

## 2. NIH R01 Research Strategy (Specific Aims + Significance / Innovation / Approach)
- **Origin/owner:** U.S. National Institutes of Health.
- **Used by:** All NIH R01/R21/R35 applications; adopted widely by academic biomedical funders.
- **Core questions/sections:**
  - **Specific Aims** (1 page): long-term goal, objective of this application, central hypothesis, 2–4 aims with expected outcomes, impact statement.
  - **Significance:** importance of the problem, critical barrier to progress, how success will change practice/concepts.
  - **Innovation:** novel concepts, tools, methods; shift from existing paradigms.
  - **Approach:** rationale, preliminary data, research design per aim, expected outcomes, **potential problems / alternative strategies / benchmarks for success** (contingency planning), rigor and reproducibility.
  - 2024+ "Simplified Review Framework" reorganizes to three factors: Importance of the Research, Rigor and Feasibility, Expertise and Resources.
- **Strengths:** Forces mechanistic hypothesis, preliminary data, and explicit fallback plans; rigor and reproducibility are first-class.
- **Gaps vs. Heilmeier:** Adds preliminary data, hypothesis rigor, contingency plans, rigor/reproducibility, investigator/environment — but is weak on cost-to-value, hard milestones, and why-ARPA-style.
- **Fit for Cytognosis:** Best source of language for biological rigor, preliminary data, and aim-level structure; import the "alternative strategies / benchmarks" subsection verbatim.
- **Source URLs:**
  - https://www.niaid.nih.gov/grants-contracts/draft-specific-aims
  - https://www.niaid.nih.gov/research/review-criteria
  - https://grants.nih.gov/grants-process/write-application/advice-on-application-sections

## 3. NSF Merit Review (Intellectual Merit + Broader Impacts) and NSF Tech Labs
- **Origin/owner:** U.S. National Science Foundation (PAPPG III.A); Tech Labs initiative (TIP directorate, 2025–26).
- **Used by:** All NSF proposals; Tech Labs targets FRO-style translational platforms.
- **Core questions/sections:**
  - Two statutory criteria, each evaluated against five review elements:
    1. What is the potential for the proposed activity to: (a) advance knowledge (Intellectual Merit) or (b) benefit society (Broader Impacts)?
    2. To what extent do activities suggest creative, original, or potentially transformative concepts?
    3. Is the plan well-reasoned, well-organized, and based on sound rationale, with a mechanism to assess success?
    4. How well qualified are the individual / team / organization?
    5. Are adequate resources available?
  - **Tech Labs** adds: independent-org model, milestone-based $10–50M/yr for up to 5 years, focus areas of national technical dominance (AI, bio, quantum, semiconductors, materials), operational autonomy, translational outputs beyond publications.
- **Strengths:** Broader Impacts institutionalizes societal benefit, workforce, participation of under-represented groups, public engagement.
- **Gaps vs. Heilmeier:** Adds societal benefit, workforce/inclusion, resource adequacy — but less demanding on quantitative milestones.
- **Fit for Cytognosis:** Provides crisp "benefit to society" language and legitimizes an equity/workforce block; Tech Labs framing maps directly onto an FRO-like bio+AI nonprofit.
- **Source URLs:**
  - https://www.nsf.gov/funding/merit-review
  - https://www.nsf.gov/news/nsf-announces-new-initiative-launch-scale-new-generation
  - https://www.macroscience.org/p/nsf-tech-labs-faqs

## 4. ARPA-H Solution Summary + "Hidden Heilmeier" Questions
- **Origin/owner:** ARPA-H (HHS), 2022–present.
- **Used by:** ARPA-H Open BAA and mission-office programs (e.g., UPGRADE, ADAPT, LIGHT, Sprint for Women's Health).
- **Core questions/sections:**
  - **Solution Summary** (≈3 pages + cover): program fit, technical approach, team, budget synopsis; gating step before full proposal.
  - Standard ARPA-H pitch is organized around the 8 Heilmeier questions, each expanded with "hidden" sub-questions, notably:
    - Quantitative, ambitious metrics (e.g., "recovery time drops 4×", "cost drops 100×").
    - Phased programs (2–3 phases) with explicit **go/no-go** milestones per phase.
    - Mission fit, stakeholder map, transition/adoption plan, equity/access considerations.
    - Regulatory and ethics path (FDA, IRB, data governance).
- **Strengths:** Operationalizes Heilmeier for health: forces numeric targets, phased go/no-go, equity, transition-to-practice.
- **Gaps vs. Heilmeier:** Adds equity/access, regulatory path, transition-to-practice, stakeholder ecosystem — all absent in Heilmeier.
- **Fit for Cytognosis:** Adopt ARPA-H's expanded Heilmeier verbatim as scaffolding for bio-translation proposals; its equity/regulatory/transition blocks are essential for any therapeutic or diagnostic moonshot.
- **Source URLs:**
  - https://arpa-h.gov/research-and-funding/open-baa
  - https://arpa-h.gov/sites/default/files/2024-11/Qs_behind_the_HQs.pdf
  - https://sprint.investorcatalysthub.org/wp-content/uploads/2024/04/Solution-Summary-Submission-Template.03.27.2024.pdf

## 5. ARIA (UK) Opportunity Space + Programme Framing
- **Origin/owner:** UK Advanced Research and Invention Agency, 2023–present.
- **Used by:** ARIA Programme Directors and their funding calls (opportunity seeds ≤£500k; programmes £50–80M).
- **Core questions/sections:**
  - **Opportunity Space thesis:** an area that is (1) highly consequential for society, (2) under-explored relative to potential, (3) ripe for new talent/resources to change what's possible.
  - **Programme framing:** bold technical vision, causal roadmap, portfolio of coordinated projects, PD-led orchestration, time-bounded bets.
  - Funding calls specify: creator profile, technical thesis, bets the programme is making, anti-goals, what success unlocks downstream.
- **Strengths:** Legitimizes speculation and anti-consensus bets; explicit "why now, why this space" thesis; permissive on methodology, strict on programme-level coherence.
- **Gaps vs. Heilmeier:** Adds under-exploration thesis, creator-fit, portfolio design, anti-goals; intentionally loose on predictability.
- **Fit for Cytognosis:** Use Opportunity-Space framing for the overall Cytognosis thesis; each subprogram should articulate its own "consequential / under-explored / ripe" triple.
- **Source URLs:**
  - https://aria.org.uk/opportunity-spaces/
  - https://aria.org.uk/how-we-work/
  - https://aria.org.uk/funding-opportunities/

## 6. Horizon Europe — Excellence / Impact / Implementation
- **Origin/owner:** European Commission (Horizon Europe Framework Programme, 2021–2027).
- **Used by:** All Horizon Europe collaborative calls; mirrored in many EU member-state schemes.
- **Core questions/sections:**
  - **Excellence:** clarity and pertinence of objectives, soundness of concept, methodology rigor, ambition and beyond-state-of-the-art.
  - **Impact:** credibility of pathways to scientific / economic / societal impact; measures to maximize impact (dissemination, exploitation, IP, communication).
  - **Implementation:** quality and efficiency of work plan, risk management, consortium capacity, resource allocation.
  - Scored 0–5 per criterion; thresholds 3/criterion, 10 total. Innovation Actions apply 1.5× weight to Impact.
- **Strengths:** First-class treatment of impact pathway and dissemination; explicit consortium capacity and risk management sections.
- **Gaps vs. Heilmeier:** Adds dissemination/exploitation/communication plan, consortium governance, open-science compliance; weaker on single "exam"-style metrics.
- **Fit for Cytognosis:** Borrow the Impact Pathway logic (pathways → KPIs → barriers → measures) and the Dissemination / Exploitation / Communication triad as named subsections.
- **Source URLs:**
  - https://ec.europa.eu/info/funding-tenders/opportunities/docs/2021-2027/experts/standard-briefing-slides-for-experts_he_en.pdf
  - https://research-and-innovation.ec.europa.eu/funding/how-projects-are-chosen-funding_en

## 7. Convergent Research — Focused Research Organization (FRO) Template
- **Origin/owner:** Convergent Research (spun from Schmidt Futures / FAS concept, 2021).
- **Used by:** FRO proposals pitched to Convergent and its funder network (Astera, NIH, Open Philanthropy, philanthropic donors).
- **Core questions/sections:**
  - **Bottleneck / public good:** what field-level bottleneck does the deliverable unblock?
  - **Deliverable:** a specific, tangible artifact (dataset, tool, atlas, pipeline) released as a public good.
  - **Why-not-academia / why-not-startup:** explicit justification for the FRO form (too engineering-heavy for a PI, no direct monetizable product for a VC-backed startup).
  - **Technical roadmap:** 5-year plan, coordinated milestones, staffing plan, scale of engineering.
  - **Technology transfer / sunset plan:** how outputs transition to users; how the FRO winds down.
  - **Budget:** mid-scale (typically $20–100M over 5 years).
- **Strengths:** Forces the FRO-fit justification; foregrounds deliverable-as-public-good; explicit sunset/transition planning.
- **Gaps vs. Heilmeier:** Adds org-form justification, public-good deliverable, sunset plan, engineering vs. science ratio.
- **Fit for Cytognosis:** Directly applicable — Cytognosis is an FRO-shaped bet. Use "why-not-academia / why-not-startup / why-FRO" as a mandatory block.
- **Source URLs:**
  - https://www.convergentresearch.org/
  - https://fas.org/publication/focused-research-organizations-a-new-model-for-scientific-research/
  - https://issues.org/focused-research-organizations-fro-marblestone-gamick-wang-fridman/

## 8. Astera Institute Residency Rubric
- **Origin/owner:** Astera Institute (Eric Schmidt-funded), 2023–present.
- **Used by:** Astera Residency cohorts (open science + bold tech projects).
- **Core questions/sections (application):**
  1. Describe the problem and proposed solution — college-freshman level (≤3000 chars).
  2. Why isn't this problem adequately addressed by academia, government, or industry?
  3. Who will use your residency's outputs, and how?
  4. How long have you been thinking about this problem?
  5. Most impressive thing you've done that isn't on your LinkedIn?
  6. Most exciting scientific/technical problem to you other than this one?
  7. Plus a 2-minute founder/project video and resume.
- **Strengths:** Blends FRO "why-not-academia" logic with personal/character signals (obsession, adjacent curiosity, non-legible accomplishment). Short-form, anti-jargon.
- **Gaps vs. Heilmeier:** Adds founder-fit, openness of users, non-legible expertise; skips cost/time/risk.
- **Fit for Cytognosis:** Use questions 2, 3, 4, 5, 6 as a "Founder & Fit" block in any internal residency/fellowship call Cytognosis issues.
- **Source URLs:**
  - https://astera.org/residency/
  - https://asterainstitute.substack.com/p/astera-residency-application
  - https://openheartmind.org/astera-open-science-residency-program/

## 9. Gates Foundation Grand Challenges (Two-Page Proposal)
- **Origin/owner:** Bill & Melinda Gates Foundation Grand Challenges (2003–present).
- **Used by:** GCE, GCGH, and themed Grand Challenges calls globally.
- **Core questions/sections (typical 2-page):**
  - Bold one-to-two-sentence thesis in the opening paragraph.
  - Problem statement tied to the challenge topic.
  - Unconventional / creative approach — explicitly rewarded.
  - Plan and key experiments for the funded phase (usually ~12–18 months, $100k seed).
  - Team and prior work relevant to approach.
  - Clarity for non-expert reviewers; jargon-free; no preliminary data required.
- **Strengths:** Enforces concision and lay-readable prose; explicitly invites unconventional ideas; lowers bar for preliminary data in favor of hypothesis boldness.
- **Gaps vs. Heilmeier:** Adds "unconventionality" scoring, accessibility to non-experts; skips detailed milestones.
- **Fit for Cytognosis:** Best model for seed/explore-tier calls within Cytognosis (short, bold, no prelim data required); adopt the "bold thesis in first sentences" convention.
- **Source URLs:**
  - https://gcgh.grandchallenges.org/application-guidelines
  - https://gcgh.grandchallenges.org/grant-opportunities/faq

## 10. Amazon Working Backwards / PR-FAQ
- **Origin/owner:** Amazon (Bezos-era), 1990s–present; popularized externally by Bryar & Carr (2021).
- **Used by:** Amazon product teams; widely borrowed by product orgs; increasingly used in moonshot labs and nonprofits for customer-framed proposals.
- **Core questions/sections:**
  - **Press Release (from future, customer-facing):** headline, sub-headline, summary paragraph, problem, solution, leader quote, customer experience, customer quote, getting-started call to action.
  - **External FAQ:** how does it work, price, availability, warranty, support — the questions press/customers will ask.
  - **Internal FAQ:** market size, unit economics, dependencies, risks, org impact, build-vs-buy, alternatives considered — the questions senior leaders will ask.
- **Strengths:** Forces the team to articulate the end-user experience before committing engineering; surfaces unit economics and switching costs early.
- **Gaps vs. Heilmeier:** Adds end-user narrative, switching-cost analysis, alternatives considered, "would I be excited to read this?" gut-check; skips hypothesis rigor.
- **Fit for Cytognosis:** Ideal for product-shaped deliverables (software tools, diagnostic assays, datasets-as-products). Attach an FAQ-style block — external ("what can a scientist do with this?") and internal ("why won't this be out-competed?").
- **Source URLs:**
  - https://workingbackwards.com/resources/working-backwards-pr-faq/
  - https://workingbackwards.com/concepts/working-backwards-pr-faq-process/

## 11. Lean Canvas / Business Model Canvas
- **Origin/owner:** Business Model Canvas — Osterwalder & Pigneur (2010); Lean Canvas — Ash Maurya (2012).
- **Used by:** Startups, accelerators (YC, Techstars), SBIR commercialization plans, tech-transfer offices.
- **Core sections (Lean Canvas, 9 blocks):** Problem, Customer Segments, Unique Value Proposition, Solution, Channels, Revenue Streams, Cost Structure, Key Metrics, Unfair Advantage. (BMC substitutes Key Partners / Key Activities / Key Resources / Customer Relationships.)
- **Strengths:** One-page synopsis; forces alignment between problem, customer, channel, and unfair advantage; checks for business-model coherence.
- **Gaps vs. Heilmeier:** Adds customer segments, channels, unfair advantage, unit economics; skips technical risk and scientific novelty.
- **Fit for Cytognosis:** Use a one-page "Nonprofit Lean Canvas" where "Revenue Streams" becomes "Funding Model + Sustainability" and "Unfair Advantage" becomes "Why-Cytognosis + Why-Now". Good artifact for concept-tier reviews.
- **Source URLs:**
  - https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Business_model_canvas
  - https://businessmodelanalyst.com/lean-canvas/

## 12. MIT Media Lab Antidisciplinary Norms
- **Origin/owner:** MIT Media Lab (Joi Ito-era formalization, ~2014); rooted in Lab culture since Negroponte.
- **Used by:** Media Lab faculty / student proposals; Journal of Design and Science; influenced countless "bridge labs" (Santa Fe, Radical Xchange, BioMedX, etc.).
- **Core norms / questions:**
  - **Antidisciplinary fit:** does this project live in the whitespace between disciplines — neither interdisciplinary sum nor discipline-specific?
  - **Uniqueness, Impact, Magic:** the Lab's three-word mantra — unique (no one else is doing this), impactful (matters at scale), magical (evokes wonder, exceeds incremental).
  - **Permissionless innovation:** ability to pursue without asking, tolerate unfashionable directions.
  - **Deploy-first mindset:** prototypes shipped to the world, not confined to papers.
  - Openness norms: open source, open publishing, public engagement (JoDS).
- **Strengths:** Captures the "why this won't happen in a normal department" argument; licenses weirdness; privileges demos over papers.
- **Gaps vs. Heilmeier:** Adds antidisciplinary fit, magic/wonder criterion, deploy-over-publish norm; skips budget/risk discipline entirely.
- **Fit for Cytognosis:** Borrow "Uniqueness / Impact / Magic" as a 3-sentence gut-check attached to every proposal; pair with the FRO "why-not-academia" question for coverage.
- **Source URLs:**
  - https://joi.ito.com/weblog/2014/10/02/antidisciplinar.html
  - https://medium.com/mit-media-lab/crowdsourcing-disobedience-96f6a275089b
  - https://jods.mitpress.mit.edu/pub/designandscience

## 13. Y Combinator Application
- **Origin/owner:** Y Combinator (Graham, Livingston, et al.), 2005–present.
- **Used by:** YC application; widely mimicked by accelerators and startup fellowships.
- **Core questions (compact startup framing):**
  - Company / product one-sentence description; website & demo URL.
  - What problem are you solving? Who has the problem?
  - What's your progress? (users, revenue, traction).
  - Why you? (founder background and domain obsession).
  - How do you make money? (revenue model, pricing).
  - Who are your competitors? Why will you win?
  - Legal/equity structure and founder split.
  - 1-minute founder video.
  - Why did you apply / who encouraged you?
- **Strengths:** Radical compression — forces the team to articulate problem, traction, team, and defensibility in minutes. Video humanizes the pitch.
- **Gaps vs. Heilmeier:** Adds founder-fit, traction metrics, competitive dynamics, revenue model; skips technical risk and scientific rigor.
- **Fit for Cytognosis:** Use a YC-style short-form for spinout / external-partner pitches (where a Cytognosis-born technology needs to go to market). Not the right frame for research proposals, but useful for translation-phase artifacts.
- **Source URLs:**
  - https://www.ycombinator.com/apply/
  - https://www.peakdigitalstudio.com/articles/yc-application-questions-template

## 14. Wellcome Leap Program Framing
- **Origin/owner:** Wellcome Leap (spun from the Wellcome Trust, 2020), founding CEO Regina Dugan (ex-DARPA).
- **Used by:** Wellcome Leap programs (HOPE, CARE, Dynamic Resilience, SAVE, 1kD, etc.).
- **Core questions/sections:**
  - **Program thesis:** 5–10 year breakthrough in human health, milestone-driven, time-bounded.
  - **Program Director-led:** a named PD orchestrates a consortium of performers toward shared milestones.
  - **3-year performance period:** clear milestones, go/no-go, standard funding agreement to reduce admin delay.
  - Per-call FAQs specify: eligibility, consortium composition, multi-country teams, IP and data-sharing defaults, proposal format.
  - Strong DARPA lineage: pitches answer the Heilmeier 8 plus a Leap-specific "why this program, why now, why Leap".
- **Strengths:** Standard funding agreement across performers (low-friction consortia); orchestrated portfolio with explicit milestones; international by default.
- **Gaps vs. Heilmeier:** Adds consortium orchestration, standard-FA mechanics, cross-country execution; reinforces Heilmeier rather than replacing it.
- **Fit for Cytognosis:** Model for any Cytognosis-led consortium call — adopt the "PD orchestrates performers against shared milestones" operational frame.
- **Source URLs:**
  - https://wellcomeleap.org/
  - https://wellcome.org/research-funding/funding-portfolio/major-initiatives/wellcome-leap
  - https://wellcomeleap.org/hope-faq/

## 15. Schmidt Futures / Schmidt Sciences Program Framing
- **Origin/owner:** Schmidt Futures (2017) → restructured into Schmidt Sciences (2024) + Schmidt Science Fellows (with Rhodes Trust).
- **Used by:** Schmidt Sciences programs (AI-for-Science, biosciences), Schmidt Science Fellows, Schmidt AI in Science Postdoctoral Fellowships.
- **Core questions/sections:**
  - **Pivot thesis** (Fellows): proposal must represent a genuine, ambitious change of focus from PhD research; state the new tools/skills needed.
  - **Programmatic bet:** a thematic bet (e.g., AI-for-science) framed as infrastructure for a whole community, not a single PI.
  - Personal statement (motivation), research proposal (up to 1000 words for Fellows), publications, references.
  - Review via discipline panels → interdisciplinary panel interview (academia + industry + society).
- **Strengths:** Pivot requirement explicitly rewards cross-domain moves; reviewers span academia + industry + society, mimicking impact-diversity.
- **Gaps vs. Heilmeier:** Adds interdisciplinary-pivot requirement, philanthropic-infrastructure thesis, multi-sector review; less rigorous on cost/milestones.
- **Fit for Cytognosis:** Directly relevant for Cytognosis fellowship/residency track; also a model for framing Cytognosis itself as philanthropic infrastructure for bio+AI convergence.
- **Source URLs:**
  - https://www.schmidtsciences.org/
  - https://schmidtsciencefellows.org/selection/how-to-apply/
  - https://graduateschool.brown.edu/sites/default/files/pdfs/Schmidt%20Science%20Fellows%20Sample%20Application%20Form.pdf

## 16. SBIR / STTR Phase I / II
- **Origin/owner:** U.S. Federal SBIR program (1982); DoD, HHS/NIH, NSF, DoE, NASA, DARPA, ARPA-H participants.
- **Used by:** Small businesses applying for federal R&D commercialization funding; standard template across agencies with agency-specific twists.
- **Core sections (DoD Phase I, 5 volumes):**
  - Vol 1 — Cover sheet and eligibility.
  - Vol 2 — Technical Volume (≤15 pp): identification and significance of opportunity; Phase I technical objectives; work plan; related R&D; key personnel; facilities/equipment; commercialization strategy; references.
  - Vol 3 — Cost Volume.
  - Vol 4 — Company Commercialization Report (prior SBIR outcomes).
  - Vol 5 — Supporting docs (incl. optional 10-slide deck, foreign-ownership disclosure).
  - HHS/NIH variant: Research Plan with Significance / Innovation / Approach plus a commercialization plan (required only for Fast Track / Phase II).
- **Strengths:** Commercialization plan is first-class; Phase I/II/III pipeline models technology maturity; standardized cost volumes.
- **Gaps vs. Heilmeier:** Adds commercialization strategy, market size and path, TRL maturation, prior-funding accountability.
- **Fit for Cytognosis:** Useful for spinout-stage templates; import the Commercialization Strategy block when Cytognosis outputs approach productization.
- **Source URLs:**
  - https://www.dodsbirsttr.mil/submissions/learning-support/firm-templates
  - https://www.sciencedocs.com/dod-sbir-template/
  - https://www.sbir.gov/tutorials/preparing-proposal/tutorial-6

## 17. Speculative Technologies — Coordinated Research Programs
- **Origin/owner:** Speculative Technologies (spec.tech), nonprofit founded by Ben Reinhardt, 2022.
- **Used by:** Spec.Tech program design; supported by Open Philanthropy; sibling to FRO model.
- **Core questions/sections:**
  - **Program thesis:** an "ideal end state" written out in detail, plus a causal narrative for how it plausibly happens.
  - **Block diagrams:** coordinated projects across multiple performers (academic labs, CROs, startups) mapped to sub-goals.
  - **Three-stage lifecycle:** design → derisking experiments → main program (3–5 years).
  - Request-for-Proposals issued at each block; orchestrated by a program manager.
  - Emphasis on "too speculative for a startup, too coordination-heavy for academia."
- **Strengths:** Explicit causal narrative from end-state backward; block-diagram decomposition; acknowledges and funds the "derisking" pre-phase before the main program.
- **Gaps vs. Heilmeier:** Adds causal-narrative format, program design as its own phase, block-diagram decomposition of consortia.
- **Fit for Cytognosis:** Best model for Cytognosis-internal program design — author each program as an end-state + causal narrative + block diagram before issuing internal RFPs.
- **Source URLs:**
  - https://spec.tech/model
  - https://spec.tech/library/introducing-speculative-technologies
  - https://spec.tech/library/research-leaders-playbook

## 18. DoE ARPA-E — Performance Period Goals + Tech-to-Market (T2M) Plan
- **Origin/owner:** U.S. Department of Energy ARPA-E (2009–present).
- **Used by:** ARPA-E awardees (energy moonshots); model echoed across ARPA-H and ARPA-Climate proposals.
- **Core questions/sections:**
  - **Technical Volume:** Heilmeier-structured pitch plus quantitative performance metrics and Performance Period Goals (milestones per budget period with go/no-go criteria).
  - **Tech-to-Market (T2M) Plan** — four-area framework, required for all awardees:
    1. **Market:** will a product create enough economic value to drive adoption?
    2. **Manufacturing:** can it be produced cost-effectively at scale?
    3. **Intellectual Property:** plan to secure and manage IP.
    4. **Funding:** how will the effort be sustained after ARPA-E?
  - Plus a techno-economic analysis (TEA) and life-cycle analysis (LCA) where relevant.
- **Strengths:** T2M is the strongest commercialization framework in federal R&D; explicit post-award funding plan forces sustainability thinking; TEA/LCA quantify cost-of-impact.
- **Gaps vs. Heilmeier:** Adds manufacturing, IP, sustainability funding, TEA/LCA; these are largely absent from Heilmeier.
- **Fit for Cytognosis:** Adopt the four-question T2M framework (rename to "Translation-to-Mission") for all Cytognosis programs producing tangible outputs; use TEA/LCA analogs for biological cost-per-insight.
- **Source URLs:**
  - https://arpa-e.energy.gov/technology-to-market
  - https://arpa-e.energy.gov/technology-to-market/resources

## Recurring Extensions Beyond Heilmeier

Cross-cutting categories that appear repeatedly in the frameworks above but are **not** covered by the original Heilmeier 8. For Cytognosis's universal template, each should be a named block.

| Extension | Why it matters | Frameworks that surface it |
|---|---|---|
| **Open science & openness commitments** | Public-good outputs, data/code sharing, sunset plan, publishing norms | FRO (deliverable-as-public-good), Astera (open science residency), Media Lab (permissionless, deploy-first), Horizon Europe (dissemination + open-access), Wellcome Leap (standard FA defaults), Speculative Technologies |
| **Equity & accessibility** | Who benefits, who is excluded, workforce inclusion | NSF Broader Impacts, ARPA-H (access/affordability metrics), Horizon Europe Impact, Gates Grand Challenges |
| **AI safety / responsible AI** | Dual-use model risks, evaluations, red-teaming, governance | ARPA-H (emerging block for AI-enabled proposals), NSF Tech Labs (responsible innovation), Horizon Europe (ethics self-assessment) — note: this is the area *least* covered in 2024 frameworks and where Cytognosis should lead |
| **Boldness / moonshot justification** | Why big, why now, why not incremental | ARIA (opportunity space thesis), Media Lab (Uniqueness/Impact/Magic), Gates (unconventional approach), ARPA-H (quantitative ambition: 4×, 100×), Wellcome Leap, Speculative Technologies |
| **"Why not academia / why not for-profit" (org-fit)** | Justifies the FRO/nonprofit/DARPA form | FRO template, Astera (Q2), Speculative Technologies, NSF Tech Labs |
| **Team & founder fit** | Obsession, track record, non-legible expertise, diversity | Astera (Q4–Q6), YC (founder section + video), NIH (Investigators factor), NSF (qualifications element), Schmidt (pivot + personal statement) |
| **IP / data governance** | Who owns, who can use, what's open, what's restricted | ARPA-E T2M (IP), SBIR (IP rights), Wellcome Leap (standard FA data-sharing), Horizon Europe (exploitation/IP), FRO (open-by-default) |
| **Regulatory path** | FDA, IRB, dual-use, biosecurity review, export control | ARPA-H (regulatory/ethics), NIH (human subjects, rigor), Horizon Europe ethics |

| **Transition / sunset / sustainability plan** | How outputs continue after the grant period | FRO (sunset), ARPA-E T2M (funding), SBIR (Phase III), Wellcome Leap (transition), Horizon Europe (exploitation) |
| **Customer / end-user narrative** | Who picks this up, switching cost, adoption story | Amazon PR-FAQ, Lean Canvas (customer segments + channels), YC, ARPA-H (transition-to-practice), SBIR (commercialization) |
| **Quantitative targets & go/no-go** | Numeric ambition and phased decision gates | ARPA-H (4×/100× metrics), ARPA-E (Performance Period Goals), Wellcome Leap (3-yr milestones), DARPA phasing |

### Synthesis: what a Cytognosis-universal template needs on top of Heilmeier

A minimal Cytognosis template = **Heilmeier 8** + **12 extension blocks**:

1. Bold thesis (Gates, ARIA)
2. Opportunity-space / why-now (ARIA)
3. Why-not-academia / why-not-for-profit / why-Cytognosis (FRO, Astera, Speculative Technologies)
4. Quantitative ambition with go/no-go milestones (ARPA-H, ARPA-E, Wellcome Leap)
5. Deliverable as public good + open-science commitments (FRO, Media Lab, Horizon Europe)
6. End-user narrative / adoption story (Amazon PR-FAQ, SBIR, ARPA-H transition)
7. Team & founder fit — including non-legible expertise (Astera, YC, NIH)
8. Equity, access, workforce (NSF Broader Impacts, ARPA-H)
9. IP & data governance (ARPA-E T2M, Wellcome Leap standard FA, FRO)
10. Regulatory + dual-use / biosecurity + AI-safety block (ARPA-H, NIH DURC, Horizon Europe ethics)
11. Translation-to-Mission plan: Market/Manufacturing/IP/Funding adapted for nonprofit bio+AI (ARPA-E T2M)
12. Sunset / sustainability / alumni plan (FRO, Horizon Europe exploitation)

These 12 extensions plus Heilmeier form the scaffolding for the universal grant-application template.
