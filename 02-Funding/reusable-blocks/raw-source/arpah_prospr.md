## 2. Program Information

### 2.1 Background

Despite major advances in scientific discovery and clinical interventions that have added years to life expectancy, there has not been a corresponding increase in healthspan, the number of years spent in good health. Instead, the prevalence of chronic diseases in American adults has dramatically increased, particularly worsening with age. In the United States and elsewhere, medicines and scientific research are heavily focused on reactionary, post-symptomatic illness treatment, despite overwhelming evidence that early interventions are far more effective, and many diseases are preventable.

The Proactive Health Office (PHO) at ARPA-H is seeking solutions to extend the healthspan for Americans, with a focus on interventions aimed at early disease prevention. In the United States, age-related chronic diseases come with an annual healthcare cost burden of nearly $1 trillion. The healthcare costs for the aging population is projected to increase by 75% if nothing is done to prevent the progressive loss of physical functioning during aging.

**Four major factors limiting progress in healthspan extension:**

1. **Health decline during aging is accepted as normal.** Aging is the most significant risk factor for a variety of diseases and yet age-associated health decline is not researched nor treated like other risk factors, such as hypertension or pre-diabetes. However, considering that some individuals maintain excellent health far longer than others, it is clear aging does not automatically necessitate poor health. Animal studies with parabiosis, exercise, and caloric restriction demonstrate that a youthful healthy state can be maintained in older ages.

2. **Geriatric medicine focuses on post-symptomatic disease treatments.** Because the early decline in health during aging is not the focus of geriatric medicine, much of the progress in healthspan research has been incidental, following specific disease targeting. Aging is now known to involve systemic changes such as increased inflammation and metabolic dysfunction, which often precede age-related diseases. Drugs like Rapamycin, Ozempic, and Metformin have shown pleiotropic effects, though most studies are limited to individuals with various diseases.

3. **Clinical trials are too costly and time-consuming for age-induced health outcomes.** A clinical trial with healthspan changes as the outcome could take 20-50 years. Considering the average clinical trial for diseases costs about $2 billion to conduct, a healthspan study could cost well over $6 billion.

4. **Lack of validated surrogates for healthspan.** The major limiting factor that contributes to the cost and time requirements for human clinical trials on healthspan is the designated clinical endpoint. One way to measure age-induced general health is through intrinsic capacity (IC), a score generated by assessing a wide-range of physical and mental abilities. IC progressively declines with age, and low IC has recently been demonstrated to roughly correlate with an increased risk of future chronic disease development. Blood-derived biomarkers, particularly those associated with inflammation and metabolism, have shown promising correlations with IC in older adults. However, IC studies are in their infancy, being performed in small scale, short duration studies and have not been used to generate a 20-year mortality or morbidity prediction.

### 2.2 PROSPR Program Description and Scope

PROSPR is a **5-year program** that aims to develop interventions that **extend healthspan by 20 years** by targeting the underlying causes of age-induced health loss and thereby prevent the onset of multiple age-related diseases simultaneously.

To facilitate the study and testing of healthspan interventions, PROSPR will first reduce the time and cost investments that currently prevent a thriving healthspan therapeutics industry from developing. By directly demonstrating that intrinsic capacity measures and blood-derived biomarkers can predict 20-year health outcomes, PROSPR will provide the scientific basis for these measures to serve as surrogate endpoints, dramatically shortening clinical trial timelines and reducing costs.

### 2.3 Technical Approach and Structure

#### 2.3.1 Approach by Technical Area (TA)

##### Technical Area 1 (TA1): PROSPR IC Score (60 months)

TA1 focuses on developing a PROSPR Intrinsic Capacity (IC) score that predicts 20-year health outcomes. The TA1 effort consists of two tracks:

**Track 1a: PROSPR IC Score Development**

- TA1 Track 1a performers will develop the PROSPR IC score by analyzing existing datasets to develop a composite health measure that predicts 20-year mortality and multi-morbidity outcomes.
- Track 1a performers will identify physiological and biochemical components predictive of health outcomes.
- A sex-specific PROSPR IC score must be developed that accounts for differences in aging trajectories between men and women.
- Track 1a performers will engage with the FDA to advance the PROSPR IC score toward validated surrogate endpoint designation.

**Track 1b: PROSPR Assessment Technology and Lifestyle Interventions**

- TA1 Track 1b performers will build in-home PROSPR IC assessment technology for consistent health monitoring.
- Track 1b will establish a multi-modal decentralized or hybrid lifestyle intervention study.
- Track 1b performers will collect empirical evidence demonstrating that lifestyle interventions can improve or maintain general wellness in relatively healthy, aging individuals.
- Track 1b performers will commercialize PROSPR IC assessment technology (target: less than $100 out of pocket).

##### Technical Area 2 (TA2): Repurposing FDA-approved Interventions (60 months)

TA2 aims to accelerate the creation of a healthspan industry by identifying and repurposing FDA-approved drugs.

**Track 2a: Biochemical Marker Identification**

- Track 2a performers will analyze blood samples from existing clinical trials where a therapeutic intervention has demonstrated broad health improvements to identify biochemical markers (genes, metabolites, proteins, etc.) consistently responsive across interventions.
- Encouraged treatments for consideration: rapamycin, metformin, GLP-1, acyclovir, naltrexone, and somatostatin.
- Performers must have letters from trialists agreeing to share stored blood samples for omics analysis.
- Track 2a will conduct comprehensive omics analyses on these blood samples during an 18-month funding period.

**Track 2b: FDA-approved Drug Clinical Trials**

- Track 2b performers will identify up to two FDA-approved drugs with strong safety data and evidence of multimorbidity reduction in humans or evidence of healthspan, frailty, or lifespan improvement in preclinical animal models.
- Conduct a 3-year, Phase 3 randomized clinical trial, conducted in a decentralized manner (DCT), testing drug efficacy for improving PROSPR IC and health outcomes in 60-year-old participants.
- Track 2b performers will work with the FDA on decentralized clinical trial design and validated surrogate endpoints for healthspan.

##### Technical Area 3 (TA3): Second-Generation Interventions (60 months)

TA3 focuses on identifying the most efficacious interventions that can extend human healthspan. TA3 performers will propose interventions with strong evidence of extending healthspan, lifespan, or reducing frailty symptoms in preclinical rodent models.

| Phase | Duration | Focus |
|-------|----------|-------|
| **Phase I** | 18 months | Preclinical validation using aged animal models |
| **Phase II** | 18 months | IND-enabling studies to evaluate safety, pharmacokinetics, and behavior of second-generation interventions |
| **Phase III** | 6 months | Establishing IRB approval and preparing for clinical trial |
| **Phase IV** | 18 months | First-in-human clinical testing (Phase 1b) to assess safety and efficacy |

Innovative therapeutics with the highest safety profile and greatest efficacy in aged animal models will be prioritized for the Phase 1b human clinical trials. Performers may propose various interventions such as small molecules, organelle transplant, or lifestyle mimetics.

> **NOTE:** TA3 performers are expected to collaborate and share data, both among the TA3 performers (specifically the preclinical method of IC assessment) and with performers in TAs 1 and 2 to ensure the overall success of the program.

#### 2.3.2 Deliverables and Transition Activities

| Technical Area | Deliverables and Transition Activities |
|----------------|---------------------------------------|
| **TA1** | A highly accurate and easy-to-measure intrinsic capacity score (PROSPR IC score) that quantifies age-associated health changes, responds to interventions, and predicts 20-year health outcomes. A user-friendly proactive health management tool (in-home PROSPR IC assessment technology). Deployment strategies for regulatory approval and reimbursement pathways. PROSPR IC as an FDA-validated surrogate endpoint. A multi-modal decentralized/hybrid lifestyle intervention study. Empirical evidence that lifestyle interventions improve wellness in healthy individuals. |
| **TA2** | Intervention-responsive biomarkers identified through omics analysis. Repurposed drugs that extend healthspan in aged individuals. Establishment of a basis for consensus around decentralized clinical trials for aged, non-diseased populations. Regulatory pathway for interventions targeting age-induced intrinsic capacity decline. |
| **TA3** | Phase 1b clinical trial for an affordable and accessible novel medicine that extends healthspan by improving or maintaining intrinsic capacity. Preclinical assessment protocol of PROSPR IC measures. |
| **All TAs** | Integrate TA1 deliverables (PROSPR IC and in-home assessment technology) into clinical trial designs throughout the program. Integrate TA2 identified biomarkers into clinical trial designs. Engage with FDA for early guidance. Demonstrate long-term planning for funding and commercialization partners. Focus on accessibility (partnerships that promote access to 75% or more of US communities). |

#### 2.3.3 Program Structure

The PROSPR program structure is based on a multi-year timeline with integration across TAs. Performers may submit proposals that address any one of three TAs or can combine more than one TA or tracks to address broader, multi-disciplinary challenges. Regardless of the number of TAs included in the proposal, performers must plan for integration and data sharing with other teams.

**Cross-TA Integration Requirements:**

- TA1 performers will provide PROSPR IC technology to TA2 performers before the end of Phase 2 to use in their Phase 3 clinical trial design.
- TA1 performers will provide PROSPR IC technology to TA3 performers before the end of TA3 Phase III to incorporate measures into Phase 1b clinical trial design.

> **NOTE:** Any performer that does not meet the accessibility, data sharing plan, and attendance requirements may not progress to subsequent phases. Progression into future phases is determined by the ARPA-H PM and is based on funding availability, performer progress toward phase metrics, and probability of success in future phases.

Detailed metrics tables are organized by TA and Track with specific Phase-level milestones, deliverables, and go/no-go criteria. Refer to the full solicitation for Tables 3-5 containing complete metrics for each TA track.

#### 2.3.5 PROSPR Data Sharing Plan

Performers are expected to develop and maintain a comprehensive data sharing plan that facilitates cross-TA integration and supports the program's overall scientific objectives.

## 6. General Requirements and Information

### 6.1 Proposing Teams

It is expected proposals will involve teams with the expertise needed to achieve the goals for the proposed TA(s). Proposers must submit a single, integrated proposal led by a Principal Investigator (PI), under a single prime awardee that addresses all program phases and metrics.

Investigators may serve as the Principal Investigator under a single prime proposal. Investigators may participate in multiple proposals within a sub-proposer/sub-awardee. An institution/organization can only submit one proposal as a Prime. In terms of a university with multiple departments, each division/department may be counted as an institution eligible to submit a full proposal as the Prime proposer.

### 6.2 Health Data Protection and Privacy

Sharing of any program information and/or program deliverables will be controlled and in accordance with the negotiated terms of the resulting Agreement. The associated Intellectual Property rights for all program deliverables will be negotiated with each selected Performer prior to Agreement award.

### 6.3 Scientifically Appropriate Representation in Clinical Study Populations

ARPA-H is committed to healthcare access for all those who need it, where they need it. Aging affects all people. Therefore, PROSPR will ensure that all performers have a clear plan to enroll clinical study populations that aim to match the demographics of the US population.

## 7. Administrative and National Policy Requirements

### 7.1 Organizational Conflicts of Interest (OCI)

Proposers are required to identify and disclose all facts relevant to potential organizational conflicts of interest (OCI) involving the proposer's organization and any proposed team member. Although the FAR does not apply to OTs, ARPA-H requires OCIs be addressed in the same manner prescribed in FAR subpart 9.5.

### 7.2 Agency Supplemental OCI Policy

ARPA-H restricts performers from concurrently providing professional support services, including Advisory and Assistance Services or similar support services, and being a technical performer.

### 7.3 Intellectual Property

The Proposer must provide a good faith representation that it either owns or possesses the appropriate licensing rights to all intellectual property (IP) that will be utilized for the proposed effort.

### 7.4 Animal Subject Research

Award recipients performing research involving the use of animals shall comply with 9 CFR parts 1-4 (USDA rules implementing the Animal Welfare Act), the Public Health Service Policy on Humane Care and Use of Laboratory Animals, and the Guide for the Care and Use of Laboratory Animals (8th Edition). All Animal Use Research must undergo review and approval by the local Institutional Animal Care Use Committee (IACUC) prior to incurring any costs.

### 7.5 Human Subjects Research

All entities submitting a proposal for funding that will involve engagement in human subjects research (as defined in 45 CFR § 46) must provide documentation of Assurance of Compliance, including at least an HHS Office of Human Research Protection Federal Wide Assurance. All human subjects research must be reviewed and approved by an IRB. Funding cannot be used toward human subjects research until ALL approvals are granted.

### 7.6 Electronic Invoicing and Payments

Performers will be required to register in and submit invoices for payment in the designated invoicing system.

Details on any Government-furnished resources will be specified in the resulting agreements.
