Funding Opportunity: DOE Releases First Solicitation for Genesis
Mission to Advance Science and Technology Challenges
Lewis-Burke Associates LLC – March 19, 2026

On March 17, 2026, the Department of Energy (DOE) released a $294 million Request for Application – the
‘Genesis Mission: Transforming Science and Energy with AI’ – to support multi-institutional and
interdisciplinary teams applying novel artificial intelligence (AI) models and frameworks to accelerate
scientific discovery and R&D workflows across DOE’s energy, environmental, and nuclear missions. This is
DOE’s first investment to advance 19 of the 26 Genesis Mission Science and Technology challenges to
accelerate innovation and discovery through AI, originally announced on February 12. Applications are due
April 28, 2026 for Phase I and May 19, 2026 for Phase II. DOE will host an informational webinar on March
26, 2026 at 3:00 PM ET. Lewis-Burke strongly encourages all researchers interested in applying to attend.

Genesis is structured as a two-phase competition. Phase I supports smaller, proof-of-concept teams for
six months, while Phase II funds large, multi-institutional teams for three-year deployments. Phase I
awardees from FY 2026 will be reviewed after their initial six months, and those approved for the next
phase can then apply for Phase II awards by December 17, 2026. For this first solicitation, DOE expects
most awards will be Phase I. Phase I awards not approved will be provided a three-month extension to
close out their work. DOE currently plans to issue a separate competition for a second round of Phase I
and Phase II applications in FY 2027.

This is a cross-cutting solicitation jointly funded by the Office of Science (SC), Office of Critical Minerals
and Energy Innovation (CMEI), Office of Environmental Management (EM), Office of Electricity (OE), Office
of Nuclear Energy (NE), and the Hydrocarbons and Geothermal Energy Office (HGEO). Teams are
encouraged to leverage the scientific data, computing resources, and facilities of DOE’s National
Laboratories, industry, and academia. Successful AI models and workflows may ultimately be integrated
into the American Science Cloud, launched in November 2025 and led by Oak Ridge National Lab, to
create a science-optimized cloud environment that integrates DOE's high-performance computing,
experimental facilities, and data resources to increase research and development productivity.

Eligibility and Key Requirements

All domestic applicant types are eligible, including universities, national laboratories, and for-profit
entities. Multi-institutional teams are required for both Phase I and Phase II submissions. Lead
institutions are limited to one application per focus area (either Phase I or II), though institutions may
participate as non-lead partners on an unlimited number of additional applications. A PI can be the lead-
PI on only one Phase I or Phase II application. For-profit entities serving as prime or subrecipient must
provide a minimum 20 percent cost share on R&D activities. DOE plans to set aside about 20 percent or
approximately $59 million of the $294 million for industry partners. No cost share is required for
universities or national laboratories. Applications must also satisfy a new Research, Technology, and
Economic Security due diligence standard and are subject to a due diligence review. Teams may still
submit proposals after the deadline, but DOE reserves the right to decline late applications without review
and may wait to make awards until the next solicitation. A foreign institution may be a team member on an
application but there must be a detailed description of how the proposed non-domestic institution
possesses skills, resources, and abilities that do not exist domestically.

Review Process and Other Considerations

Review criteria for projects include:
   • Scientific and technical merit and impact
   • Technical approach, methods, and feasibility
   • Team, resources, and management
   • Budget and cost-effectiveness
   • (only for applied technology applications) Commercialization potential for energy applications

DOE will also consider additional program policy factors including:
  • Relevance of the proposed activity to DOE priorities
  • Ensuring an appropriate balance of activities within DOE programs
  • Performance under current awards
  • Strengthening partnerships between DOE/NNSA laboratories, universities, non-profits, and
       industry
  • Commitment to sharing the results of research
For Phase I projects to move to Phase II, the team must provide a quantitative assessment of whether the
proposed approach of the team is on a trajectory toward a transformative scientific capability, justifying
further investment. Success criteria include:
    • “demonstrating increased predictive power or scientific insight from appropriately-curated data,
    • more tightly coupling data and experiments to validate hypotheses,
    • building new models and analyzing their impact on discovery speedup,
    • identifying scaling metrics that show how performance improves with more data or computing
         resources, and
    • improving and speeding up experimental workflows (e.g., through automation or AI-informed
         parameters). The goal is to provide quantitative analysis of whether a proposed approach is on a
         trajectory toward a transformative scientific capability, justifying further investment.”

Topics and Focus Areas

DOE has organized the Genesis solicitation into nine broad topic domains – advanced manufacturing,
biotechnology, critical materials, nuclear fission, nuclear fusion, quantum information science,
semiconductors and microelectronics, discovery science, and energy – with 21 challenge areas and 99
specific focus areas. Phase I applications must target a single focus area; Phase II applications must
identify a primary focus area but may address a secondary one. The table below lists each focus area.
