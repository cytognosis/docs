### Question 30, Why does your organization want to participate in Claude Corps? (limit 1,500)

> Cytognosis Foundation is a 501(c)(3) building Cytoverse, an open, AI-native platform to make precision health a human right, not a privilege. Our mission is personal. For decades our founder was misdiagnosed across ten specialties until, as a computational biologist, he analyzed his own genome and found the single rare genetic variant that explained it all. Cytognosis exists so no one else waits that long.

That origin sets our bar. We build models that reach patients and change clinical practice, not models that win a journal cover and are never used again.

We want fellows for two reasons. First, we need scientist-engineers to help build Cytoverse's nested, physically-grounded foundation models, spanning genomic to cellular scale, and because we are AI-native, a fellow builds from day one rather than fighting to introduce AI. Second, we care about training people. A fellow learns to develop and lead real modeling projects under two demanding mentors, and leaves able to build the right model for a hard problem, which is exactly the talent this field needs.

### Question 31, Describe one or two AI-related operational challenges or programmatic opportunities you'd want a fellow to work on. (limit 1,500)

> Both fellows work as scientist-engineers building novel foundation models for biology, models that are physically grounded and nested across scales rather than flat black boxes.

We would likely split them across adjacent scales. One fellow models the genomic scale, learning disease- and phenotype-associated genomic archetypes, embeddings, and latent factors. The second models the cellular scale, capturing how those genomic variations drive disease-associated cellular and pathway dysregulation. Their models nest: genotype informs cellular state, feeding toward Neuroverse, our neuro foundation-model layer, with precision psychiatry as an early proving ground, and the pair can grow toward connectomic and organismal scales over time. Both could instead go deep on one scale; we decide during project discovery.

The harder skill we teach is judgment. Rather than carrying one favored architecture and hunting for problems to fit it, a fellow learns to gather the biological requirements and constraints first, then build the model the problem actually needs. Claude accelerates every step: surveying literature, drafting and debugging model code, designing evaluations, and stress-testing assumptions. It is real research, scoped so an early-career scientist ships meaningful components with strong mentorship.

### Question 32, Which functional teams do you see the fellow supporting? (limit 1,500)

> The fellows sit at the scientific core of the organization: our research and model-development function. That is where our mission lives, so it is where a fellow has the greatest impact.

Day to day they build and evaluate foundation models, supervised by Ananth Grama on research direction and by Shahin Mohammadi on scientific and mission alignment. Because we are an AI-native research nonprofit, this is not a support function bolted onto operations; it is the work itself.

We are deliberately not placing fellows on fundraising or communications. They are scientist-engineers, and we want their year spent building, evaluating, and learning to lead technical projects, with Claude as a daily force-multiplier. We would sharpen each fellow's exact scope during project discovery so each owns a coherent piece of Cytoverse, our multiscale platform.

### Question 33, How is your organization currently using Claude or other AI tools? (limit 1,500)

> We use Claude daily and by design. As an AI-native research nonprofit, Claude is embedded in how we build, not run as a side experiment.

Primary use cases:
- Research and model development: surveying single-cell, genomics, and causal-AI literature; drafting and debugging code for Cytoverse, our multiscale biology platform; and designing evaluations.
- Scientific writing: structuring methods, results, and grant sections to funder and reviewer expectations.
- Communications and operations: producing accurate content and internal tooling, so a small team operates well beyond its headcount.

We build custom Claude skills and agent workflows, and we follow AI safety and privacy practice closely, which matters because our domain is health. A fellow joins an environment where using Claude well is already the norm, and helps us raise that bar.

### Question 34, What type of AI training and enablement have your staff members completed, if any? (limit 1,500)

> Our starting point is high fluency with informal structure.

Our leadership brings deep applied-AI expertise: a founder with 20 years spanning academia (MIT, the Broad Institute) and industry (insitro, GenBio AI), and a supervisor who directs a university physical-AI institute. Applied machine learning and responsible AI practice are native to how we work, as is the discipline of turning long-horizon research into milestone-based deliverables. Enablement to date has been expert-led and self-directed: hands-on Claude use, internal skills and playbooks, and close reading of Anthropic's safety guidance.

What we have not done is formal, structured training, which is where Claude Corps helps. We would gain from CodePath's programming and from fellows who bring fresh, structured practice we can turn into repeatable standards.

### Question 35, What do you hope the fellow learns or gains from working at your organization? (limit 1,500)

> A fellow leaves us with three things.

First, mentorship that is hard to find anywhere else. They work directly with Ananth Grama, Director of Purdue's Institute for Physical AI, and with our founder, who spent 20 years across academia (MIT, the Broad Institute) and industry (insitro, GenBio AI). That dual vantage matters: academia's ambitious, long-horizon, impact-centric thinking, aligned with industry's milestone-based delivery and OKRs. Fellows learn to be ambitious and to ship.

Second, the judgment that separates engineers from tool operators. Rather than carrying one method in search of a problem, a fellow learns to gather requirements and constraints, then build the model the problem needs.

Third, purpose. They build models meant to reach patients, on a mission with real human stakes, not abstract benchmarks. Fellows leave able to lead technical projects, fluent with Claude, and clear that their work can matter.

### Question 36, How will the work of a Claude Corps fellow advance your team's AI-related priorities and strategy? (limit 1,500)

> Beyond the first models, fellows raise our research capacity ceiling.

Today our modeling capacity is concentrated in our leadership. Every model, evaluation, and pipeline a fellow builds becomes durable infrastructure inside Cytoverse, our open multiscale platform, and its neuro layer, Neuroverse, which in turn grounds downstream tools like Yar, our on-device companion, and Brainscope, our sensing work. Every fellow we train to lead projects multiplies what a small team can attempt. That is the difference between using AI and compounding it.

It also advances our open-science strategy. We release models, tools, and data under open licenses, so what a fellow builds can be validated, reused, and extended by peer researchers and health nonprofits. This mirrors Claude Corps' own plan to open-source program infrastructure.

Strategically, fellows help prove that a small, mission-driven nonprofit can build foundation models at the level of far larger labs, aimed at a goal the market underserves: intercepting disease years before symptoms, for everyone.
