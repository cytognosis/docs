## Part B — Your Vision (Questions 30-36)

### Question 30 — Why does your organization want to participate in Claude Corps?

Cytognosis Foundation is a 501(c)(3) corporation building an open, AI-native infrastructure to make precision health broadly accessible. Our mission is personal. For decades, our founder was misdiagnosed across ten specialties until, as a computational biologist, he analyzed his own genome and found the single rare genetic variant that explained his symptoms. Cytognosis exists so no one else has to endure such hardship.

That origin sets our bar. We build AI services that reach patients and reshape clinical practice, not models that win a journal cover and are never used again.

We want fellows for two reasons. First, we need scientist-engineers to help build our nested, physically-grounded foundation models, and because we are AI-native, a fellow builds from day one rather than struggling to gain expertise in AI. Second, we care deeply about training people. A fellow learns to develop and lead real-world modeling projects under two demanding yet dedicated mentors and leaves with the unique ability to build powerful models for hard problems, which is exactly the talent this field needs.

### Question 31 — One or two AI-related challenges or opportunities for a fellow

Both fellows work as scientist-engineers building novel foundation models for biology, models that are physically grounded and nested across scales, integrating diverse data modalities and associated uncertainties.

We will split them across complementary scales: One fellow models the genomic scale, learning disease- and phenotype-associated genomic archetypes, embeddings, and latent factors. The second models the cellular scale, capturing how those genomic variations drive disease-associated cellular and pathway dysregulation. Their models nest: genotype informs cellular state, and the fellows' joint efforts scale to connectomic and organismal scales over time.

The harder skill we teach is judgment. Rather than carrying one favored architecture and hunting for problems to fit it, a fellow learns to gather the biological requirements and clinical constraints first, then build the model the problem actually needs. Claude accelerates every step: surveying literature, drafting and debugging model code, designing evaluations, and stress-testing assumptions. It is ambitious research, scoped so that an early-career scientist delivers highly-impactful solutions with strong mentorship. The models in turn feed back into Anthropic's scientific AI ecosystem.

### Question 32 — Which functional teams do you see the fellow supporting?

The fellows sit at the scientific core of the organization: our research and model-development function. That is where our mission lives, and this is where a fellow makes the greatest impact.

On a daily basis, they build and evaluate foundation models, supervised by Ananth Grama on research direction and by Shahin Mohammadi on scientific and mission alignment. Because we are an AI-native research nonprofit, this is not a support function bolted onto operations; it is the institution's core mission.

We will not place fellows on outreach, fundraising, or communications. They are scientist-engineers, and we want their time and effort spent on building, evaluating, and learning to lead technical projects, with Claude as a daily force multiplier. We will sharpen each fellow's exact technical scope during project discovery so each owns a coherent piece of the platform.

### Question 33 — How is your organization currently using Claude or other AI tools?

We use Claude daily and by design. As an AI-native research nonprofit, Claude is embedded in how we build, not run as an auxiliary device.

Primary use cases:

- Research and model development: surveying single-cell, genomics, and causal-AI literature, drafting and debugging model code, and designing evaluations.
- Scientific writing: structuring methods, results, and grant sections tailored to grant programs and reviewer expectations.
- Communications and operations: producing accurate content and internal tooling, enabling a small team to operate well beyond its headcount.

We build custom Claude skills and agent workflows, and we follow AI safety and privacy practices closely, which matters because our domain is health. A fellow joins an environment where using Claude well is already the norm, and helps us raise that bar.

### Question 34 — What AI training and enablement have your staff completed?

Our starting point is high fluency with informal structure. Our leadership brings deep applied AI expertise: a founder with 20 years spanning academia (MIT, the Broad Institute) and industry (insitro, GenBio AI), and a supervisor who directs Purdue's physical AI institute, which provides a research umbrella for all AI on campus. Applied machine learning and responsible AI practice are native to how we work, as is the discipline of turning long-horizon research into milestone-based deliverables. Enablement to date has been expert-led and self-directed: hands-on Claude use, internal skills and playbooks, and close reading of Anthropic's safety guidance. What we have not done is formal, structured training, which is where Claude Corps helps. We would gain from CodePath's programming and from fellows who bring fresh, structured practice we can turn into repeatable standards.

### Question 35 — What do you hope the fellow learns or gains?

A fellow leaves us with three things.

First, mentorship that is hard to find anywhere else. They work directly with Ananth Grama, who brings extensive academic and fundamental research experience, and with our founder, who spent 20 years in academia and industry (insitro, GenBio AI) focusing on deep R&D and effective translation. That dual vantage matters: academia's ambitious, long-horizon, impact-centric thinking, aligned with industry's milestone-based delivery and OKRs. Fellows learn to be ambitious and to ship.

Second, the judgment that separates talented engineers from tool operators. Rather than carrying a methodological toolkit in search of a problem, a fellow learns to gather requirements and constraints, then build powerful deployable solutions the problem needs.

Third, purpose. They build solutions meant to reach and impact patients, on a mission with real human stakes rather than abstract benchmarks. Fellows leave with the ability to lead technical projects, fluent with Claude, and with knowledge that their work can make a profound difference.

### Question 36 — How will the fellow's work advance your AI priorities and strategy?

Beyond the high-impact personalized clinical models, fellows raise our research capacity ceiling.

Today, our modeling capacity is concentrated in our leadership. Every model, evaluation, and pipeline a fellow builds becomes durable research infrastructure, and every fellow we train to lead projects multiplies what a small team can accomplish. That is the difference between using AI and compounding it.

It also advances our open-science strategy. We release models, tools, and data under open licenses, so what a fellow builds can be validated, reused, and extended by peer researchers and health-related nonprofits. This mirrors Claude Corps' own plan to open-source program infrastructure.

Strategically, fellows help prove that a small, mission-driven nonprofit can build foundation models at a level comparable to far larger labs, aimed at a stakeholder base the market underserves: intercepting disease years before symptoms, for everyone.
