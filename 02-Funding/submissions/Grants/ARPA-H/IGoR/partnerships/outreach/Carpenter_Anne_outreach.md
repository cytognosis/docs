**Subject:** Following up on our TBX1 thread, and an ARPA-H opportunity

Hi Anne,

It has been a couple of months since we traded notes on the TBX1 paired-line idea, and a lot has come together since, in a direction that runs right through your work. Before the business: congratulations again on the IPAI role. Ananth is thrilled, and so am I that our paths are converging at Purdue.

I also have some exciting news to share. IPAI is leading a team, PsychIGoR, for ARPA-H's IGoR program, a new opportunity to realize the "lab-in-the-loop" vision that many of us, Aviv Regev among them, have argued for over the years. IGoR funds multi-institutional, deeply collaborative teams of engineers and scientists to build a bidirectional loop between modeling and hypothesis generation on one side and experimental design and validation on the other, what the program describes as "an AI-enabled, interoperable research ecosystem that produces validated knowledge at least 10x faster than conventional approaches."

Our proposal is to build a self-updating, mechanistic causal model of psychiatric disease, starting with schizophrenia and extending the platform to bipolar disorder in Phase III, that designs its own next experiments and validates them in iPSC-derived neural models across penetrant genetic forms such as 22q11.2DS, which you and Matt profiled in your recent paper, with morphological, transcriptomic, and functional readouts. One core idea is to pair primary clinical -omics (including my joint work with Brad Ruzicka at McLean, through PsychENCODE) with perturbation studies in disease-relevant cellular models such as iPSC-derived NGN2 neurons, co-embedding the factors representing natural genomic variation and induced perturbations in a shared space. That pairing lets the cellular perturbations supply causal ground truth and the clinical data supply population-scale relevance, while separating in-vitro artifacts, such as perturbation effects on differentiation trajectory, from disease signal rather than mistaking them for it.

With that said, Ananth and I came away from our meeting earlier today of one mind: your expertise in computational morphology and cellular-imaging models is a perfect fit, and we would love to have you on board, if you have the interest and the bandwidth. The division of labor we have in mind plays to your strengths: Matt Tegtmeyer's lab runs the wet-lab experiments, and your role would be the computational morphology and imaging-model lead, producing interpretable readout models that connect the experimental data to TA1. The team and scope are still being finalized, so there is real room to shape the role around what excites you, and Ananth, as PI, can give you the full picture of the program and the IPAI structure.

One timing note: the first milestone, the Solution Summary, is due June 25, so a short call in the next few days would be ideal; otherwise we can keep it async and talk when it suits you.

In either case, welcome aboard again, and I look forward to working together, whether as part of PsychIGoR or independently.
