## Abstract / BLUF

This review surveys the scientific and technical foundations of the IGoR TA1 Comprehensive Disease Models component, from the mathematical theory of causal disentanglement through to the practical data, computational, and standards infrastructure that our four-pillar architecture assembles. The central thesis -- that disease-associated genetic variation acts as a **soft intervention on a latent causal model of cellular biological processes**, for which identifiability guarantees exist (Zhang et al. NeurIPS 2023) -- is not a framing convenience; it is a mathematically precise restatement of the causal disentanglement problem that opens four lines of work simultaneously: (1) the sVAE lineage provides identifiable latent disentanglement, (2) conditional flow-matching generative models provide scalable, distribution-faithful sample synthesis from control to disease states, (3) graph-neural-network inverse-design tools (PDGrapher) provide intervention prioritization, and (4) single-cell foundation models provide the atlas-scale priors needed to ground each of these in real cellular biology. Genomic factorization converts patient genomes into the soft-intervention parameters; the standards stack (DisMech/Disease Maps, GO-CAM, INDRA, Biolink, LinkML) provides the interoperable knowledge representation. Gaps remain at the boundary of causal identifiability under polygenic disease signal, at the molecular-to-circuit scale transition, and in the temporal-disease-progression schema, each of which is an addressable TA1 deliverable rather than a blocker.

## 1. Mathematical Foundation: Mechanism Sparsity, Identifiability, and the sVAE Lineage

### 1.1 The Sparse Mechanism Shift Hypothesis

The foundational prior for our TA1 causal architecture is the **sparse mechanism shift (SMS) hypothesis** (Scholkopf et al. 2021, PLoS Comput Biol, doi:10.1371/journal.pcbi.1008467). It states: when an intervention occurs in a causal system, only a small number of the system's mechanisms change, while all others remain invariant. Formally, if a data-generating process is governed by a set of causal mechanisms {f_i}, an intervention targeting variable X_i changes only the mechanism for X_i, leaving the remaining mechanisms intact.

The SMS hypothesis is biologically motivated: a CRISPR knockout of gene X, or a disease-associated variant at locus Y, perturbs a sparse subset of downstream biological process mechanisms rather than globally reorganizing the cell's regulatory state. This sparse change is precisely what distinguishes interventional distributions from observational distributions and makes causal structure learnable from multi-environment data. Our disease-as-perturbation reframing inherits this sparsity prior directly.

### 1.2 Mechanism Sparsity Regularization and Disentanglement: sVAE

Lachapelle et al. (CLeaR 2022, PMLR vol 140:1-57, arXiv:2107.10098) translated the SMS hypothesis into a practical disentanglement algorithm via **mechanism sparsity regularization**. The model assumes:

- Observations X^t are generated by a diffeomorphism f: Z -> X from unobserved latent variables Z^t
- Latents evolve through a transition function lambda(Z^{t-1}, A^{t-1}), where A^t is an observed auxiliary variable (perturbation label or action)
- The latent factors Z_i are conditionally independent given Z^{<t} and A^{<t}, following an exponential-family conditional density parameterized by the natural-parameter function lambda_i

The key insight is that the **causal graphical model G = [G^z G^a]** governing these transitions is learnable from data if regularized to be sparse. **Theorem 5** of Lachapelle et al. establishes permutation-identifiability: under sufficient time-variability, action-variability, sparsity of G^a, and a graphical criterion on the union of parent and child sets, there exists a permutation matrix P such that P G^z P^T = hat{G}^z and P G^a = hat{G}^a -- i.e., the learned graph is a permutation of the true causal graph.

The practical implementation is a **VAE** with binary-mask regularization on the learned adjacency matrix. Latents that participate in the same biological process cluster in the same latent dimension by construction, up to permutation. This established the first principled, identifiable disentanglement method grounded in causal theory for sequential biological perturbation data.

**Citation:** Lachapelle, S., Rodriguez Lopez, P., Sharma, Y., Everett, K., Le Priol, R., Lacoste, A., Lacoste-Julien, S. (2022). Disentanglement via Mechanism Sparsity Regularization: A New Principle for Nonlinear ICA. PMLR vol 140 (CLeaR), arXiv:2107.10098.

### 1.3 sVAE+ in Single-Cell Genomics

Lopez et al. (CLeaR 2023, arXiv:2301.12946) applied the SMS framework directly to single-cell perturbation data as **sVAE+**. Each genetic or chemical perturbation is treated as a stochastic soft intervention targeting an unknown, sparse subset of latent variables. The biologically meaningful interpretation: each latent unit Z_i represents "the activity of a distinct biological process." The learned sparse bipartite graph G^a identifies which biological processes are shifted by each perturbation.

The key technical departure from the original sVAE is a **Beta(1, K) prior on perturbation-target probabilities**, avoiding the hyperparameter sensitivity of the original binary masks. Models exploiting the SMS hypothesis significantly outperform non-causal baselines on out-of-distribution transfer learning tasks on CRISPR Perturb-seq data.

**Citation:** Lopez, R., Tagasovska, N., Ra, S., Cho, K., Pritchard, J., Regev, A. (2023). Learning Causal Representations of Single Cells via Sparse Mechanism Shift Modeling. CLeaR 2023, arXiv:2301.12946.

### 1.4 Sparse Additive Mechanism Shifts: SAMS-VAE

Bereket and Karaletsos (NeurIPS 2023, arXiv:2311.02794) advanced the SMS lineage as **SAMS-VAE (Sparse Additive Mechanism Shift VAE)**. The model decomposes each cell's latent state as:

> z_i = z^b_i + z^p_i,  where z^p_i = sum_{t} d_{i,t} (e_t circle m_t)

Here z^b_i is the **basal (healthy, intrinsic) state embedding**, e_t is a global latent effect vector for perturbation t, and m_t is a **binary mask** (Bernoulli prior with small alpha) enforcing sparsity. The mask identifies which biological process dimensions are shifted by perturbation t; the effect vector encodes direction and magnitude. Training uses **IWELBO (importance-weighted ELBO)**.

The identifiability claim is inherited from the SMS framework: sparsifying the shift mechanism recovers disentangled, perturbation-specific latent subspaces that **compose additively for combinatorial generalization**. SAMS-VAE is developed with contributions from the Cytognosis team at insitro and is the **immediate architectural predecessor of our TA1 Pillar 2 causal generative core**.

**Citation:** Bereket, M., Karaletsos, T. (2023). Modelling Cellular Perturbations with the Sparse Additive Mechanism Shift Variational Autoencoder. NeurIPS 2023, arXiv:2311.02794.

### 1.5 Identifiability from Soft Interventions: Zhang et al.

Zhang, Greenewald, Squires, Srivastava, Shanmugam, Uhler (NeurIPS 2023, arXiv:2307.06250) provide the deepest formal identifiability theory for our setting. The setup: observed variables X = f(U) where U ~ P_U factorizes according to an unknown DAG G. Interventions I_1, ..., I_K produce unpaired samples from modified distributions. Under a **generalized linear faithfulness assumption**, the paper proves:

- **Theorem 1 (ancestral relations):** Under Assumptions 1 (full-rank polynomial mixing) and 2 (linear interventional faithfulness), one can identify hat{G} = TS(G_pi) and intervention targets up to permutation, where TS denotes transitive closure.
- **Theorem 2 (direct edges and CD-equivalence):** Under Assumption 3 (no cancellation of path effects), (G, I_1, ..., I_K) is identifiable up to its **CD-equivalence class** -- permutation, scaling, and shifting of latent variables.

The **deep structural causal model (DSCM)** decoder proposed by Zhang et al. maps exogenous noise Z to causal variables U^obs via a structural causal model where the adjacency matrix A_theta encodes the parent set of each variable, and the causal mechanisms s_theta map parent assignments to each U_i. The DSCM decoder is the natural downstream module for our Pillar 2 architecture.

The practical algorithm is a **discrepancy-VAE** (CausalDiscrepancyVAE): an encoder q_phi maps observational samples to exogenous Z; the DSCM decoder generates observational and counterfactual samples; an **MMD-based discrepancy loss** aligns generated counterfactuals with real interventional samples without requiring cell-level pairing.

**Why disease genotype qualifies as a soft intervention:** disease-associated variants shift the activity of biological processes in a graded, probabilistic way (they alter conditional distributions p(U_i | pa_G(i)) rather than ablating parental dependence). This maps directly onto the soft-intervention setting, providing the theoretical guarantee that disease axes are recoverable from multi-cohort genetic and single-cell data.

**Citation:** Zhang, J., Greenewald, K., Squires, C., Srivastava, A., Shanmugam, K., Uhler, C. (2023). Identifiability Guarantees for Causal Disentanglement from Soft Interventions. NeurIPS 2023, arXiv:2307.06250.

### 1.6 sVAE-ligr: Mechanism Transportability Across Disease Domains

Hediyeh-zadeh, Fischer, Theis (ICLR 2024 Machine Learning for Genomics workshop, arXiv:2503.XXXXX) extend the sVAE family to the setting where the auxiliary variable (perturbation label, or disease identity) is **not directly observed**. Their **sVAE-ligr (SpikeSlabVAE with learnable interventions by Generative Replay)** uses Continual Learning techniques -- specifically Generative Replay -- to create "realizations" of the latent auxiliary variable, enabling:

- Sparse dependencies between latent factors and unobserved auxiliary variables, without requiring label-sample pairing
- **Mechanism transportability**: the model learns a mechanism in one context (e.g., a CRISPR screen in a cell line) and generates counterfactual instances of it in a different context (e.g., primary tumors from TCGA), enabling cross-domain transfer of disease mechanisms without re-perturbing the target domain

The key biological application demonstrated: generating counterfactual ARID1A knockout transcriptional signatures in primary tumors from TCGA (three cancer types: BLCA, KIRC, LUAD), showing statistically significant correlation between the generated signatures and the observed disease-vs-WT distribution. This is directly relevant to our Phase I/II use case: learning disease mechanisms from iPSC model systems and transferring them to clinical cohort data.

A spike-slab prior on latent factors enforces sparsity: z_i | a ~ gamma_i^a Normal(mu_i^a, 1) + (1 - gamma_i^a) Normal(0, 1), where gamma_i^a ~ Bernoulli(pi_i^a) and pi_i^a ~ Beta(1, tau_a) with tau_a predicted by a neural network from the auxiliary variable realizations.

**Citation:** Hediyeh-zadeh, S., Fischer, T., Theis, F.J. (2024). Disentanglement via Mechanism Sparsity by Replaying Realizations of the Past. ICLR 2024 Machine Learning for Genomics workshop.

### 1.7 SENA-discrepancy-VAE: Causal Identifiability in Biological Pathway Space

De la Fuente, Lehmann, Ruiz-Arenas, Voges, Marin-Goni, Martinez-de-Morentin, Gomez-Cabrero, Ochoa, Tegner, Lagani, Hernaez (ICLR 2025, arXiv:2506.12439) extend the Zhang et al. discrepancy-VAE to **incorporate GO biological processes as prior knowledge for the encoder**, producing latents that are simultaneously causally identifiable and biologically interpretable.

The key innovation is the **SENA-delta (SparsE Network Activity) encoder**: a two-layer masked MLP where the first layer connects gene expression to biological process activity levels via a pathway mask matrix M, defined as M_{i,k} = 1 if gene i in BP_k (else lambda for a small learnable leakage parameter). The activity levels alpha_k = sigma((W odot M)^T x) summarize the expression of genes in each biological process. A second fully-connected linear layer then maps alpha to the mean and variance of each causal exogenous variable Z_j, making each Z_j a **linear combination of biological process activities**.

This creates a two-level latent structure: **meta-pathway activities** Z_j (exogenous noise, biologically labeled) and **causal pathway archetypes** U_j (the causal latent variables, inferred by the DSCM decoder). The SENA-delta encoder is dropped into the discrepancy-VAE architecture in place of the standard fully-connected MLP encoder, without modifying the decoder or loss function.

Benchmarks on Norman et al. (K562, 105 single-gene perturbations + 131 double-gene perturbations) and Wessels et al. 2023 (THP1, 28 single + 158 double perturbations) demonstrate that SENA-discrepancy-VAE achieves **predictive performance comparable to the non-interpretable discrepancy-VAE** while providing direct mapping between latent factors and biological processes.

**Biological process selection criteria for our TA1:** use GO biological processes (removing those with < 30 genes to ensure statistical power, removing those sharing > 50% gene overlap to reduce redundancy, keeping only those with at least 5 genes represented in the single-cell dataset, and excluding ancestor terms). This is our Pillar 2 encoder design for the neuropsychiatric disease context.

**Citation:** de la Fuente, J., Lehmann, R., Ruiz-Arenas, C., Voges, J., Marin-Goni, I., Martinez-de-Morentin, X., Gomez-Cabrero, D., Ochoa, I., Tegner, J., Lagani, V., Hernaez, M. (2025). Interpretable Causal Representation Learning in Pathway Space. ICLR 2025, arXiv:2506.12439.

## 2. Conditional Flow-Matching Generative Models as Velocity-Network Regression of the Causal Mechanism

### 2.1 The Flow-Matching Framework

The four models reviewed here (CellFlow, CFGen, scDFM, and SPEAR) all implement perturbation prediction as **conditional flow matching (CFM)**: learning a time-dependent velocity field v_theta(x, t | condition) that solves the ODE dx/dt = v_theta(x, t | condition), transporting a source distribution p_0 (unperturbed cells) to a target distribution p_1 (perturbed cells). This is a velocity-network regression of the causal mechanism: the velocity field directly encodes the "direction of causal shift" in gene expression space under each perturbation condition.

The CFM objective is:

> L_CFM(theta) = E_{c_x, c_p} E_{x_0 ~ q_0, x_1 ~ q_1(cdot|c_x, c_p)} E_{t~U(0,1)} [||v_theta(x_t | t, c_x, c_p) - v(x | x_0, x_1, t)||^2]

where v(x | x_0, x_1, t) is the reference velocity along a predefined interpolation path pi_t(x_0, x_1).

The four models differ in (a) how they encode the condition, (b) what interpolation path they use (optimal transport vs. linear), (c) whether they operate in gene expression space directly or in a latent space, and (d) what architectural inductive biases they impose on the velocity network.

### 2.2 CellFlow: Optimal-Transport CFM with Heterogeneous Condition Encoding

Klein, Fleck, Bobrovskiy, et al. (bioRxiv 2025, doi:10.1101/2025.04.11.648220) introduce **CellFlow**, a flow-matching framework for modeling single-cell phenotypes induced by diverse perturbations. CellFlow makes three key design choices:

1. **Optimal Transport (OT) coupling**: cells are paired using mini-batch OT, implementing the least-action principle -- cells follow the most direct path in expression space under perturbation. This separates inherent cellular heterogeneity from perturbation-induced distributional changes.

2. **Heterogeneous condition encoding**: perturbations are encoded using pre-trained biological entity embeddings (molecular fingerprints for small molecules; ESM2 embeddings for gene knockout protein products), enabling zero-shot generalization to unseen perturbation types. Perturbation covariates (dose, timing) and sample covariates (cell line, tissue, batch) are encoded separately and aggregated with multi-head attention and Deep Sets, permutation-invariant to perturbation order.

3. **Low-dimensional PCA latent space**: the flow is learned in PCA space of single-cell data, with a learned ODE from t=0 to t=1 integrating the velocity field. At inference, samples are drawn from the predicted perturbed distribution by solving the ODE.

**Scale and validation:** CellFlow is trained on up to 10 million PBMCs (90 cytokines, 12 donors), demonstrating log-log scaling in the number of training conditions, with a linear fit (R^2 = 0.999) for 16, 32, 64, and 80 cytokines. It successfully predicts whole-embryo zebrafish development (ZSCAPE7 atlas, 23 knockouts across 5 developmental time points), and guides cell-fate engineering in organoid protocol optimization.

**Limitation for our use case:** CellFlow operates in PCA latent space and does not impose causal structure on the velocity network. It is primarily a generative perturbation-response model, not a causally identifiable disentangler. It does not recover the CD-equivalence class of the underlying causal model. Its role in our TA1 is as the **generative backbone for the cellular phenotype layer**, not as the causal identification engine.

**Citation:** Klein, D., Fleck, J.S., Bobrovskiy, D., et al. (2025). CellFlow enables generative single-cell phenotype modeling with flow matching. bioRxiv 2025.04.11.648220, doi:10.1101/2025.04.11.648220.

### 2.3 CFGen: Multi-Modal Multi-Attribute Flow for Discrete Single-Cell Data

Palma, Richter, Zhang, Lubetzki, Tong, Dittadi, Theis (ICLR 2025, arXiv:2407.11734) introduce **CFGen (CellFlow for Generation)**, a conditional flow-matching generative model designed specifically for the **discrete nature of single-cell RNA-seq count data**. Unlike CellFlow (which operates on normalized, continuous approximations), CFGen models the underlying count process explicitly.

CFGen defines a generative process p(x, z, l, y) factored as:

> p(x | z, l) p(z | l, y) p(l | y) p(y)

where z is the latent continuous cell state, l is the library size (total UMI counts), y is a categorical biological attribute (cell type, tissue, batch), and x is the discrete count vector. The decoder p(x | z, l) uses a **Negative Binomial (NB)** distribution: x ~ NB(i*softmax(h_psi(z_1)), theta), where h_psi maps the terminal latent state z_1 to NB parameters. For multi-modal data (scRNA-seq + scATAC-seq), a separate Bernoulli decoder p(b | z) handles binary accessibility peaks.

The **compositional guidance** extension for multi-attribute generation implements the flow-matching analog of classifier-free guidance:

> u_tilde(cdot | y) = (1 - omega) u_t(cdot) + omega u_t(cdot | y)

where omega controls the trade-off between diversity and attribute adherence. Multiple attributes are composed by summing per-attribute guidance fields, enabling simultaneous conditioning on cell type, tissue, and batch without training separate models.

**Key advantages over prior models for our TA1:** (a) correct NB noise model preserves count discreteness, critical for downstream statistical testing of mechanistic predictions; (b) multi-modal outputs (RNA + ATAC) from a single latent state support the multi-scale data assimilation our Pillar 1 network priors require; (c) compositional guidance enables counterfactual generation under multiple simultaneous perturbations.

**Limitation:** CFGen does not encode causal structure and is primarily a high-fidelity generative model. Its velocity network does not correspond to a mechanistic causal mechanism. Its role in TA1 is as the **high-fidelity counterfactual synthesis engine** for generating realistic patient-specific cellular profiles given inferred disease axes.

**Citation:** Palma, A., Richter, T., Zhang, H., Lubetzki, M., Tong, A., Dittadi, A., Theis, F.J. (2025). Multi-Modal and Multi-Attribute Generation of Single Cells with CFGen. ICLR 2025, arXiv:2407.11734.

### 2.4 scDFM: Distributional Flow Matching with PAD-Transformer

Yu, Wang, Liao, Wu (ICLR 2026, arXiv:2602.07103) introduce **scDFM (single-cell Distributional Flow Matching)**, addressing two limitations of prior flow-matching perturbation models: (a) neglect of population-level distributional fidelity (most models minimize cell-level MSE, missing distributional shifts), and (b) failure to capture gene co-expression regulatory dependencies.

scDFM makes two primary innovations:

1. **MMD regularization for distributional fidelity**: the training objective combines the standard CFM trajectory loss with a population-level Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) penalty directly comparing the terminal distribution of generated cells against the empirical post-perturbation distribution:

   > L = L_CFM + lambda L_MMD,  where L_MMD = E[k_mix(x_1, hat{x}_1)]

   with a multi-kernel squared MMD using RBF kernels at multiple bandwidths. This ensures the model reproduces the full distributional shift induced by perturbations rather than merely the mean expression profile.

2. **Perturbation-Aware Differential Transformer (PAD-Transformer)**: a novel backbone architecture consisting of (a) a **gene-gene co-expression attention mask** derived from Pearson co-expression correlations (sparse KNN adjacency A) that constrains attention to biologically relevant gene neighbors, and (b) **differential attention** that contrasts control and perturbation hidden states to sharpen attention to perturbation-specific expression changes, applying softmax(QY K_Y^T / sqrt{d_h}) - lambda softmax(QX K_X^T / sqrt{d_h}).

**Benchmark results:** scDFM reduces MSE by 19.6% over the strongest baseline (CPA) on the Norman combinatorial perturbation dataset in the holdout setting. It outperforms CPA, GEARS, CellFlow, and foundation models (Geneformer, scGPT) across Norman (single/double gene KO), ComboSciPlex (drug combinations), and Parse-PBMC (cytokine) benchmarks on distributional metrics (Discrimination Score, DE-Spearman-LFC).

**Role in our TA1:** The PAD-Transformer backbone and MMD distributional objective are architectural choices we adopt or adapt in our Pillar 2 causal generative model. The differential transformer is a natural place to encode mechanism sparsity constraints, since the perturbation-vs-control attention differential can be regularized to be sparse (mapping onto the SAMS-VAE mask m_t). The gene-gene co-expression attention mask is aligned with our CS-CORE-informed decoder design (Pillar 2).

**Citation:** Yu, C., Wang, C., Liao, B., Wu, T. (2026). scDFM: Distributional Flow Matching for Robust Single-Cell Perturbation Prediction. ICLR 2026, arXiv:2602.07103. Code: https://github.com/AI4Science-WestlakeU/scDFM.

### 2.5 SPEAR: Spectral Adaptive Repositioning for Flow-Based Perturbation Modeling

**SPEAR (SPEctral Adaptive Repositioning)** addresses a fundamental architectural gap shared by all flow-matching perturbation models including scDFM: existing approaches either assign genes arbitrary positional indices (Geneformer, scGPT), use fixed binary attention masks derived from control-state co-expression (scDFM, GEARS), or ignore gene identity structure entirely (CPA). None encodes the co-expression network topology as a **continuous geometric prior** that adapts dynamically to perturbation context.

SPEAR addresses this with two complementary positional mechanisms:

1. **Spectral Co-expression Encoding (SPE):** Gene positional information is derived from the **eigenvectors of the co-expression graph Laplacian** L = I - D^{-1/2} A D^{-1/2}, where A is the sparse KNN co-expression adjacency. The spectral coordinate of gene g_i is s_i = U_{i, 1:K+1} in R^K, where U is the eigenvector matrix. These coordinates are stored in a lookup tensor S in R^{|V| x K} and projected to per-head rotation angles via a learnable tensor Omega in R^{H x (d_h/2) x K}. Angle theta_{h,i,n} = sum_k Omega_{h,n,k} s_{i,k} enters a RoPE-style rotation, rotating query-key products to encode topology-aware gene proximity as a continuous geometric prior.

2. **Adaptive Repositioning (AR):** Gene positions update dynamically from the perturbation-conditioned hidden state at every backbone layer. At layer l, the hidden state h_i^{(l)} of gene i is projected to a scalar position per attention head via a SwiGLU gate: r_i = sigma_SiLU(h_i^T W_g) odot (h_i^T W_c), then z_{i,h} = r_i w_{z,h}. This scalar is converted to rotary angles at geometric frequencies and added to the spectral component, allowing attention geometry to reorganize dynamically under perturbation without explicit supervision on gene-gene relationships.

**Benchmark results (from manuscript, internal):** SPEAR outperforms CPA, GEARS, Geneformer, scGPT, STATE, and CellFlow across combinatorial genetic (Norman holdout single and double), pharmacological (ComboSciPlex), and cytokine (Parse-PBMC) perturbation benchmarks on Spearman-LFC, Pearson-Delta, Discrimination Score, and Direction Match. The largest relative gains are on Spearman-LFC and Pearson-Delta, confirming that the spectral and adaptive positional encodings contribute beyond the scDFM backbone and training objective alone. SPEAR also captures latent positional relationships that closely map to known transcription factor-target interactions.

**Fit to TA1 thesis:** SPE plus AR give the causal model a gene-identity prior that is biologically structured (co-expression graph geometry) and perturbation-aware (functional rewiring encoded dynamically). This is the gene-identity representation layer of our Pillar 2 causal generative model. The spectral coordinates are a natural link to the SAMS-VAE mask structure: genes in the same spectral community (co-expression cluster) should have correlated mask entries in the sparse mechanism shift.

## 3. Graph and Intervention-Design Models: PDGrapher

### 3.1 PDGrapher: Inverse-Design GNN for Therapeutic Perturbagens

Gonzalez, Lin, Herath, Veselkov, Bronstein, Zitnik (Nature Biomedical Engineering 2025, doi:10.1038/s41551-025-01481-x) introduce **PDGrapher**, a two-module causally inspired graph neural network for **inverse perturbation design**: given a diseased cell state, identify the set of therapeutic gene or chemical targets that will shift the cell toward a healthy treated state.

PDGrapher solves the inverse problem rather than the forward problem. Existing perturbation predictors (CPA, scGen, GEARS) predict how a cell responds to a given perturbation. PDGrapher instead directly predicts which perturbation elicits a desired response, given a paired disease-to-treatment training set.

**Architecture:** Both modules are GNNs operating over a proxy causal graph (PPI network from BIOGRID: 10,716 nodes, 151,839 undirected edges; or GRN from GENIE3: ~10,000 nodes, ~500,000 directed edges). Interventions are represented as **edge mutilations** on the causal graph, removing edges incident to the targeted gene nodes:

- **Perturbagen discovery module f_p:** takes concatenated diseased (x^d) and healthy (x^h) gene expression profiles as node features; outputs per-gene perturbagen scores U' as candidate therapeutic targets. Trained with a cross-entropy cycle loss: CE(x^t, f_r(x^d, f_p(x^d, x^t)))), ensuring that predicted targets, when fed through the response prediction module, return a treated expression profile consistent with x^t.

- **Response prediction module f_r:** takes a diseased cell state and a predicted perturbagen set U'; predicts the gene expression response of the cell to perturbing each gene in U'. Trained with supervised cross-entropy directly on the known therapeutic targets CE(U', f_p(x^d, x^t)).

**Benchmark results on 38 datasets (9 cancer cell lines, 11 cancer types, 2 causal graph types):** PDGrapher outperforms all competing methods (ChemCPA, Biolord, GEARS, CellIOT, scGen) on chemical perturbation datasets by 7.73-13.37% (accurately predicted samples), with up to 0.13 higher nDCG on breast cancer. For genetic datasets, performance is more modest but PDGrapher maintains the highest overall ranking (statistically significant Mann-Whitney p < 0.001). Training speed: up to 25x faster than indirect methods like scGen and CellIOT. Ground-truth therapeutic targets predicted by PDGrapher are on average 11.58% closer to true therapeutic targets in the gene-gene interaction network than expected by chance.

**Integration in our TA1 Pillar 2:** PDGrapher is adopted as the **intervention-design component** of Pillar 2. Phase 1 (f_p) proposes perturbagens that shift a diseased state toward healthy; Phase 2 (f_r) predicts the cellular response to a candidate perturbation. Interventions are represented as edge mutilations on the Pillar 1 mechanistic network. The output of PDGrapher is the ranked experiment-candidate list that Pillar 4 hands to TA2 for value-of-information scoring.

**Key gap addressed:** PDGrapher requires paired disease-treated samples for training. In our Phase I setting (22q11DS iPSC neurons), we generate this pairing via the CRISPR-corrected isogenic control design: the diseased line is the TBX1 exon 2 CNV line; the treated line is the CRISPR-corrected isogenic control. For Phase III (idiopathic schizophrenia), paired samples must be derived from the clinical cohort data using the Pillar 3 joint shift-space representation.

**Citation:** Gonzalez, G., Lin, X., Herath, I., Veselkov, K., Bronstein, M., Zitnik, M. (2025). Combinatorial prediction of therapeutic perturbations using causally inspired neural networks. Nature Biomedical Engineering, doi:10.1038/s41551-025-01481-x.

## 4. Single-Cell Foundation Models and the Virtual-Cell World Model

### 4.1 Correlational Foundation Models: The Gap They Cannot Fill

The landscape of correlational single-cell foundation models (scGPT, Geneformer, scFoundation, UCE, TranscriptFormer, scPRINT) is well-surveyed in IGoR Research Master Section 20.1. The key finding is **categorical**: none natively support causal perturbation prediction. The 2025 Arc Institute Virtual Cell Challenge demonstrated that no pure foundation model outperformed conventional statistical methods on perturbation prediction (Eisenstein, Nature 2026). The gap motivates the shift to world-model and causal architectures.

Foundation models are **not useless** in our architecture: their pre-trained embeddings (gene identity, cell type, tissue context) serve as **initialization priors** for the Pillar 1 network and the Pillar 2 gene-identity encoder. Geneformer embeddings are used in GEARS; ESM2 embeddings are used in CellFlow for gene perturbation encoding. We use scGPT or scFoundation embeddings as initial gene-identity representations that SPEAR's spectral co-expression encoding then refines.

### 4.2 AlphaCell: OT-CFM at Atlas Scale

Chuai et al. (bioRxiv 2026, cited in Research Master Section 20.4) propose **AlphaCell**, a three-component world model trained on 220 million cells including 90 million perturbed profiles. AlphaCell uses **Optimal Transport CFM (OT-CFM)** over a genome-wide Virtual Cell Space, providing the largest-scale implementation of the flow-matching perturbation paradigm. AlphaCell demonstrates strong scaling behavior but remains anchored to the CRISPR/chemical perturbation paradigm and does not treat disease-associated genetic variation as the causal operator.

**Our use:** AlphaCell is a baseline and a data resource. Its pre-trained OT-CFM velocity network can initialize Pillar 2 flow parameters; its Virtual Cell Space provides a shared coordinate for normalizing cross-cohort expression profiles.

### 4.3 STACK: In-Context Learning for Whole-Organism Perturbation

Dong et al. (bioRxiv 2026) propose **STACK**, an in-context learning framework trained on 149 million cells including the Perturb Sapiens whole-organism perturbation atlas. STACK demonstrates partial causality through in-context generalization to unseen cell types and perturbation combinations. Its multi-scale awareness (across 24 cell types, whole-organism context) is relevant to our Pillar 3 joint shift-space, which must reconcile cellular and clinical evidence scales.

### 4.4 X-Cell/X-Atlas: Diffusion LM at Scale

Wang et al. (bioRxiv 2026) propose **X-Cell**, a diffusion language model trained on 25.6 million CRISPRi Perturb-seq cells at 4.9 billion parameters (X-Cell-Ultra). X-Cell demonstrates power-law scaling and multi-modal priors but remains anchored to discrete gene perturbation labels rather than continuous genetic variation. X-Cell-Ultra's embedding space is a candidate external prior for Pillar 1's gene-regulatory network.

### 4.5 Virtual Cell World Model: The Operational Framework

Xing and Song (GenBio AI 2026) provide the sharpest operational definition of the virtual cell as an AI-driven world model, establishing a formal framework with direct relevance to our TA1 architecture:

**Operational definition:** p(x' | x, a, e) -- next cell state x' conditioned on current state x, intervention/action a, and cellular environment e.

**Three-component architecture:**

1. **Structured encoder E:** z = E(x, G, e) in M, where G = (V, E) is the biological network graph and M is the learned cellular manifold. The encoder maps multi-modal observations (sequences, transcriptomic profiles, proteomic abundances, structural conformations, interaction profiles, pathway activations, morphological descriptors) together with the cellular micro-environment into a unified latent cellular manifold M. In practice, E is implemented as a GNN over G with transformer-style attention, combined with modality-specific encoders for each observable type.

2. **Action-conditioned transition core F:** z_{t+1} = F(z_t, a_t), an action-conditioned operator that evolves latent state on M. F integrates two complementary components: (a) a symbolic reasoning module operating over G that infers relational updates (pathway activation, network rewiring) in response to interventions; (b) a continuous latent dynamics module (diffusion or flow-based) that propagates these updates as smooth trajectories on M.

3. **Structured decoder D:** x' = D(z, G), producing coherent outputs across modalities (expression profiles, protein localization, interaction probabilities, structural conformations, morphological descriptors) under cross-modal consistency constraints.

**The key mapping to our TA1:** The VCWM transition core F is exactly our proposed causal perturbation operator: disease-associated genetic variation constitutes the action a that drives state evolution on the cellular manifold M. This transforms the genotype-phenotype problem into action-conditioned latent state evolution, for which the identifiability guarantees of Zhang et al. 2023 apply under the soft-intervention setting. The encoder E is our Pillar 1 (network priors) plus Pillar 2 (causal generative core). The decoder D is our covariance-preserving, vine-copula-based decoder (scDesign3, CS-CORE). The VCWM operational definition thus provides the formal system-level specification that our four-pillar architecture instantiates.

**Data requirements per VCWM:** (a) multi-modal atlas data for encoder-decoder learning (state manifold); (b) perturbation-response paired data for transition-core learning (one-step dynamics, provided by TA4 Perturb-seq, Cell Painting); (c) temporal trajectory data for stable global flow learning (live-cell imaging from Cellanome R3200 Perturb-LINK, RNA velocity from scVelo/CellRank).

**Citation:** Xing, E., Song, L. (2026). A World Model of the Virtual Cell. GenBio AI technical report.

## 5. Genomic Factorization as Input Building Blocks

### 5.1 The Role of Genomic Factorization in TA1

The sVAE/SAMS-VAE/Zhang et al. identifiability framework requires that the disease-perturbation operator be encoded as a **soft intervention parameter** that enters the latent causal model. In the CRISPR/chemical perturbation setting, this parameter is an observed discrete label (the gene knocked out, the compound administered). In our disease setting, the parameter must be derived from patient genomes.

This derivation is **Pillar 2b (factorized-PRS)**, which is proprietary and documented in Research Master Section 31. Here we describe the published landscape that contextualizes and defends its novelty.

### 5.2 Multi-Trait Genomic Factorization

The key published methods for factorizing GWAS summary statistics into shared latent axes are:

- **Genomic SEM** (Grotzinger et al. Nature Hum Behav 2019, doi:10.1038/s41562-019-0566-x): structural equation modeling on the LDSC genetic covariance matrix; the 5-factor model explaining 66% of per-disorder genetic variance across 14 psychiatric disorders (Nature 2025, doi:10.1038/s41586-025-09820-3) is the genomic scaffold our axes align to.
- **GLEANR** (AJHG 2025, bioRxiv 2024.11.12.623313): sparse matrix factorization of the variant-by-phenotype Z-score matrix with explicit sample-sharing noise handling; produces sparse, pathway-enriched factors that replicate across biobanks. This is the discovery engine we use for transdiagnostic axis identification.
- **FactorGo** (Zhang et al. AJHG 2023, doi:10.1016/j.ajhg.2023.09.003): variational Bayesian factor analysis with ARD prior on Pan-UKBB sumstats; provides loading uncertainty for propagation into per-person PRS.
- **DeGAs** (Tanigawa et al. Nat Commun 2019, doi:10.1038/s41467-019-11953-9): truncated SVD on variant x phenotype Z matrix; closest published precedent for a data-driven decomposition of the genetic architecture across traits.

### 5.3 Multi-Trait Polygenic Scoring

The published multi-trait PRS comparators (wMT-SBLUP, PRSmix/PRSmix+, PPRS, PRScsx, SDPRx) provide population-level multi-trait scores but do not produce per-person, per-pathway axes. Our factorized PRS is different in kind: it is a per-person coordinate system in the space of biological processes, not an additive risk sum.

The nearest published method is **PRSet/PRSice-2** (Choi et al. 2020): pathway-restricted polygenic scores computed by partitioning the genome by annotated gene sets. PRSet produces one score per pathway per person but does not learn a disentangled factorization, does not enforce sparsity, and is not tied to a latent causal model. Our Pillar 2b advances beyond PRSet by learning per-person genome axes as sparse mechanism identifications expressed over TF-binding-footprint regions (CREB, SREBP programs), binding them to the SAMS-VAE-style latent intervention so the axes are causal coordinates rather than additive risk sums.

### 5.4 Genomic Foundation Models as Functional Priors

AlphaGenome (Avsec et al. Nature 2026, doi:10.1038/s41586-025-10014-0), Borzoi (Linder et al. Nat Genet 2024, doi:10.1038/s41588-024-02053-6), and Evo 2 (Nature 2026, arXiv:2502.18638) provide sequence-to-regulatory-grammar representations that we use as **functional priors** for fine-mapping disease variants onto biological processes. The validated chain: score GWAS credible-set variants with Borzoi/AlphaGenome in brain tracks (allelic difference or in-silico mutagenesis), feed predicted functional effects as continuous annotations into PolyFun/SuSiE for fine-mapping, use fine-mapped posterior variant sets as input to TF-binding-footprint region definitions for Pillar 2b.

AlphaGenome resolves direction for 49% of GWAS credible sets (complementary to COLOC) and is Apache-2.0 (code) with noncommercial weights. We use it as a tool for COMT/TBX1/DGCR8 regulatory effect scoring in the Phase I 22q11DS anchor, not as a retrained model (per Research Master Section 20.6).

### 5.5 Genomic-to-Disease-Axis Front-End (Detailed Architecture; PROPRIETARY)

> [!CAUTION]
> **PROPRIETARY, CROWN-JEWEL IP. Internal only.** This section elaborates the method behind Research Master Section 31b. Do not include in the `shareable` build, partner-facing materials, or any non-proprietary part of an ARPA-H submission. Mark any pages that must appear in a submission "Proprietary."

This front-end converts a patient genome (or GWAS summary statistics) into a small set of interpretable, sparse **disease-axis factors** that condition the generative core, replacing the fixed hand-engineered PRS scores used by all prior approaches.

**Step 1: Sequence to gene embeddings.** Initialize from pretrained genomic foundation models -- **AlphaGenome, VariantFormer** (Casale, Widmer, et al.; see Step 1 note below), **Evo 2** -- to map base-pair sequence to embeddings, focusing on **gene bodies and regulatory regions**. VariantFormer-style **cross-attention** contextualizes the gene-body embeddings with flanking regulatory-region representations, capturing the cis-regulatory grammar that links individual variants to the gene-level functional consequence. An auxiliary gene-expression-prediction head (optional) can sharpen the embeddings during fine-tuning, but the primary path uses the **contextualized gene-sequence embeddings** directly as input to Step 2.

**Collaboration note.** A potential collaboration with **Francesco Paolo Casale** (a senior VariantFormer author, previously at insitro) is being tracked to cover this sequence-to-embedding map, either within IGoR or as an independent joint project. If realized, Casale's group contributes the pretrained VariantFormer weights and fine-tuning protocol; Cytognosis contributes the downstream factorization and causal model integration.

**Step 2: Project onto a gene functional network.** A projection head (output dimension D) places the contextualized gene embeddings as **initial node embeddings** on a gene functional network (STRING functional associations, GRN edges from PsychENCODE brainSCOPE, or a composite graph). This ties sequence-derived functional information into the topology of known gene relationships.

**Step 3: Multiresolution graph diffusion.** Expand context from a gene's local regulatory neighborhood to **cross-gene relationships** with a **multiresolution graph-diffusion** operator in the spirit of graph wavelets (WaveGC style). This produces node embeddings that reflect not just a gene's own sequence context but the functional context of its network neighborhood at multiple resolution scales. Where the graph carries **typed, signed, and weighted edges** (activating vs. repressing regulation, physical vs. co-expression association), a relation-aware propagation operator such as **NBFNet (Neural Bellman-Ford Networks)** handles edge heterogeneity correctly by decomposing multi-hop path scores along typed edge paths.

**Step 4: Archetypal factorization into disease axes.** Learn **k archetypes** over the final node embeddings. Each archetype is a **convex weight vector over nodes** (nonneg, summing to one), so the archetype representation is the convex combination of node embeddings defined by that weight vector. The simplex constraint makes the weight vectors inherently **sparse**: most nodes receive near-zero weight, and only a small, pathway-coherent subset define each archetype. Two design choices are available and may be selected empirically:

- **Archetypal analysis (primary).** The simplex constraint may additionally be reinforced with **graph-total-variation (graph-TV) regularization**, which penalizes large differences between the weights assigned to adjacent nodes in the gene network, producing graph-aware sparsity and biologically coherent archetypes.
- **Sparse autoencoders (alternative).** If the archetypal simplex constraint proves too rigid for the data geometry, sparse autoencoders provide a more flexible sparse factorization with explicit L1 sparsity on the encoder activations.

The result of either approach is a set of **k disease-axis factors of dimension D**, each acting as a candidate disease axis or transdiagnostic biotype that conditions the downstream generative model.

**Step 5: Deep structural causal model over the axes.** The k genomic factors additionally serve as **exogenous variables of a deep structural causal model** (Zhang et al. discrepancy-VAE style). The deep SCM simultaneously learns the **causal interactions among the disease axes** -- a directed graphical structure over the k axes, not just their individual effects -- rather than treating them as independent. This enables the model to distinguish axes that causally drive one another from axes that merely co-occur. The deep SCM over axes is the disease-effect operator of the three-latent SCM (Section 7.2).

**Integration with the generative core.** The k disease-axis factors, together with their causal structure from the deep SCM, flow into the SAMS-VAE additive mechanism-shift layer as the soft-intervention parameters z^d. The generative model (Pillar 2) is therefore conditioned on genomically grounded, causally structured, sparse disease axes rather than raw genotype or hand-engineered scores.

**How this differs from all prior work.** No published method chains pretrained genomic foundation model embeddings through graph diffusion, archetypal factorization, and a deep causal model over the factors into a generative single-cell model. The nearest published comparators are: (a) PRSet/PRSice-2 (pathway-restricted additive scores; no factorization, no causal structure), (b) FactorGo (variational factor analysis of summary statistics; no per-person causal model), (c) GLEANR (sparse matrix factorization at the variant-by-phenotype level; no gene-network contextualization). Our method is different in kind from all three.

### 5.6 Sparse LD Infrastructure

**LDGM precision matrices** (O'Connor lab, Nat Genet 2023, doi:10.1038/s41588-023-01487-8) provide sparse inverses of the LD correlation matrix, derived from ancestral-recombination-graph genealogies. They cover ~18 million variants across five ancestry groups (AFR, AMR, EAS, EUR, SAS from 1000 Genomes tree sequences) and deliver 10-100x speedups on LD operations. Production toolkits: **graphld** (Python, O'Connor lab) and **score** (Giulio Genovese bcftools plugin). This is the LD reference infrastructure for scalable, multi-ancestry Pillar 2b computation.

## 6. Standards and Schemas: DisMech, Biolink, and LinkML

### 6.1 Why Standards Matter for TA1

The IGoR program requires TA1 models to be **containerized and independently executable** by an IV&V partner (Phase I milestone: 100% of computational artifacts). This requires that the knowledge representation, causal statements, and model interfaces use machine-readable, open standards. Standards also provide the semantic backbone for TA2 (hypothesis extraction and ontology-aligned evidence retrieval) and TA3 (protocol metadata conformance).

### 6.2 DisMech: Disease Mechanism Representation

"DisMech" in earlier internal notes refers to the **Disease Maps** pattern (not a standalone ontology, but a standards pipeline): curated, comprehensive molecular-mechanism maps of a specific disease, stored in CellDesigner with SBGN semantics, annotated via MIRIAM, and runnable through:

> Literature curation -> CellDesigner (SBGN + SBML) -> CaSQ -> SBML-qual -> MaBoSS -> state-probability time courses

The Monarch Initiative (https://github.com/monarch-initiative/dismech) maintains related phenotype-mechanism integration tools; the Disease Map Community (disease-maps.org) hosts CellDesigner maps for COVID-19, Parkinson's, and other diseases. **CaSQ** (PMC7575051) automates Boolean model inference from CellDesigner maps; **MaBoSS** (Stoll et al. BMC Bioinformatics 2012) simulates the SBML-qual Boolean model into stochastic state-probability time courses.

**Our TA1 use:** We build a schizophrenia/22q11DS Disease Map in CellDesigner, encompassing the TBX1/COMT/DGCR8 -> oligodendrocyte lineage -> thalamocortical circuit causal chain. The CaSQ -> SBML-qual -> MaBoSS pipeline provides the verifiable mechanistic layer that IV&V can execute independently. This is deposited as OMEX/COMBINE archives for full reproducibility.

### 6.3 Biolink Model

The **Biolink Model** (Unni et al. arXiv:2203.13906) is a YAML-based linked-data modeling language that defines a knowledge graph metagraph schema for biomedical entities and relationships. Key properties:

- **Per-edge provenance**: every edge has a required `has_evidence` attribute linking to PMIDs and ECO codes
- **Typed predicates with directionality**: `causally upstream of`, `positively regulates`, `negatively regulates`, with subproperties organized in a causal hierarchy via the Relation Ontology
- **Subject-predicate-object structure** compatible with KGs, enabling traversal for hypothesis retrieval and gap detection

We adopt Biolink as the **knowledge graph metagraph schema** for our Pillar 1 mechanistic network. All nine harmonized interaction databases (STRING, Reactome, SIGNOR, TFLink, IntAct, BioGRID, OmniPath, Co-abundance Atlas, PsychENCODE brainSCOPE) are harmonized to Biolink predicates before integration. This provides a uniform query interface for TA2 hypothesis generation.

### 6.4 LinkML

**LinkML (Linked data Modeling Language)** is a YAML-based schema-definition language that compiles to JSON Schema, OWL, SHACL, RDF, and Python dataclasses. It is the standard for defining open schemas that must be machine-readable, interoperable, and version-controlled.

We use LinkML for:

1. **TA3 protocol schemas**: all experiment metadata (intent, protocol parameters, calibration artifacts, QC thresholds) are defined as LinkML schemas, enabling validation of TA4 data returns against a formally specified schema before ingestion into TA1.
2. **IGoR common data model**: the structured data package returned from TA4 to TA1 is a LinkML-defined data structure with required fields for cell type (CL identifier), perturbation (gene HGNC ID or compound ChEMBL ID), readout modality, QC flags, and data provenance.
3. **TA1 model interface definition**: the structured output of Pillar 1 (under-constrained parameters, hypothesized edges, inconsistent predictions) is a LinkML-defined data structure exposed to TA2 as a queryable object.

### 6.5 INDRA and GO-CAM: Causal Knowledge Assembly and Gap Tracking

**INDRA** (Gyori et al. Mol Syst Biol 2017) is a Python-based knowledge assembly and inference system that (a) extracts mechanism-typed statements (Activation, Inhibition, Phosphorylation, etc.) from literature (via machine reading: REACH, SPARSER, TRIPS), databases (BioGRID, Reactome, SIGNOR, OmniPath, Open Targets), and curated pathway databases; (b) deduplicates and scores them by belief; (c) assembles them into Bayesian networks, causal graphs (BEL, SIF, Cytoscape), or SBML models; and (d) continuously self-updates via the EMMAA (Ecosystem of Machine-Maintained Models with Automated Assembly) framework.

**CoGEx** (Gyori lab) provides model checking and reverse causal reasoning over INDRA-assembled models. **EMMAA** maintains live, continuously updating models for COVID-19, ALS, SARS-CoV-2, and cancer, with automated model checking against new literature.

**GO-CAM (Gene Ontology Causal Activity Models)** (Thomas et al. Nature Genetics 2019, PMC7012280) encodes mechanism-typed causal chains using the Relation Ontology causal hierarchy (directly positively regulates, directly negatively regulates, etc.), Gene Ontology molecular functions (MF terms as activities), and Biological Process terms as process contexts. GO-CAM models structurally mark gaps: an incomplete model explicitly lacks edges where mechanism is unknown, providing a formal gap representation.

**Our TA1 use of INDRA + GO-CAM:** INDRA is the **knowledge assembly engine** for Pillar 1: it reads the nine interaction databases and the 22q11DS/schizophrenia literature into mechanism-typed statements, assembles them into the Pillar 1 mechanistic network, scores belief, and exports to Biolink-formatted graph edges. GO-CAM curated models (from the GO consortium) provide the **ground truth causal layer** for the most-confident pathway edges. The EMMAA self-update cycle is the template for our TA1 Bayesian update loop (Phase II target: update latency <=24 hours; Phase III <=4 hours).

### 6.6 OBO Ontologies: The Semantic Backbone

The required OBO Foundry ontologies for our TA1 semantic backbone:

- **GO (Gene Ontology)**: biological process, molecular function, cellular component terms for all pathway and process-level assertions; GO-plus extension for cell-type-specific processes
- **CL (Cell Ontology)**: cell-type identifiers (e.g., CL:0000127 astrocyte, CL:0000746 cardiomyocyte; CL is the mandated cell-type vocabulary for CZ CELLxGENE)
- **UBERON**: anatomy/tissue identifiers (brain regions, organ systems)
- **MONDO**: disease identifiers (MONDO:0005090 schizophrenia, MONDO:0011511 22q11DS/DiGeorge)
- **HPO (Human Phenotype Ontology)**: phenotype terms for clinical data integration
- **Relation Ontology**: causal and temporal relations (causally upstream of, has participant, etc.)
- **MIRIAM/Bioregistry**: resolvable cross-references for all biological entities

These ontologies are pre-embedded with **TransBox box embeddings** (Xiong et al. arXiv:2410.14571) in Pillar 4, providing OOD generalization and gap-finding in ontology space.

## 7. Mapping onto Our TA1: Four Pillars, Three-Latent SCM, IGoR Phases

### 7.1 The Four-Pillar Architecture

Our TA1 consists of four pillars flowing in sequence, with Pillar 4 feeding back into Pillar 1 via new experiment data:

```
Pillar 1 (Network priors) --> Pillar 2 (Causal generative modeling)
--> Pillar 3 (Joint shift-space) --> Pillar 4 (Ontology-conditioned OOD + experiment design)
--> (TA4 experiments --> Pillar 1 update)
```

**Pillar 1: Cell-type-aware mechanistic network**

The MI ontology (term MI:0190) is the unifying vocabulary for nine harmonized interaction databases. Neuro-specific layers added: (a) cell-type-specific PPIs from Kasper Lage AP-MS in iPSC-derived neurons; (b) cell-type GRNs from PsychENCODE brainSCOPE multiome (snRNA + snATAC, 24 brain cell types; Emani et al. Science 2024, doi:10.1126/science.adi5199); (c) CS-CORE depth-corrected co-expression (Su et al. Nat Commun 2023, doi:10.1038/s41467-023-40503-7) as the decoder prior. INDRA assembles the causal knowledge; GO-CAM provides ground-truth causal assertions; Biolink provides the metagraph schema.

**Where the literature maps:** Pillar 1 is the structural causal graph G (the DAG over biological processes) that the sVAE/SAMS-VAE/Zhang et al. framework assumes. It provides the prior graph structure, the edge-type taxonomy, and the coverage map that drives TA2 experiment selection. AlphaGenome is used here to encode COMT/TBX1/DGCR8 regulatory effects into the network prior.

**Pillar 2: Causal generative modeling**

The mechanism-shift backbone is SAMS-VAE (Bereket and Karaletsos NeurIPS 2023). Disease and disease-associated genetic variation enter as sparse additive shifts: z_i = z^b_i + z^p_i, where z^p_i = sum_t d_{i,t}(e_t circle m_t). The Zhang et al. 2023 soft-intervention identifiability guarantees apply: disease-associated variants are soft interventions, recoverable up to CD-equivalence class. The SENA-discrepancy-VAE pathway-space encoder provides biological interpretability (each latent dimension = a linear combination of GO biological process activities). The SPEAR spectral gene-identity prior grounds the velocity network. The covariance-preserving decoder uses CS-CORE-informed vine-copula decoders from scDesign3 (Song et al. Nat Biotechnol 2024, doi:10.1038/s41587-023-01772-1). PDGrapher provides intervention design (edge mutilation on the Pillar 1 graph).

**Pillar 2b (factorized-PRS, proprietary, restricted sections 31 and 31b):** Patient genetic variation is factorized into sparse, pathway-disentangled, transdiagnostic axes. These axes serve simultaneously as (a) soft-intervention parameters driving the disease-as-perturbation model and (b) interpretable candidate biotypes spanning diagnostic boundaries. The method advances beyond PRSet by learning per-person genome axes as sparse mechanism identifications over TF-binding-footprint regions (CREB, SREBP), binding them to the SAMS-VAE latent intervention. The full genomic-to-disease-axis front-end architecture -- starting from pretrained genomic foundation models (AlphaGenome, VariantFormer, Evo 2), through cross-attention gene embeddings, gene functional network projection, multiresolution graph diffusion (WaveGC style, NBFNet for typed edges), and archetypal factorization into k disease-axis factors -- is detailed in Section 5.5 of this document and Research Master Section 31b. A deep structural causal model over the resulting axes (discrepancy-VAE style) learns the causal interactions among disease axes and supplies the disease-effect operator for the three-latent SCM. [No further proprietary detail beyond Sections 5.5 and 7.2; see restricted Research Master sections 31 and 31b.]

**Pillar 3: Joint cellular and clinical shift-space**

Every signal -- from a patient cohort or a dish -- is represented as a shift (delta) in pathway space relative to a dataset-specific control. Common representation: delta = z_perturbed - z_baseline, where z is the SAMS-VAE latent state. Cellular data (iPSC Perturb-seq, Cell Painting, live-cell imaging) and clinical data (PGC schizophrenia GWAS, SCHEMA rare-variant data, PsychENCODE multi-cohort atlas, Ruzicka Mohammadi et al. Science 2024) are aggregated in the same pathway-space coordinate. sVAE-ligr provides the mechanism transportability framework for bridging model-system mechanisms to clinical cohort data (Pillar 3's core cross-domain transfer problem).

**Pillar 4: Ontology-conditioned OOD generalization and experiment design**

Domain ontologies (MONDO, CL, UBERON, GO/Pathway Ontology) are pre-embedded with TransBox box embeddings. OOD generalization uses ontology-space interpolation. Gap finding: under-explored ontology subtrees become high-value experiment targets. Fixed hypothesis template: "Perturbing pathway/process X will shift the disease phenotype in disease Y toward healthy by modulating Z." Hub/key-node selection identifies transcription factors and signaling biomolecules predicted to modulate the largest number of downstream processes. Disease Map pattern (CellDesigner -> CaSQ -> SBML-qual -> MaBoSS) provides the verifiable mechanistic layer.

### 7.2 The Three-Latent Structural Causal Model

The three-latent SCM (Research Master Section 30.5) factorizes each cell's latent state into:

1. **Basal state (z^b, intrinsic):** the cell's healthy, intrinsic latent state (the SAMS-VAE basal embedding)
2. **Disease effect (z^d):** latent factors driven by disease-associated genetic variation; causal on cell state via the disease-as-perturbation operator; the Pillar 2b factorized-PRS provides the soft-intervention parameterization of z^d
3. **Treatment/intervention effect (z^t):** latent factors for an experimental or therapeutic intervention, entering the cell state via two distinct paths: (a) direct path (off-target/side-effect route) and (b) indirect path modulating the disease effect (therapeutic route)

The observed cell state is generated from: x ~ D(z^b + z^d + z^t_direct + f(z^d, z^t_indirect)), where D is the covariance-preserving NB decoder and f is a learned function encoding the interaction between disease and treatment.

This three-latent structure makes the clinically critical distinction identifiable: whether a candidate intervention improves the phenotype because it corrects the disease mechanism (z^t_indirect path) or independently pushes the cell state (z^t_direct path), separating efficacy from side-effect liability.

**Genomic grounding of z^d.** The disease-effect operator z^d is not a free latent; it is constrained by the genomic front-end (Section 5.5). The k disease-axis factors produced by archetypal factorization enter the deep SCM as **exogenous variables**: they drive the disease axes causally, with the deep structural causal model over axes learning which axes are downstream of which. This distinguishes our architecture from prior SAMS-VAE implementations where the disease perturbation label enters only as a conditioning index.

**Identifiability basis:** the three-latent SCM extends the Zhang et al. 2023 soft-intervention framework from one intervention type to disease and treatment as **separate, composable causal operators**, jointly constrained by TA4 Perturb-seq data and clinical cohort data.

## 8. Gaps and Best-Fit Components

### 8.1 Gap 1: Causal Identifiability under Polygenic Disease Signal

**The problem:** The Zhang et al. 2023 identifiability results assume at least one intervention per latent node. In the CRISPR setting, each gene knockout is a well-controlled intervention. In the polygenic disease setting, no individual is "knocked out" at a single biological process; the disease-associated genetic variation is distributed across thousands of loci, each with small effect. This means we cannot guarantee that the soft-intervention parameterization of disease genotype satisfies the "sufficient variability" assumption (Assumption 2 of Zhang et al.).

**Mitigation:** (a) use the GLEANR/Genomic SEM factorization to identify the ~5 latent axes that explain the majority of genetic variance; these are the "effective interventions" we model as soft interventions; (b) validate identifiability empirically by checking that the recovered disease axes are stable across held-out cohorts (cross-validation in the PGC/SCHEMA data); (c) the SCHEMA rare-variant layer provides near-hard interventions (protein-truncating variants in high-penetrance genes like SETD1A, GRIN2A) that can anchor the soft-intervention coordinate system.

**Best-fit component:** SCHEMA rare-variant data (Singh et al. Nat Genet 2022, doi:10.1038/s41586-022-04556-w) provides the anchor interventions; Genomic SEM provides the factorization of the polygenic background. Together these bracket the polygenic causal space.

### 8.2 Gap 2: Molecular-to-Circuit Scale Transition

**The problem:** Our Pillar 2 operates at the molecular-to-cell-type scale. The IGoR requirement for "multiscale" models implies at least one additional scale: circuit-level physiology (thalamocortical, fronto-temporal circuits in our disease context). The Virtual Brain Twin (virtualbraintwin.eu 2025) demonstrates data-assimilation at the circuit level, but does not have a molecular or cellular causal layer.

**Mitigation:** (a) SBML-qual/MaBoSS provides a Boolean attractor model of the cellular pathway layer, whose attractors can be mapped to circuit-scale input parameters (e.g., inhibitory/excitatory balance, myelination state) via a parameterized interface; (b) NeuroML/LEMS (PMC11723582) provides the circuit-level model formalism; (c) the Virtual Brain Twin's data-assimilation loop is the template for updating circuit parameters from new clinical imaging data; (d) the Pillar 3 joint shift-space, which reconciles cellular and clinical signal, provides the bridge variable.

**Best-fit component:** The Disease Map -> CaSQ -> SBML-qual -> MaBoSS -> attractor readout pipeline provides the molecular-to-cell-state layer; NeuroML + SBML-comp compose it with the circuit-level layer.

### 8.3 Gap 3: Temporal Disease-Progression Schema

**The problem:** No machine-readable schema integrates all four of {signed causal mechanism, disease-progression time axis, cell-type-resolved omics context, automated gap tracking with provenance}. SuStaIn and Event-Based Models provide clinical staging without mechanistic content; SBML-qual provides mechanism without clinical time; CellRank provides pseudotime without schema formalization.

**Our contribution:** A thin, FAIR, RO/MONDO/CL-grounded disease-progression schema linking clinical staging (SuStaIn/EBM outputs for schizophrenia prodrome and first-episode psychosis) to mechanistic state transitions (SBML-qual attractors and MaBoSS state-probability time courses) and to cell-state trajectories (CellRank 2 pseudotime). This schema is a defensible, ownable open-standards contribution (Research Master Section 35) and is a Phase I TA3 deliverable.

### 8.4 Gap 4: Covariance Structure in Counterfactual Synthesis

**The problem:** Prior SAMS-VAE and sVAE+ implementations use conditionally independent negative-binomial decoders (as in scVI), discarding gene-gene covariance structure. Generated counterfactual cells do not reflect real co-expression patterns and cannot support downstream analyses that depend on covariance (e.g., GRN inference, co-expression module analysis).

**Our solution (implemented):** Replace independent NB decoders with CS-CORE-informed, vine-copula decoders from scDesign3 (Song et al. Nat Biotechnol 2024). This preserves real gene-gene covariance in reconstructed and counterfactual cells, supporting all downstream analyses.

### 8.5 Gap 5: Non-Identifiability of the Flow-Matching Layer

**The problem:** CellFlow, CFGen, scDFM, and SPEAR are not causally identifiable in the Zhang et al. sense; they are high-fidelity generative models that learn distribution-to-distribution mappings without guaranteeing recovery of the underlying causal structure. Using them alone as the Pillar 2 core would not satisfy the IGoR requirement for mechanistic, not merely correlational, models.

**Our solution (the core novelty):** The flow-matching backbone (scDFM/SPEAR) provides the velocity network for the distribution-level transition operator. The SAMS-VAE + Zhang et al. DSCM layer provides the causal identifiability. The two are composed: the velocity field is regularized to be sparse in the SAMS-VAE mask structure (each velocity component is non-zero only for the biological processes that are shifted by the perturbation). This composition is the novel contribution of Pillar 2.

### 8.6 SPEAR Extensions: Specializing the Generative Core for Disease-Axis Conditioning (CONFIDENTIAL)

> [!CAUTION]
> **CONFIDENTIAL, UNDER ANONYMOUS REVIEW.** These are planned extensions to SPEAR that have not yet appeared in the submitted manuscript. They are internal only. Never name these mechanisms in external documents, partner materials, or the `shareable` build.

The four extensions below specialize the SPEAR generative backbone to integrate the disease-axis conditioning from Section 5.5. They address specific architectural gaps at the flow-matching/causal-conditioning interface.

**Extension 1: Row-sparse differential-attention residual.** The PAD-Transformer differential-attention mechanism (scDFM) computes the residual map softmax(Q1 K1^T) minus lambda times softmax(Q2 K2^T). In SPEAR, we additionally impose that this residual map be **row-sparse**: each gene's attention is allowed to concentrate on only a small number of other genes under perturbation. This row-sparsity encodes the **sparsity of the shift mechanism** directly in the attention geometry, consistent with the SMS hypothesis and the SAMS-VAE binary mask m_t. The row-sparse residual and the SAMS-VAE mask are jointly trained so the attention pattern reinforces the latent mechanism identification.

**Extension 2: Compositional conditioning across axes (generalized CFG for flow matching).** Standard classifier-free guidance (CFG) is defined for diffusion models. We use the **Guided Compositional Generation with Multiple Attributes** formulation, which generalizes CFG from diffusion to **conditional flow matching**: each disease axis or archetype contributes an additive guidance term to the velocity field, and multiple axes compose by summing their individual guidance fields. This allows the generative model to simultaneously condition on a combination of disease axes (for example, a patient with overlapping schizophrenia and myelination axes) without requiring separate models per axis combination. This is the flow-matching analog of the compositional guidance in CFGen (Section 2.3), extended from categorical cell-type attributes to continuous, graph-structured disease-axis factors.

**Extension 3: Delta-pathway featurization.** The differential transformer in the current SPEAR design operates on **delta-gene** representations (the difference in gene expression between control and perturbation hidden states). We reformulate this to operate on **delta pathways** -- the difference in GO biological process activity (the SENA-delta alpha vector; Section 1.7) between control and perturbation. Delta-pathway featurization provides a **uniform representation across modalities**: RNA-seq, ATAC-seq, and proteomics all project through the SENA pathway mask to the same biological-process activity space before the differential transformer sees them, removing modality-specific gene-index mismatches and improving cross-modality generalization.

**Extension 4: Multimodal fusion by barycenter.** Where multiple cellular modalities (transcriptomics, chromatin accessibility, proteomics, morphology) are jointly observed, each has a **modality-specific encoder** that maps it to the shared latent space. Rather than concatenating or averaging these encodings, we take their **barycenter** in the optimal-transport sense: the point that minimizes the sum of squared Wasserstein distances to each modality-specific embedding. This barycenter acts as the multimodal representation that is fit to the SPEAR/SAMS-VAE generative system. The barycenter weighting can be learned or set by data quality/coverage, and it degrades gracefully when one modality is missing (the barycenter collapses to the available modalities).

**Extension 5: Coupling to the three-latent SCM.** The disease-axis conditioners from the genomic front-end (Section 5.5) enter the SPEAR generative backbone via Extensions 2 and 3 above, and simultaneously enter the three-latent SCM as the disease-effect operator z^d (Section 7.2). The deep SCM over axes (Section 5.5, Step 5) supplies the causal structure over z^d, ensuring that the SPEAR velocity field is not just conditioned on the disease axes but on their causally ordered structure.
