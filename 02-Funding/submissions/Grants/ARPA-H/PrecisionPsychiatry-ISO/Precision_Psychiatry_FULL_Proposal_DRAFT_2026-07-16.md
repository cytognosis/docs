## If You Only Read One Thing

Psychiatry is the only major branch of medicine that selects treatment from a subjective symptom interview, with no objective biological test to confirm the diagnosis and no continuous readout to confirm whether treatment is working. We propose to build the first causal, genomics-grounded coordinate system for the human psyche, a multi-scale map of psychiatric biotypes spanning molecular, connectomic, and phenotypic evidence, paired with a causal digital psychiatric twin that simulates candidate treatments for a specific person before they are prescribed and adapts as that person's trajectory unfolds. Anchored first in schizophrenia, bipolar disorder, and major depressive disorder, and designed from the outset to generalize across diagnostic boundaries, the goal is measured plainly: more people restored to functioning lives, sooner, with fewer failed treatments along the way.

## Executive Summary

Mental illness is, by the numbers, the defining chronic-disease crisis of this decade. The Global Burden of Disease Study 2023, published in *The Lancet* in May 2026, estimates that **1.17 billion people worldwide** now live with a mental disorder, a 95.5 percent increase in prevalent cases since 1990, and that mental disorders account for **171 million disability-adjusted life-years (DALYs)**, ranking fifth among 22 cause groups for total disease burden and first for years lived with disability (GBD 2023 Mental Disorders Collaborators, 2026). In the United States, the April 18, 2026 Executive Order on Accelerating Medical Treatments for Serious Mental Illness (Executive Order 14401) states plainly that more than 14 million American adults live with serious mental illness, that suicide rates rose 37 percent from 2000 to 2018 before peaking again in 2022, and that despite substantial federal investment, "the medical research system has yet to produce approved therapies that promote enduring improvements for the most complex patients" (The White House, 2026).

Behind these numbers sits a structural failure that is not primarily a failure of drug discovery. It is a failure of measurement. Psychiatry remains the only major branch of medicine that selects treatment from a categorical label produced by a subjective interview, with no laboratory test to confirm the diagnosis, no biomarker to stratify treatment choice, and no continuous signal to confirm whether a chosen treatment is working before months have passed. We propose to close this measurement gap directly, not by inventing a new drug, but by building the coordinate system psychiatry has never had: a universal, genomics-grounded dimensional map of the human psyche, instantiated as **multi-scale biotypes** spanning molecular and cellular biology (micro), brain connectivity (meso), and clinical phenotype (macro), and a **causal digital psychiatric twin** that uses this map to simulate the effect of candidate treatments for a specific person before they are prescribed, and to adapt its recommendation as that person's own trajectory unfolds.

The scientific foundation for this map already exists in the published literature and is more mature than most funders realize. Grotzinger and colleagues resolved shared genetic risk across 14 psychiatric disorders into five genomic factors explaining approximately 66 percent of genetic variance (Grotzinger et al., *Nature*, 2025), with a distinct cellular foundation for each factor. The Research Domain Criteria (RDoC) framework and the Hierarchical Taxonomy of Psychopathology (HiTOP) independently converge on the same dimensional architecture from clinical phenomenology, and Michelini and colleagues formally crosswalked the two (Michelini et al., *Clinical Psychology Review*, 2021). Treatment science is converging on the same axes: ketamine, psilocybin, and related neuroplastogens act through shared binding to the BDNF receptor TrkB (Moliner et al., *Nature Neuroscience*, 2023; Casarotto et al., *Cell*, 2021), the exact mechanistic target of ARPA-H's EVIDENT program ($139.4 million, first 13 teams announced April 2026), and Stanford's accelerated, connectivity-targeted neuromodulation protocol (SNT, formerly SAINT) produced 79 percent remission in a randomized, sham-controlled trial of treatment-resistant depression (Cole et al., *American Journal of Psychiatry*, 2022). A 2026 personalized-lifestyle pilot trial nearly doubled standard depression remission rates using per-person machine learning (Nan et al., *NPP Digital Psychiatry and Neuroscience*, 2026). The pieces exist. What is missing is the integration: a single coordinate system that places a person on the map, and a decision engine that uses the map causally rather than descriptively.

Cytognosis proposes to build both, anchored first in schizophrenia, bipolar disorder, and major depressive disorder (the three disorders with the deepest joint coverage across genomic, connectomic, and phenotypic evidence layers), and designed from the outset to extend to post-traumatic stress disorder and anxiety, and eventually to the full transdiagnostic scope. The team combines a founder with lived experience of a genomically defined psychiatric risk syndrome (22q11.2 deletion / TBX1) and two decades of computational biology (MIT, the Broad Institute, insitro, GenBio AI) with the senior author of the genomic architecture the map is built on (Jordan Smoller), cellular and connectomic expertise (W. Brad Ruzicka at McLean, Anne Carpenter for morphology), computational infrastructure (Ananth Grama, Purdue Institute for Physical Artificial Intelligence), and a sensing partnership building the objective measurement layer this program needs in the field (Hervé Marie-Nelly, spectroscopy-based Brainscope). The deliverable is not a new pill. It is the map, the twin, and the products, Yar, Neuroverse, and Brainscope, that put dimensional, causal psychiatry into a clinician's hands and a patient's daily life. Success is measured the way ARPA-H measures success: in lives and life-years restored, and in more therapies that work the first time a person tries them.

## 1. Significance: The Problem and the Gap

### 1.1 The general healthcare failure: symptoms, not causes; averages, not people; late, not early

Reactive medicine, across every chronic disease, shares three interlocking failure modes that Cytognosis treats as a single structural problem rather than three separate ones.

**Treating symptoms, not causes.** Broad clinical classifications systematically obscure the specific molecular and cellular drivers of disease, forcing clinicians to manage conditions by population-average statistical decision rules rather than each patient's own biology. Phenotypically identical presentations frequently arise from mechanistically distinct causes: asthma resolves into at least two endotypes (T2-high and T2-low) requiring different interventions (Woodruff et al., *Am J Respir Crit Care Med*, 2009), rheumatoid arthritis into three molecular pathotypes with divergent treatment response (Lewis et al., *Cell Reports*, 2019), and depression, as discussed below, into multiple biologically distinct subtypes visible only through neuroimaging. Without visibility into individual mechanism, treatment defaults to sequential, population-guided trial and error: first-line antidepressant response sits at 42 to 53 percent (Cipriani et al., *Lancet*, 2018), and the landmark STAR*D trial showed relapse rates climbing from 40 to 71 percent across four sequential treatment steps (Rush et al., *American Journal of Psychiatry*, 2006), a pattern that recurs across chronic disease generally.

**Averaging across biologically heterogeneous populations.** Standard clinical trial design and standard-of-care guidelines treat large patient populations as statistically homogeneous, averaging out the molecular signal that would otherwise identify who actually benefits from a given treatment. Where stratified, biomarker-guided treatment has been deployed in oncology, it works decisively (trastuzumab in HER2-positive breast cancer, imatinib in BCR-ABL-positive leukemia), yet biomarker-driven trials remain the exception, not the rule, across most of medicine, including psychiatry, where pharmacogenomic testing penetrates fewer than 5 percent of new antidepressant prescriptions despite FDA-cleared metabolism panels.

**Intervening after the window closes.** Disease processes exhibit intervention windows during which progression remains reversible and treatment costs a fraction of late-stage care; once pathology crosses a tipping point, that window closes, often permanently. Molecular and cellular dysregulation precede clinical symptoms by years: Alzheimer's biomarkers appear 15 to 30 years before cognitive symptoms (Jack et al., *Lancet Neurology*, 2013), and, specific to mental illness, a landmark PNAS study demonstrated that elevated temporal autocorrelation, variance, and emotional correlation in passively collected mood data predict transitions between depressed and non-depressed states days to weeks before they clinically manifest (van de Leemput et al., *PNAS*, 2014). Current psychiatric care has no equivalent of continuous glucose monitoring or continuous ECG; it waits for a crisis, then reacts to it.

These three failures compound rather than operate independently: without mechanistic understanding, clinicians cannot identify the molecular targets that would enable earlier intervention; without stratification, average-outcome treatment guidelines cannot serve an individual; without continuous monitoring, no one can detect the trajectory before it becomes a crisis. Mental illness is where all three failures are most severe and most consequential, and it is the sharpest test case for a platform built to close them.

### 1.2 The neuropsychiatric-specific gap

Every failure mode above is present in psychiatry in its most acute form, plus a set of failures specific to the field.

**No objective, clinical psychiatric diagnostic.** There is no blood test for depression, no scan that distinguishes bipolar disorder from unipolar depression in routine clinical use, and no objective marker for schizophrenia, PTSD, or addiction. Diagnosis rests on a subjective symptom interview scored against DSM-5-TR criteria (American Psychiatric Association, 2013), and the reliability of that interview is measurably poor: the DSM-5 field trials found that 39 percent of tested diagnoses fell into the "questionable" or "unacceptable" inter-rater reliability range (kappa below 0.4), including major depressive disorder and generalized anxiety disorder (Regier et al., *American Journal of Psychiatry*, 2013). The same patient, in other words, can receive different diagnoses from different clinicians applying the same manual. ARPA-H's own EVIDENT program was created to address exactly this absence of objective, biomarker-based measurement for behavioral health therapeutics (ARPA-H, 2026).

**Masked within-diagnosis phenotypic heterogeneity.** Patients who share a single DSM label are frequently biologically distinct from one another, and the specific combination of symptoms a given patient presents with is often not even recorded in a form that supports later analysis. Depression alone resolves into at least four (Drysdale et al., *Nature Medicine*, 2017) and, in the more recent and better-replicated Williams six-axis scaffold (Williams et al., 2024), six neurocognitive-circuit biotypes with distinct treatment response profiles; the B-SNIP consortium defines two, not one, biosignatures across the schizophrenia-bipolar spectrum (Clementz and Tamminga, *American Journal of Psychiatry*, 2016). DSM and ICD provide no tool to represent this heterogeneity, so two patients who receive the identical diagnostic label may in fact require, and respond to, entirely different treatments, while the label itself gives no clinician a way to tell which is which.

**Complex biological-plus-exposome interplay.** Psychiatric risk is neither purely genetic nor purely environmental; it is an interaction the field calls gene-by-environment (GxE), and its clearest demonstration is twin discordance. Monozygotic twins, who share essentially all of their genome, show only 40 to 50 percent concordance for schizophrenia, meaning roughly half of genetically identical co-twins of an affected individual never develop the disorder themselves (twin registry literature reviewed in Hilker et al., 2018, and classical twin studies broadly). This gap is the signature of environmental and exposome contribution acting on a shared genetic foundation, and disentangling the two, using genetics as a fixed reference against which environmentally driven connectomic and phenotypic variation can be measured, is a foundational design principle of our map rather than an afterthought.

**The dimensional, quantitative nature of phenotypes is ignored.** Symptoms and their underlying processes are continuous, not binary, yet DSM-style categorical thresholds force a continuous reality into an artificial cut point, producing low diagnostic reliability, diagnostic instability near the threshold, and clinically important subthreshold presentations that current nosology simply does not register (Markon et al., 2011; Shankman et al., 2009). Two complementary frameworks, RDoC (Insel et al., 2010; Cuthbert and Insel, *BMC Medicine*, 2013) and HiTOP (Kotov et al., *Journal of Abnormal Psychology*, 2017), were built specifically to replace categorical diagnosis with dimensional structure, one from neurobiological first principles, one from the empirical covariance structure of symptoms themselves, and Michelini and colleagues formally crosswalked the two frameworks by direction of association (Michelini et al., *Clinical Psychology Review*, 2021), a crosswalk that our own harmonized clinical-instrument mapping extends and independently re-verifies (0 of 88 base associations contradicted on re-verification; 29 directly corroborated).

**Cross-disorder transdiagnostic overlap, comorbidity, and the missing diagnosis.** Psychiatric comorbidity is not statistical noise; it is evidence that different DSM labels frequently share causal, endotypic drivers. The most direct demonstration is genomic: Grotzinger and colleagues applied genomic structural equation modeling to genome-wide association data from more than one million cases across 14 psychiatric disorders and resolved shared genetic risk into five latent factors explaining roughly 66 percent of genetic variance, with over 99 percent of case-case genome-wide significant variants distinguishing disorders that load on *different* factors rather than the same one (Grotzinger et al., *Nature*, 2025). Disorders sharing a factor are, at the level of individual genetic variants, largely indistinguishable from one another; the DSM label that separates them captures a real clinical distinction but not, in large part, a distinct causal one. This is precisely why comorbidity is so pervasive and why a given patient's true combination of symptoms, cutting across two or more DSM categories at once, is so often the actual clinical picture and yet has no formal diagnostic slot: a genuinely dimensional, multi-axis position, not a missing label to be added to the manual, but a coordinate the manual was never built to represent.

**Treatment failure and treatment resistance from trial-and-error symptom treatment.** The downstream consequence of the four gaps above is that treatment selection in psychiatry is, structurally, a sequence of population-level guesses rather than a mechanism-informed choice. STAR*D remains the definitive demonstration: cumulative remission climbed from roughly 33 percent after the first treatment step to 67 percent after four steps, but relapse rates rose in parallel from 40 to 71 percent, with each failed step costing 6 to 12 weeks of continued suffering and new medication-specific side effects before the next attempt (Rush et al., 2006). Patients who fail enough sequential steps are eventually labeled "treatment-resistant," a term that describes the failure of the selection process, not necessarily the biology of the patient.

### 1.3 The scale of the burden

The GBD 2023 analysis (published *The Lancet*, May 2026) is the most current global accounting and the estimate to cite going forward: **1.17 billion people** (95% uncertainty interval 1.06 to 1.31 billion) lived with a mental disorder in 2023, an age-standardized prevalence of 14,210.7 per 100,000, a 95.5 percent increase in prevalent cases and a 24.2 percent rise in age-standardized prevalence since 1990 (GBD 2023 Mental Disorders Collaborators, 2026). Mental disorders caused **171 million DALYs** in 2023 (age-standardized rate 2,070.5 per 100,000), 6.1 percent of all-cause DALYs, ranking fifth among 22 GBD cause groups (up from twelfth in 1990) and first for years lived with disability. Anxiety disorders alone reached 470 million prevalent cases (a 158.6 percent increase since 1990) and their DALY rate rose 47.1 percent since 2019, the largest pandemic-era jump of any mental disorder; major depressive disorder reached 236 million prevalent cases; schizophrenia 26.9 million; bipolar disorder 35.7 million. Burden peaks in the 15-to-19 age group and is 27.6 percent higher, age-standardized, in females than males.

In the United States, Executive Order 14401 (April 18, 2026) cites more than 14 million adults with serious mental illness, roughly 8 million on prescription medication for these conditions, a 37 percent rise in suicide rates from 2000 to 2018 followed by a rebound to a new peak in 2022, and a veteran suicide rate more than double the non-veteran adult rate, at over 6,000 veteran suicides annually for more than two decades (The White House, 2026). Racial and ethnic minorities face documented, measurable diagnostic inequity layered on top of this burden: Black Americans are diagnosed with schizophrenia at rates up to four times higher than white Americans presenting with comparable symptoms, a disparity attributed substantially to clinician bias interacting with the subjectivity of the diagnostic interview itself (Schwartz and Blankenship, 2014; Olbert et al., *Journal of Abnormal Psychology*, 2018), a second, independent line of evidence for why an objective diagnostic matters, beyond inter-rater unreliability alone.

### 1.4 Federal alignment: why this program, why now

The federal posture toward mental illness shifted decisively in 2026. Executive Order 14401 frames the core problem as innovation failure, not resource scarcity, prioritizes breakthrough-therapy designation and expanded access for novel treatment modalities, and directs ARPA-H to allocate at least $50 million toward state-collaborative advancement of innovative mental health treatments, with an explicit call for real-world evidence generation in partnership with the Department of Veterans Affairs and the private sector (The White House, 2026). ARPA-H's EVIDENT program (Evidence-Based Validation and Innovation for Rapid Therapeutics in Behavioral Health, $139.4 million, first 13 teams announced April 21, 2026) operationalizes this directive by funding objective, biomarker-based responder identification for neuroplastogens, closed-loop neuromodulation, and digital therapeutics (ARPA-H, 2026; HHS, 2026). Our program is the direct complement to EVIDENT rather than a competitor to it: EVIDENT validates individual rapid-acting interventions against objective endpoints; we build the underlying coordinate system, the dimensional map and the causal twin, on which any such objective endpoint, present or future, is interpreted at the level of an individual person. The Proactive Health Office's own stated interest areas, methods for continuous and widespread sensing of health state and early disease indicators deployable at population scale, and novel, robust predictive surrogates for long-term health outcomes (ARPA-H-SOL-24-106, Amendment 03, Section 2.1), describe precisely the map-plus-twin architecture this proposal builds, instantiated first in the disease area where the measurement gap is largest and the federal mandate is most explicit.

## 2. Innovation

### 2.1 A universal, genomics-grounded dimensional map

The central innovation of this proposal is a **universal dimensional map of the human psyche**: a continuous coordinate system, grounded in genomic architecture, on which any patient can be located, replacing the discrete DSM label with a measurable position. We build this map on the largest and most rigorously derived transdiagnostic genomic structure published to date. Grotzinger and colleagues applied genomic structural equation modeling to genome-wide association data spanning more than one million cases across 14 psychiatric disorders and resolved shared genetic risk into **five latent genomic factors** explaining roughly 66 percent of genetic variance across 238 pleiotropic loci (Grotzinger et al., *Nature*, 2025). Each factor carries a distinct cell-type signature: the Schizophrenia-Bipolar (SB) factor is enriched in excitatory neurons, consistent with single-nucleus RNA-sequencing evidence that excitatory neurons are the most transcriptionally altered cell class in schizophrenia (Ruzicka et al., 2024); the Internalizing factor (major depressive disorder, PTSD, anxiety) is enriched in oligodendrocytes and myelin biology, consistent with decades of postmortem glial findings in depression; a Neurodevelopmental factor spans autism, ADHD, and Tourette syndrome; and two additional factors (Compulsive/eating and Substance-use) extend the map to further disorders as scope allows. This is the structural anchor of our map because it connects a genome-wide statistical factor to a concrete cellular foundation that can, in principle, be measured or targeted, rather than to an abstract statistical artifact.

We align this genomic factor structure to the two leading clinically derived dimensional frameworks, ensuring the map speaks the language clinicians and reviewers already use rather than inventing a fourth, incompatible taxonomy. **DSM-5-TR cross-cutting symptom measures** (American Psychiatric Association, 2013) supply the clinically familiar entry point; **RDoC** (Insel et al., 2010) supplies the neurobiological units-of-analysis structure from genes to circuits to self-report across six domains; **HiTOP** (Kotov et al., 2017) supplies the empirically derived symptom-covariance hierarchy culminating in six spectra and a general psychopathology factor. Michelini and colleagues formally crosswalked RDoC to HiTOP (Michelini et al., *Clinical Psychology Review*, 2021); we extend and independently re-verify that crosswalk (0 of 88 base associations contradicted, 29 directly corroborated, 43 consistent-indirect on independent re-verification) and join it to a harmonized clinical-instrument mapping of 111 instruments and 2,399 items, each carrying an RDoC construct and a HiTOP dimension alongside standard biomedical ontology codes (HPO, SNOMED CT, LOINC, ICD-10-CM). The result is a three-way crosswalk, genomic factor to RDoC/HiTOP construct to DSM cross-cutting measure, so that a single patient's position on the map can be read simultaneously in the language of genetics, of neurobiological systems, and of the symptom checklist a clinician already administers.

We anchor the map's core scope in three disorders with the deepest joint coverage across all three evidence layers described below: **major depressive disorder, bipolar disorder, and schizophrenia**. Extension to PTSD and anxiety disorders (both loading on the Internalizing factor alongside MDD) is a scope filter, not new synthesis, because both are already fully represented in our underlying transdiagnostic canon; further extension to OCD, autism, ADHD, Tourette syndrome, eating disorders, and substance use disorders follows the same pattern as resources allow.

### 2.2 Multi-scale biotypes: micro, meso, macro

A genomic factor alone is not a clinically actionable coordinate; it must be connected to measurable biology at every scale between genotype and bedside. We characterize each anchor disorder, and ultimately each individual patient, across three evidence layers, combined into a single per-person coordinate rather than treated as three separate, disconnected findings.

**Micro (molecular and cellular).** The BDNF/TrkB neuroplasticity axis is the keystone molecular finding, discovered by our own team in the McLean cohort (published in the PsychENCODE schizophrenia capstone) and independently cross-validated out-of-distribution in the Mount Sinai cohort. Mature BDNF binds TrkB and drives a convergent neuroplasticity program through CREB; the ratio of mature BDNF to its precursor, proBDNF, sets the balance between synaptic strengthening and pruning, and this ratio is measurably state-dependent in both major depressive disorder and bipolar disorder. TrkB itself is cholesterol-sensitive, and classical antidepressants (fluoxetine, ketamine) act as allosteric "BDNF sensitizers" at this same site (Casarotto et al., *Cell*, 2021). Beyond the BDNF/TrkB axis, parvalbumin-interneuron and excitation/inhibition imbalance is the single most reproducible molecular finding in schizophrenia across all 14 disorders in our synthesis, and oxidative-stress and inflammatory subgroups stratify meaningful subsets of bipolar disorder and major depressive disorder respectively.

**Meso (connectomic nodes, edges, and subgraphs).** We privilege edges, the relationship between two brain regions, over single-region activity, because edges are what map onto interventions. The load-bearing example is the dorsolateral prefrontal cortex to subgenual anterior cingulate cortex (dlPFC-sgACC) anticorrelation in depression: stronger baseline anticorrelation predicts better response to targeted, accelerated neuromodulation (Cole et al., 2022). Schizophrenia shows a reciprocal, disorder-specific thalamocortical pattern (hyperconnectivity to sensorimotor cortex, hypoconnectivity to prefrontal cortex), and the B-SNIP consortium's two-biotype connectomic-plus-EEG solution (cognitive-deficit versus sensory-hyperresponsive) remains the best-replicated stratification in psychosis (Clementz and Tamminga, 2016). The default-mode network's internal core is hyperconnected in depression and schizophrenia (rumination, self-referential processing) and hypoconnected in PTSD and autism (reduced within-network integration), giving the meso layer a genuinely transdiagnostic edge and subgraph vocabulary rather than a disorder-specific one.

**Macro (phenotypic and cross-trial).** Standard clinical instruments, PHQ-9 for depression, YMRS for mania, PANSS for psychosis, GAD-7 for anxiety, function as the macro-level readout that closes the loop back to the RDoC/HiTOP crosswalk: because every item in `_MASTER_ontology_mapping.csv` is individually mapped to an RDoC construct and a HiTOP dimension, a change in an outcome instrument's score can be interpreted as movement along a specific dimensional axis rather than only as a change in an aggregate symptom count. This is what allows cross-trial outcome data, collected under entirely different DSM labels and entirely different instruments, to be pooled onto the same underlying coordinate system.

We state plainly, in keeping with the rigor this science demands, where the map's current limits sit: the exact per-disorder genomic factor loadings from Grotzinger et al.'s supplementary tables have not yet been extracted into continuous form, so the map's initial figure positions disorders using qualitative confidence tiers rather than invented decimals, a limitation to close in Aim 1 rather than a claim to overstate now; and several molecular findings (BDNF in MDD and BD, glutamate in schizophrenia) are state-dependent rather than trait-stable, a caveat the map preserves explicitly rather than silently resolving.

### 2.3 The causal digital psychiatric twin

A static map, however well constructed, only describes where a person sits. The second innovation is a **causal digital psychiatric twin**: a dynamic AI world-model of an individual's cognitive, emotional, and behavioral state that does not merely classify a person's current position on the map, but simulates, in silico, the causal effect of candidate treatments before they are administered, ranks the treatment most likely to work for that specific person, and adapts its recommendation continuously as the person's own trajectory unfolds. The twin is built using causal reinforcement learning over future outcomes rather than supervised pattern-matching against population averages, so that its output is a counterfactual prediction ("if this person receives treatment A rather than treatment B, their trajectory on these axes is expected to diverge in this way") rather than a correlational risk score. Conservative, off-policy evaluation methods are used throughout so the twin's recommendations can be validated against retrospective data before any prospective deployment, and the system is designed from the outset as clinical decision support that keeps a clinician in the loop, not as an autonomous prescribing agent.

### 2.4 Mechanism-grounded treatment mapping across scales

The map and the twin are only as useful as the treatment vocabulary they operate over, so we map treatments to the same three evidence layers rather than to legacy indication-based drug classes alone.

**Micro.** The Neuroscience-based Nomenclature (NbN) system reclassifies psychotropic medications by pharmacological mechanism (target system, mode of action) rather than legacy indication. SSRIs and SNRIs map to the serotonergic axis; ketamine and esketamine map to the glutamatergic and BDNF/TrkB axis; second-generation antipsychotics (dopamine D2 plus serotonin 5-HT2 antagonism) map to the hyperdopaminergic axis relevant to schizophrenia and bipolar mania. Neuroplastogens, ketamine, psilocybin, and related TrkB-binding compounds, are mechanistically distinguished from classical antidepressants by binding affinity: psilocin and LSD bind TrkB at roughly 1,000-fold higher affinity than fluoxetine, and their plasticity effects are TrkB-dependent but independent of the serotonin 2A receptor responsible for their hallucinogenic properties (Moliner et al., *Nature Neuroscience*, 2023). This is the direct mechanistic bridge to ARPA-H's EVIDENT program, which funds objective, biomarker-based responder identification for exactly these neuroplastogen and neuromodulation mechanisms (ARPA-H, 2026).

**Meso.** The dlPFC-sgACC anticorrelation targeted by Stanford's accelerated neuromodulation protocol generalizes across disorders as a template: the orbitofrontal-caudate cortico-striato-thalamo-cortical loop in OCD and Tourette syndrome, the nucleus accumbens-orbitofrontal reward circuit across substance use disorders, and the default-mode network's internal core across depression, schizophrenia, PTSD, and autism.

**Macro.** PHQ-9, GAD-7, PANSS, and YMRS function as cross-trial outcome readouts, individually mapped back to RDoC constructs and HiTOP dimensions, so that treatment effects observed in one trial population can be compared, on the same coordinate system, to treatment effects observed under a different DSM label entirely.

**Therapeutic-hypothesis grouping.** We organize this treatment vocabulary by the legacy indication class a clinician already recognizes, antidepressants, anticonvulsants and mood stabilizers, and antipsychotics, each cross-referenced to its NbN pharmacology class and mode of action, so the map and the twin can present both what a clinician calls a treatment and what it mechanistically does, closing the gap between familiar clinical language and the dimensional biology underneath it.

### 2.5 The evidence that personalization already works

The reason this program is proposed now, and not a decade ago, is that personalized psychiatric treatment has already been demonstrated to work at small scale; what is missing is the integrated map and twin needed to deliver it broadly. Two recent, independently reported results anchor this claim.

**A personalized lifestyle intervention nearly doubled standard depression remission.** Nan and colleagues enrolled 50 individuals with mild-to-moderate depression in a single-arm, open-label pilot trial (PerMA, NCT05662254), using two weeks of smartphone ecological momentary assessment and smartwatch tracking to build per-person machine-learning models identifying each individual's own top mood-predictive lifestyle factor, then implementing an individualized mood augmentation plan (iMAP) over six weeks with weekly coach guidance (Nan et al., *NPP Digital Psychiatry and Neuroscience*, 2026). Fifty-five percent of participants no longer met criteria for depression by PHQ-9 at six weeks, against a standard remission rate of roughly 30 percent for existing interventions; anxiety symptoms fell 36 percent and clinician-rated depression (HDRS) improved with a large effect size (Cohen's d = -1.03). We report this honestly as a small, single-arm pilot rather than a randomized trial, which is precisely why it is evidence of feasibility, not yet of population-scale effect; it demonstrates that per-person, data-driven personalization measurably outperforms one-size-fits-all coaching even using peripheral, non-brain signals alone, the exact starting point our platform is built to extend.

**Accelerated, connectivity-targeted neuromodulation produced high remission in a randomized, sham-controlled trial.** Stanford's neuromodulation therapy (SNT, formerly SAINT), an accelerated intermittent theta-burst protocol individually targeted to each participant's own dlPFC region most anticorrelated with sgACC by functional connectivity MRI, produced remission in 11 of 14 participants (79 percent) in the active arm at some point during a four-week follow-up, against 2 of 15 (13 percent) in the sham arm, in a randomized, double-blind, sham-controlled trial of treatment-resistant depression (Cole et al., *American Journal of Psychiatry*, 2022). This result matters to our program for two reasons beyond its effect size: first, it validates that a single, individually targeted meso-scale edge (dlPFC-sgACC) is causally, not merely correlationally, linked to treatment response, exactly the kind of edge-level target our meso evidence layer is built to represent; second, it demonstrates that when treatment is targeted using an individual's own connectivity rather than a population-average stimulation site, response rates rise from the roughly 17 percent typical of standard, non-personalized rTMS in comparably treatment-resistant patients to something several-fold higher.

Together, these two results bracket the opportunity from opposite ends of the evidence stack: a peripheral, behavioral personalization signal moves remission from 30 to 55 percent; a brain-connectivity-targeted personalization signal moves remission from roughly 17 to 79 percent in a matched treatment-resistant population. Our platform is designed to combine both kinds of personalization signal, peripheral and central, molecular and connectomic, inside a single causal twin, rather than leaving each as an isolated demonstration.

## 3. Approach: Specific Aims

### Aim 1: Build the universal dimensional map

Extract continuous, per-disorder genomic factor loadings from the Grotzinger et al. (2025) supplementary data, replacing the current qualitative confidence tiers with fitted values. Complete the three-way crosswalk (genomic factor to RDoC/HiTOP construct to DSM-5-TR cross-cutting measure) for the core scope (MDD, bipolar disorder, schizophrenia) and the extension scope (PTSD, anxiety), drawing on our existing 111-instrument, 2,399-item harmonized ontology mapping. Render the map as a continuous two-axis figure for the core scope (SB factor loading by Internalizing factor loading) and a five-spoke radar figure for the full transdiagnostic scope, per the existing figure specification, with an explicit legend distinguishing evidence confidence, core versus extension scope, and treatment-layer overlays. Deliverable: a peer-reviewable figure and an accompanying methods paper suitable for a review-journal submission, kept strictly separate from the confidential factorized polygenic score that remains platform intellectual property.

### Aim 2: Instantiate multi-scale biotypes

For each anchor disorder, formalize the micro (molecular and cellular), meso (connectomic node, edge, and subgraph), and macro (phenotypic and cross-trial instrument) evidence layers into a single, machine-readable per-person coordinate schema, built on our existing transdiagnostic micro, meso, and macro synthesis documents and the machine-readable connectomic node and edge data already specified for the meso layer. Validate biotype-assignment concordance against held-out, multimodal cohort data (target: Jaccard concordance at or above 0.85, rising to 0.90 as data volume increases), explicitly tracking the replication caveats already known in this literature (the Drysdale four-biotype MDD solution's published replication failure, the state-dependence of several molecular markers) rather than silently resolving them.

### Aim 3: Build and validate the causal digital psychiatric twin

Construct the causal reinforcement learning architecture that takes a person's map coordinate and trajectory history as input and outputs a ranked, counterfactual estimate of treatment response across candidate interventions. Validate initially against retrospective, held-out treatment-response data (STAR*D-style sequential trial data, SNT and iMAP trial data where accessible under appropriate data use agreements) using conservative off-policy evaluation, then prospectively in a decision-support, clinician-in-the-loop configuration. Success criterion: treatment-response prediction that outperforms standard-of-care sequential selection by a pre-registered margin, and prospective trajectory prediction that anticipates relapse ahead of standard three-to-twelve-week visit-interval detection.

### Aim 4: Mechanism-grounded treatment mapping and platform integration

Complete the micro/meso/macro treatment mapping across the full NbN pharmacology table (222 drugs, extendable), the connectomic neuromodulation-response literature, and the cross-trial instrument outcome set, organized by therapeutic-hypothesis grouping (antidepressants, anticonvulsants and mood stabilizers, antipsychotics) cross-referenced to mechanism. Integrate the map and twin into the Cytognosis platform and its products (Section 4), with an explicit, staged transition path: the dimensional map is released as an open scientific resource; clinical decision support and continuous monitoring move toward deployment through a public-benefit delivery vehicle, keeping platform-confidential intellectual property (the factorized polygenic score) permanently separate from anything published or transferred.

Across all four aims, human subjects protections are already in place through our existing institutional review board relationship (North Star IRB, reliance agreement executed; Federalwide Assurance filed), and all patient-facing sensing and inference is designed for on-device, privacy-preserving operation by default, consistent with our broader platform's privacy-by-design commitment.

## 4. Solution Stack

The map and the twin are computational and scientific deliverables; the products below are how they reach clinicians and patients.

- **Cytognosis platform.** The general-purpose, multimodal, causal, knowledge-graph-grounded engine underlying every product below: the modality-agnostic layer that stores the dimensional map, runs the digital twin's causal inference, and harmonizes data across modalities and ontologies.
- **Yar.** An on-device causal AI and neurodivergent-friendly companion application that captures a person's real-world cognitive, emotional, and behavioral trajectory continuously, in daily life rather than during an occasional clinic visit, feeding the digital twin the longitudinal signal it needs to detect deviation from an individual's own baseline and to adapt its treatment ranking over time. On-device processing keeps psychological data under the person's control by design.
- **Neuroverse.** The neuro foundation-model layer that learns shared structure across neuroimaging, genomic, and behavioral modalities, providing the representational backbone the dimensional map and the twin draw on for cross-modal inference.
- **Brainscope.** A spectroscopy-based sensing partnership with Hervé Marie-Nelly, targeting objective, continuous, non-invasive measurement at greater depth and cellular specificity than current wearable or fNIRS approaches, closing the gap between the map's molecular and connectomic layers and what can actually be measured outside a research MRI suite.

## 6. Risks and Mitigations

- **Biotype validity and replication.** The strongest cautionary tale in this literature is the Drysdale et al. (2017) four-biotype depression solution, which failed independent external replication (Dinga et al., 2019). We mitigate by multi-cohort validation from the outset (published McLean discovery plus out-of-distribution Mount Sinai cross-validation for the molecular layer), by pre-registering held-out concordance thresholds before claiming a biotype is validated, and by preferring the better-replicated Williams six-axis scaffold over the original four-biotype solution wherever the two conflict.
- **Causal identifiability.** Inferring causal treatment effects from largely observational psychiatric data is genuinely hard. We mitigate by triangulating genetics (a naturally randomized instrument), cellular perturbation data, and longitudinal trajectory data rather than relying on any single data type, and by using conservative off-policy evaluation for the digital twin, so that the system's recommendations are validated against retrospective outcomes before any prospective clinical use.
- **Data access and human subjects protection.** Psychiatric data, especially longitudinal and genomic data, carries elevated privacy and consent obligations. We mitigate through an existing institutional review board reliance relationship (North Star IRB) with a Federalwide Assurance already filed, and through privacy-by-design, on-device processing in Yar so that raw psychological data does not need to leave a person's device to contribute to their own trajectory model.
- **Clinical translation and over-reliance risk.** A causal decision-support system that is wrong, or that a clinician follows uncritically, carries real harm potential. We mitigate by framing the digital twin explicitly as decision support, not autonomous prescribing, keeping a clinician in the loop at every recommendation, and by validating conservatively against retrospective data before any prospective deployment.
- **State-dependence and overclaiming.** Several of the molecular findings this map depends on (BDNF in mood disorders, glutamate in schizophrenia) are state-dependent rather than trait-stable, which complicates their use as fixed biomarkers. We mitigate by stating this limitation explicitly in every internal and external document that discusses the map, rather than presenting a static biotype where the underlying finding is dynamic.

## 7. Schedule, Milestones, and Quantitative Exams

- **Early go/no-go.** Extraction of continuous genomic factor loadings (Aim 1) and completion of the three-way crosswalk for the three core disorders; biotype-schema formalization (Aim 2) for the micro, meso, and macro layers with an initial concordance readout against available held-out data.
- **Mid-term go/no-go.** Biotype-assignment concordance on held-out multimodal data reaching a pre-registered Jaccard threshold of at least 0.85, rising toward 0.90 as additional cohort data becomes available; initial causal twin treatment-response prediction beating standard-of-care sequential selection by a pre-set margin on retrospective data.
- **Final exam.** Prospective trajectory prediction that anticipates relapse ahead of the standard three-to-twelve-week visit-interval detection window; digital-twin in-silico treatment ranking validated against retrospective response data across the three core disorders, with the extension disorders (PTSD, anxiety) validated as a scope-filter exercise rather than new model-building.
- **Sequencing.** The founder's own genomically defined risk syndrome (22q11.2 deletion / TBX1) provides an internally motivating and scientifically tractable entry point for schizophrenia-spectrum work; the program then extends to bipolar disorder and major depressive disorder, and finally to the PTSD and anxiety extension scope, in that order.

## 9. Impact

We measure success the way the underlying human cost is actually felt: in lives and life-years restored, not in dollars spent or saved. Mental disorders account for 171 million disability-adjusted life-years annually and are now the leading cause of years lived with disability worldwide (GBD 2023 Mental Disorders Collaborators, 2026); every improvement in diagnostic speed, treatment selection, and relapse prevention translates directly into years of functioning life returned to people who would otherwise lose them to a disease that current care detects too late, treats by guesswork, and monitors too rarely. If personalized treatment selection at the scale already demonstrated in small pilots (a near-doubling of remission from lifestyle personalization, a several-fold increase in remission from connectivity-targeted neuromodulation) were reliably deliverable at population scale through an integrated map and twin, the plain-language translation is millions of additional people moving from active illness to remission each year, each failed treatment step avoided sparing that person six to twelve additional weeks of continued suffering and unnecessary side effects. Beneficiaries include the people living with serious mental illness whose treatment is chosen correctly the first time rather than the third or fourth; the clinicians who gain an objective coordinate where they previously had only a subjective interview; and drug developers who gain biotype-stratified trial populations, addressing the same trial-and-error inefficiency that drives psychiatric drug development's persistently poor success rates. For the mission, the deliverable this program transitions is objective psychiatric measurement paired with personalized, mechanism-grounded intervention, released as an open scientific resource (the dimensional map) alongside a public-benefit path for the clinical decision-support layer, so this program's contribution to relapse prevention, diagnostic delay compression, and treatment-resistance reduction, the concrete drivers of restored life-years, outlasts any single funding cycle.
