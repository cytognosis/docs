## Q3. Risk Assessment

| | Risk | Mitigation Strategy |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| **1** | **Patients from underrepresented ancestries get the worst molecular predictions** (modeling failure mode + equity failure mode). PGC benchmarks lean European-ancestry; AlphaGenome-derived predictions inherit imbalance from PsychENCODE. | Stratify NBB validation by ancestry sub-cohort; ancestry-disaggregated demographic-performance cards as a hard release gate. Cross-ancestry validation with NeuroGAP-Psychosis (Ethiopia, Kenya, South Africa, Uganda) and H3Africa where DUAs permit. Pan-UKBB sum stats as a complementary M4 cross-ancestry reference for genotypic axes. PAC reviews fairness audits before any external delivery; ASAM and APA Council on Digital Health partners specifically vet ancestry-stratified outputs for the populations they represent. |
| **2** | **Patients are categorized by dimensional axes that don't replicate** (within-cohort axes don't recover PGC 14-disorder five-factor on independent samples; genotypic axes don't generalize to broader phenotypes). | NBB-internal leave-one-cohort-out across the six NBB sub-biobanks runs as a hard Go / No-Go gate: D_geno modularity Q > 0.3 (permutation p < 0.05) vs PGC 5-factor cluster assignment on ≥4 of 6 leave-one-out folds. Genotypic axes additionally validated against Pan-UKBB sum stats. Phenotypic-archetype validation against M2 D_pheno modularity is exploratory / stretch. Apply for [Fondation FondaMental open-science research data](https://www.fondation-fondamental.org/en/our-actions/open-science-access-to-research-data) as an independent external validation cohort. If gates miss: bioRxiv negative-result + diagnostic decomposition (which factors recovered, which did not, which cohort effects detected); multi-target validation across PGC + Pan-UKBB independently. |
| **3** | **Patients lose access to the model because controlled-access cohorts can't be openly released** (NBB, PsychENCODE WGS must remain controlled-access; weights may inadvertently re-encode individual-level information). | Cytognosis controlled-access release pattern, Astera-OSP-aligned: code MIT; weights from open data CC0; weights from controlled-access cohorts under OpenRAIL-M (Astera FAQ data-embargo exception, with Prachee Avasthi). | **4** | **Patients face delays from compute capacity ceilings** (AlphaGenome 24-BICCN fine-tune + joint co-embedding + ablation can exceed initial compute allocation during M12-18). | Stage compute hot-spots: schedule embedding generation early Phase 2 (M6-9) so it doesn't collide with the Phase-3 optimization peak. | **Patient Advocacy Council, why and how** | Models that don't reach patients don't reduce suffering. Too many open-science deliverables ship and shelve: published, indexed, ignored. The PAC is our structural defense against that outcome. Members are **representatives of patient-advocacy organizations**, not individuals with diagnoses, so engagement is structurally aligned with the communities served: One Mind (transdiagnostic), Center for Digital Mental Health (Harvard / MGH, 125+ scientist-clinician network), APA Council on Digital Health Innovation and Technology, and ASAM (SUD-specific privacy + adoption). Three engagement stages are funded in this budget. **Stage 1 (M1)** gathers patient priorities, fairness concerns, adoption barriers, and clinician-facing communication standards that gate every release. **Stage 2 (M3)** delivers a partner-only prototype preview with structured feedback sessions surfacing translational + adoption blockers. **Stage 3 (M4)** supports translational-research pilots through Council partner organizations, feeding adoption telemetry + clinician feedback into a v1.1 refinement cycle. Each stage produces a CC BY 4.0 brief that becomes a formal release-gate document for everything downstream. The same Council is engaged through the Google.org Round-2 track, ensuring one coherent patient-advocacy interface across the full Neuroverse program. |
| **Helix Framework rationale** | Openness as a stated principle is now standard; openness as functioning practice that delivers patient impact is rarer. The Helix Framework community commentary, **released at M6** as a structural / open-science deliverable (CC BY 4.0; bioRxiv + Zenodo, DOI), takes the ~5-year mark since FROs were introduced as the moment to look back at what FROs, ARPA-like agencies, and hybrid for-profit / nonprofit models have actually delivered, and to propose a path forward for impact-first organizations targeting moonshots. **Two arguments.** (1) Field needs **shared boilerplate**, not re-derived legal + operational scaffolding for every cross-institution collaboration: federated DUAs, evaluation harnesses, reproducibility tooling, licensing patterns (MIT for code; CC0 for weights and data from open sources; OpenRAIL-M for weights from controlled-access cohorts where unrestricted release would re-encode individual signal; CC0 for synthetic-data substitutes; CC BY 4.0 for articles), building on Wellcome Leap MARFA / CORFA. (2) Organizational structures should **intertwine nonprofit mission-alignment with for-profit scalability from Day 1**, not retrofit scale at Year 5. Co-authored: Astera (Prachee Avasthi), Convergent Research (Adam Marblestone), Speculative Tech (Ben Reinhardt). Cytognosis Foundation deliberately implements the framework as a **first-wave testbed**, generating evidence the path forward is operational rather than theoretical. M6 release maximizes the time the framework has to influence the 2027-2028 wave of FROs / ARPA-like agencies / hybrid orgs forming. |
