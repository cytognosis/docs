# Neuroanatomy Guide for the Biotype Docs

> **Plain-language reference for every brain region and network the biotype docs mention.** Use it whenever a term like "subgenual ACC" or "default mode network" shows up and you want to know what it actually is, where it sits, and what it does. ADHD-friendly: every region is a one-paragraph card; tables let you scan.

---

## Big-picture orientation

Before anything: the human brain has two halves (hemispheres), each with the same set of regions. When the docs say "right TPJ", they mean the temporoparietal junction on the right side. Most regions exist on both sides.

The brain has four cortical lobes and a bunch of deep structures.

**The four lobes (think of these as the outside surface):**

| Lobe | Where it sits | What it does (one line) |
|------|---------------|-------------------------|
| **Frontal** | Front, behind your forehead | Planning, decision-making, motor control, personality |
| **Parietal** | Top-rear, above your ears | Spatial sense, attention, body awareness |
| **Temporal** | Sides, behind your temples | Hearing, memory, language, face recognition |
| **Occipital** | Back of your head | Vision |

**Deep structures (think of these as inside, under the cortex):**

| Structure | Where it sits | What it does (one line) |
|-----------|---------------|-------------------------|
| **Amygdala** | Deep in the temporal lobe, almond-sized, near the front | Emotional learning, threat detection |
| **Hippocampus** | Just behind the amygdala, curled like a seahorse | Memory formation, spatial maps |
| **Thalamus** | Center of the brain, between the hemispheres | Sensory relay station |
| **Striatum** | Deep, surrounds the thalamus | Reward, habit, movement |
| **Cerebellum** | Behind the brainstem, looks like a cauliflower | Coordination, learning, also cognitive functions |
| **Brainstem** | Bottom, connects to spinal cord | Breathing, sleep-wake, autonomic |

**Cortical anatomical directions (the docs use these constantly):**

- **Dorsal** = top (toward the top of your head)
- **Ventral** = bottom (toward your eyes / mouth)
- **Anterior** = front (toward your forehead)
- **Posterior** = back (toward the back of your head)
- **Medial** = middle (toward the midline, between the hemispheres)
- **Lateral** = side (away from midline, toward your ear)
- **Rostral** = same as anterior (toward the front)
- **Caudal** = same as posterior (toward the back)

So **dorsolateral prefrontal cortex (DLPFC)** = top + side + front-most lobe. **Subgenual ACC** = below the genu (curve) of the corpus callosum, in the cingulate gyrus.

## Prefrontal cortex regions (the front of the brain)

The prefrontal cortex (PFC) is where most psychiatric biotypes live. It is the front part of the frontal lobe.

### Dorsolateral PFC (DLPFC, Brodmann areas 9 and 46)

**Where:** top-and-side region of the frontal lobe (left and right). If you put your finger on your temple and slide it forward about 2 cm and up about 4 cm, you are over the DLPFC.

**What it does:** working memory, executive function, cognitive control, planning. The "thinking" part of the PFC.

**Why it matters:** the canonical target for TMS in depression. Stanford SAINT targets it with personalized rsfMRI. Implicated in the cognitive-control biotype across all 6 disorders.

### Dorsomedial PFC (dmPFC) and dorsal anterior cingulate cortex (dACC)

**Where:** top of the frontal lobe at the midline. The dACC sits just above the corpus callosum on the medial wall.

**What it does:** monitoring conflict and errors, switching attention, weighing salient information, monitoring effort. The brain's "what is important right now" detector.

**Why it matters:** part of the salience network. Enhanced ERN (error-related negativity, an EEG signature) here is an OCD endophenotype. Frontal midline theta over this region tracks trait anxiety.

### Ventromedial PFC (vmPFC)

**Where:** bottom-and-middle of the frontal lobe. Right behind and slightly above your eyes, at the midline.

**What it does:** value computation, emotion regulation, fear extinction, self-referential thinking. The PFC's "is this good or bad for me" calculator.

**Why it matters:** central to reward biotypes, emotion regulation in depression and PTSD, the threat-circuit (it normally inhibits the amygdala; when it fails, threat reactivity goes up).

### Orbitofrontal cortex (OFC), medial and lateral

**Where:** bottom of the frontal lobe, right above the eye sockets (orbits). Medial OFC is more central; lateral OFC is more to the sides.

**What it does:** value-based decision-making, reward valuation, flexible behavior. Medial OFC handles reward; lateral OFC handles punishment and behavioral inhibition.

**Why it matters:** addiction biotypes (the reward-deficiency biotype centers here), OCD (medial OFC hyperactivity is the canonical CSTC marker), and depression-anhedonia.

### Subgenual ACC (sgACC, Brodmann area 25)

**Where:** below the genu (the front curve) of the corpus callosum. Deep, medial, near the front. Hard to reach with surface optics; usually indexed indirectly via its connectivity with the DLPFC.

**What it does:** sadness, depressive rumination, autonomic regulation, emotional pain.

**Why it matters:** Helen Mayberg's DBS target for treatment-resistant depression. Fox and Cash 2012 showed that the strength of anticorrelation between sgACC and DLPFC predicts TMS response. Stanford SAINT uses this anticorrelation to pick each patient's individualized DLPFC target.

### Pregenual ACC (pgACC)

**Where:** just above the sgACC, in front of (anterior to) the genu of the corpus callosum.

**What it does:** emotion regulation, fear extinction, top-down control over the amygdala.

**Why it matters:** threat-dysregulation biotype in anxiety (when pgACC fails to regulate amygdala). SSRI and CBT response is partly predicted by baseline pgACC reactivity.

### Inferior frontal gyrus (IFG)

**Where:** bottom-front of the frontal lobe, on the lateral surface. The left IFG contains Broca's area (language production). The right IFG handles response inhibition.

**What it does:** language (left), response inhibition and motor inhibition (right), mirror neuron system, action observation.

**Why it matters:** added to the headset target list for autism (social brain biotype, mirror neuron involvement). Also relevant in addiction (right IFG inhibitory control).

## Cingulate cortex (a long strip on the medial wall)

The cingulate gyrus runs along the medial surface of each hemisphere, wrapped around the corpus callosum like a collar.

### Anterior cingulate cortex (ACC)

Divided into subgenual (sgACC, below the genu), pregenual (pgACC, above and in front), and dorsal (dACC, on top). See above for each part.

### Posterior cingulate cortex (PCC)

**Where:** posterior (back) end of the cingulate gyrus, on the medial wall, behind the corpus callosum.

**What it does:** self-referential processing, autobiographical memory, mind-wandering. The "where am I in my life right now" hub.

**Why it matters:** central node of the default mode network. Implicated in depression-rumination and PTSD.

### Precuneus

**Where:** just above and behind the PCC, on the medial parietal lobe.

**What it does:** self-awareness, episodic memory retrieval, perspective-taking.

**Why it matters:** part of the default mode network. Tightly coupled with PCC; the docs often refer to them together as "PCC/precuneus".

---

## Insula (hidden between frontal and temporal lobes)

**Where:** if you pulled apart the frontal and temporal lobes, you would see a triangular slab of cortex called the insula tucked inside. Divided into anterior, mid, and posterior parts.

**What it does:** interoception (sensing your internal body state), emotional feeling, salience detection, taste.

**Subregions:**

- **Anterior insula:** subjective feeling of emotion, craving, salience switching. The most-cited insula subdivision in the biotype docs.
- **Mid-insula:** integrative; physical interoception.
- **Posterior insula:** primary interoception (heart rate, breathing, pain).

**Why it matters:** salience-interoceptive biotype in panic and addiction-craving (Naqvi insula lesion studies showed insula damage stops smoking craving). Central in anxiety biotypes. Part of the salience network with dACC.

## Temporal lobe regions

### Superior temporal gyrus (STG), Heschl's gyrus, and planum temporale

**Where:** top of the temporal lobe. Heschl's gyrus is the primary auditory cortex (where sound first arrives in the cortex). Planum temporale sits just behind Heschl's.

**What it does:** hearing, auditory feature extraction, speech perception.

**Why it matters:** B-SNIP Biotype 2 (sensory-hyperresponsive psychosis) shows reduced 40Hz auditory steady-state response here. Heschl's is the psychosis-specific anchor region.

### Superior temporal sulcus (STS)

**Where:** the long groove (sulcus) that runs along the top of the temporal lobe, separating STG from the middle temporal gyrus.

**What it does:** social perception, biological motion, gaze processing, speech-related multisensory integration. The "watching other people" hub.

**Why it matters:** added to the headset target list for autism social-brain biotype. STS hypoactivation during social tasks is one of the most-replicated autism fMRI findings.

### Fusiform face area (FFA)

**Where:** on the ventral (bottom) surface of the temporal lobe, in the fusiform gyrus. Right hemisphere FFA dominates for face processing.

**What it does:** face recognition, face individuation. Specialized for faces in most people.

**Why it matters:** autism social brain biotype (reduced FFA activation for faces in autism). Also added to the headset target list.

### Temporoparietal junction (TPJ)

**Where:** where the temporal and parietal lobes meet, on the lateral surface. Usually discussed as right TPJ.

**What it does:** attention reorienting (the ventral attention network), theory of mind (understanding others' mental states), self-other distinction.

**Why it matters:** VAN-deficit biotype in PTSD (Etkin's right TPJ finding predicts psychotherapy non-response). Also autism social-brain biotype.

### Hippocampus

**Where:** curled inside the medial temporal lobe (toward the midline), shaped like a seahorse. Subdivided into CA1, CA2, CA3, CA4, and dentate gyrus.

**What it does:** episodic memory formation, spatial maps, contextual fear conditioning.

**Why it matters:** depression and PTSD show hippocampal volume reductions. Schizophrenia (Grace lab) shows CA1 hyperactivity that drives downstream dopamine release in the striatum. CA1 is the schizophrenia-specific hippocampal subregion.

### Amygdala

**Where:** just in front of (anterior to) the hippocampus, deep in the medial temporal lobe. Almond-sized and almond-shaped (amygdala = "almond" in Greek). Subdivided into basolateral, central, and several other nuclei.

**What it does:** emotional learning (especially fear), threat detection, salience, emotional memory.

**Why it matters:** core of the threat-reactive biotype across anxiety, PTSD, and anxious depression. Overmodulated in dissociative PTSD (Lanius). Cannot be directly reached by surface optics; usually indexed via vmPFC connectivity.

### Bed nucleus of the stria terminalis (BNST)

**Where:** extended amygdala. A small structure near the midline, just dorsal (above) to the amygdala proper, near the anterior commissure.

**What it does:** sustained threat response (vs the amygdala's phasic threat response). When the threat is not immediate but is potentially coming, BNST is active.

**Why it matters:** BNST-driven sustained-threat biotype in generalized anxiety (Davis, Grillon, Shackman). Also addiction stress-driven negative-affect biotype.

## Subcortical (deep) structures

### Striatum (caudate, putamen, nucleus accumbens)

**Where:** deep, surrounds the thalamus. Three parts:

- **Caudate nucleus:** C-shaped, runs along the lateral ventricle. The "head of the caudate" sits near the front.
- **Putamen:** lateral to (outside of) the caudate, separated by the internal capsule.
- **Nucleus accumbens (NAc):** the ventral striatum, where caudate and putamen merge at the bottom-front.

**What it does:** reward (NAc), habit and motor learning (putamen), goal-directed action (caudate). Receives massive dopamine input from the midbrain.

**Why it matters:** central to addiction (all 5 biotypes implicate striatum), depression-anhedonic biotype (NAc hypofunction), OCD (caudate head hyperactivity is the CSTC marker), psychosis (Howes Type A hyperdopaminergic associative striatum).

### Thalamus

**Where:** center of the brain, between the hemispheres. Divided into many nuclei.

**Key subdivision: mediodorsal thalamus (MD).** Sits in the medial part of the thalamus.

**What it does:** sensory relay (almost all sensory information passes through the thalamus before reaching cortex), prefrontal-cortex coordination (via MD), arousal.

**Why it matters:** B-SNIP Biotype 1 and 2 psychosis both show MD thalamus abnormalities (Anticevic and Woodward thalamocortical dysconnectivity). CSTC loops route through MD thalamus. Sleep-spindle deficits (schizophrenia, autism, PTSD) come from the thalamic reticular nucleus.

### Ventral tegmental area (VTA)

**Where:** midbrain (top of the brainstem). Small but high-impact.

**What it does:** produces dopamine. Projects to the NAc (mesolimbic pathway, reward) and to the PFC (mesocortical pathway, cognition).

**Why it matters:** anhedonic and reward-deficit biotypes. Grace's hippocampus-VTA loop drives the hyperdopaminergic state in schizophrenia Type A.

### Cerebellum

**Where:** behind the brainstem, looks like a cauliflower. Subdivided into many lobules. Crus I and Crus II are part of the lateral cerebellum (in the posterior lobe).

**What it does:** motor coordination (classic), but also cognitive and emotional functions, especially in lateral cerebellum (Crus I/II). The "internal model" hub.

**Why it matters:** right Crus I/II added to the headset target list for autism. Also implicated in psychosis (Andreasen's "cognitive dysmetria") and addiction-alcohol (cerebellar atrophy).

## Major functional networks (groups of regions that work together)

Functional networks are groups of brain regions that consistently activate or deactivate together. They are not separate from the anatomical regions above; they are patterns of co-activation. Each region can belong to one or several networks.

### Default Mode Network (DMN)

**Members:** posterior cingulate cortex, precuneus, medial PFC (including vmPFC), inferior parietal lobule (angular gyrus), lateral and medial temporal cortex.

**What it does:** activates when you are at rest, mind-wandering, thinking about yourself, recalling memories, imagining the future. Deactivates when you focus on external tasks.

**Why it matters:** depression-rumination biotype (PCC and sgACC hyperconnectivity). PTSD also shows DMN abnormalities. Psilocybin produces dramatic DMN desynchronization during the trip; this correlates with sustained antidepressant response.

### Salience Network (SN)

**Members:** anterior insula (bilateral) and dorsal anterior cingulate cortex (dACC). Sometimes also includes amygdala.

**What it does:** detects salient stimuli (internal or external), switches between the DMN and the central executive network. The "gatekeeper" between resting and task-focused brain states.

**Why it matters:** anxiety-panic biotype (insula and dACC hyperconnectivity), addiction craving (Naqvi insula), interoceptive vigilance in anxiety. Salience network connectivity predicts deep TMS response for smoking cessation.

### Central Executive Network (CEN) / Frontoparietal Network

**Members:** dorsolateral PFC (DLPFC), posterior parietal cortex (especially intraparietal sulcus), dorsomedial PFC.

**What it does:** working memory, cognitive control, goal-directed attention. Activates when you focus on a demanding task.

**Why it matters:** cognitive-control biotype across all 6 disorders. Canonical TMS target.

### Threat / fear circuit

**Members:** amygdala (basolateral and central), bed nucleus of stria terminalis (BNST), ventromedial PFC (which normally inhibits the amygdala), pregenual ACC, anterior insula, hippocampus.

**What it does:** detects and responds to threat. The amygdala learns associations; vmPFC and pgACC modulate (turn down) the response; BNST sustains anxiety when the threat is uncertain or distant.

**Why it matters:** anxiety biotypes (especially threat-dysregulation in GAD), PTSD threat-reactive biotype, anxious-depression. When the modulators (vmPFC, pgACC) fail to control the amygdala, threat reactivity stays high.

### Reward circuit (mesolimbic and mesocortical)

**Members:** ventral tegmental area (VTA, dopamine source), nucleus accumbens (NAc), ventromedial PFC, orbitofrontal cortex. Also: ventral pallidum, lateral hypothalamus.

**What it does:** processes reward, predicts reward, drives motivation. Phasic dopamine release in NAc signals reward prediction errors.

**Why it matters:** addiction biotypes (Volkow framework), depression-anhedonic biotype (Pizzagalli's work), PTSD-dysphoric biotype, schizophrenia negative symptoms.

### Ventral Attention Network (VAN)

**Members:** right temporoparietal junction (TPJ), right ventral frontal cortex.

**What it does:** reorients attention when an unexpected event happens. Acts as a "circuit breaker" for the dorsal attention network.

**Why it matters:** Etkin and Maron-Katz VAN-deficit biotype predicts psychotherapy non-response in PTSD and treatment-resistance in depression.

### Dorsal Attention Network (DAN)

**Members:** frontal eye fields, intraparietal sulcus, superior parietal lobule.

**What it does:** top-down goal-directed attention. When you look for a specific thing, DAN is active.

**Why it matters:** less prominent in the biotype docs but appears in autism (Bethlehem-Lombardo normative deviation) and attention-deficit profiles.

### Cortico-striato-thalamo-cortical (CSTC) loops

**Members:** orbitofrontal cortex (medial and lateral), dorsal anterior cingulate, caudate (especially head of caudate), mediodorsal thalamus. Loops back to cortex.

**What it does:** action selection, habit formation, repetitive behavior control. The "decide what to do, do it, evaluate, repeat" circuit.

**Why it matters:** OCD biotype (CSTC hyperactivity). FDA-approved deep TMS for OCD targets exactly this loop. Also relevant in addiction (compulsive use is partly a CSTC dysfunction).

### Social brain network

**Members:** superior temporal sulcus (STS), fusiform face area (FFA), medial PFC, temporoparietal junction (TPJ), inferior frontal gyrus (IFG), amygdala.

**What it does:** social perception, theory of mind, face and biological motion processing, empathy.

**Why it matters:** autism social-brain biotype (hypoactivation across all these regions). Added 4 regions to the headset target list specifically to cover this network.

## What's accessible to a wearable optical sensor

Cytoscope reaches surface and shallow-subcortical depths (roughly 3 to 4 cm into adult cortex, with Marie-Nelly's depth-zoom extending to 2 to 3 cm at higher spectroscopic resolution). Here is what is and is not directly reachable:

**Directly reachable from a wearable optical headset:**

- All cortical regions (DLPFC, dACC, dmPFC, vmPFC, OFC, IFG, STS, STG/Heschl's, FFA, PCC/precuneus, TPJ, insula via thinned anterior temporal cortex, sensorimotor)

**Not directly reachable; must be indexed indirectly:**

- Subgenual ACC (deep midline; indexed via DLPFC anticorrelation, the SAINT trick)
- Hippocampus (medial temporal; could be partially accessed with Marie-Nelly depth-zoom)
- Amygdala (medial temporal, deep; index via vmPFC and pgACC connectivity)
- BNST (deep midline)
- Ventral striatum / NAc (deep; index via vmPFC and OFC connectivity)
- Caudate, putamen, MD thalamus (deep central; not reachable noninvasively with optics)
- VTA (midbrain; not reachable)
- Cerebellum (the lateral cerebellum is reachable; deeper cerebellar nuclei are not)

The eight-anchor headset target list (DLPFC, dACC, vmPFC/OFC, anterior insula, sgACC via anticorrelation, superior temporal/Heschl's, PCC/precuneus, ventral striatum/amygdala via cortical effectors) covers the directly reachable cortex and the indirectly indexable deep structures.
