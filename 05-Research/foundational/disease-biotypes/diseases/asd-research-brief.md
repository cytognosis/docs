# Biotypes of Autism Spectrum Disorder: Multi-Modal Evidence for a Mental Health Coordinate System

*Research brief prepared for the Cytognosis Foundation + Hervé Marie-Nelly NSF X-Labs and ARPA-H PHO submission.*

*Date: 25 May 2026.*

## Framing and a note on language

Autism spectrum disorder (ASD) is constitutionally heterogeneous. The clinical category includes nonspeaking children with profound disability, intellectually gifted adults who reach diagnosis in their 40s, and many people in between. Decades of attempts to find a single biological signature for "autism" have failed, and contemporary opinion in both basic science and the autistic community converges on the view that "autism" denotes a family of overlapping neurodevelopmental conditions with shared behavioural surface features. Stratification, not unification, is the productive research direction (1, 2).

Throughout this document, we use both person-first ("people with autism") and identity-first ("autistic people") language. Many autistic self-advocates prefer identity-first language; many parents and clinicians prefer person-first; the autistic community is genuinely split (3). We also frame biotypes as serving personalized support, accommodation, and (where wanted) targeted therapeutics for co-occurring conditions, not as instruments to eliminate autism itself. Several frameworks reviewed below, notably the extreme male brain hypothesis and the intense world theory, have drawn substantive critique from autistic researchers and the broader community; we flag those critiques where relevant.

For an adaptive spectroscopy headset that maps users into a "mental health coordinate space," autism is unusually informative for two reasons. First, autism has the richest pediatric EEG and MEG biotype literature of any of the conditions in our scope, providing dense electrophysiological signatures that are detectable noninvasively. Second, autism overlaps genetically and phenotypically with schizophrenia, ADHD, anxiety, and intellectual disability, allowing transdiagnostic anchors.

## 1. Connectomic and circuit biotypes

### 1.1 Lombardo and Bethlehem normative modelling (Cambridge)

The Cambridge group around Bethlehem, Lombardo, Marquand and Beckmann has pushed normative modelling as a replacement for hard clustering. Normative modelling fits a typically developing reference trajectory across age (analogous to growth charts) and then expresses each individual as a deviation map. Zabihi, Floris, Lombardo and colleagues (4) applied this to EU-AIMS LEAP cortical thickness data (316 autistic and 206 typically developing participants, ages 6 to 31, across six sites) and derived five biologically grounded putative subtypes via spectral clustering of deviation maps: three with widespread decreased cortical thickness and two with regions of increased thickness. Cluster 4 (about 17% of autistic participants) showed thickening centred on primary somatosensory and motor cortex, superior parietal lobule, associative visual cortex, and frontal eye fields, consistent with a sensory-perceptual subtype.

Bethlehem and colleagues (5) extended this to functional networks, finding that age-atypicality in DMN, salience, and limbic networks tracks behavioural variability, with a subgroup showing marked age-divergence and another tracking close to typical maturation. Critically, the authors caution that these subtypes are statistical decompositions, not natural kinds, and they have explicitly retreated from earlier hard clustering work that did not replicate across cohorts (1).

### 1.2 Default mode network: long-range under-connectivity vs local hyperconnectivity

The "long-range under-connectivity, local hyperconnectivity" hypothesis is one of the older organising frames for autism connectomics. Most large-scale studies confirm that children with autism show reduced long-distance functional connectivity between DMN nodes (medial PFC, posterior cingulate, angular gyrus) along with increased local connectivity within DMN nodes and within visual and motor networks (6, 7). The picture is age- and method-dependent. Typical DMN long-distance functional connectivity grows quadratically through adolescence, peaking around ages 11 to 13, whereas autistic children show attenuated maturation of these connections (8). In adults, the pattern partly normalises and sometimes inverts, especially when motion is rigorously regressed (7).

The ABIDE I and II mega-analyses (over 2000 participants combined) reframed the field. ASD-related decreases in intrinsic connectivity dominate cortico-cortical edges, especially in unimodal association areas and paralimbic circuits, whereas the subcortical hyperconnectivity findings (notably thalamic) are smaller and less consistent (9, 10). Posterior cingulate, mid- and posterior insula, and thalamus emerge as cross-study convergent loci.

### 1.3 Salience network and social brain

Salience network (anterior insula, dorsal anterior cingulate) connectivity is altered in autism from infancy. Six-week-old infants at elevated likelihood of developing autism show enhanced within-salience-network connectivity but reduced salience-to-orbitofrontal coupling, and salience-to-sensory connectivity predicts later sensory hypersensitivity (11). In children and adults, anterior insula coupling to amygdala, mPFC, and STS is reduced (12), and these alterations track sensory over-responsivity and socio-affective processing.

The "social brain" (STS, fusiform face area, TPJ, mPFC, amygdala, OFC) shows reproducible hypoactivation to face and biological-motion stimuli in autism, with the strongest effects in right STS and right inferior frontal cortex (13, 14). Effective connectivity studies place STS as a hub whose modulatory input to FFA and amygdala is reduced. Current consensus favours a distributed-network framing over single-region accounts (15).

### 1.4 Mottron enhanced perceptual functioning

Mottron, Dawson, Soulieres and colleagues advanced the Enhanced Perceptual Functioning (EPF) model: autistic perception is locally oriented, discrimination thresholds are lower for first-order static stimuli, and posterior cortical regions remain engaged in tasks that typically recruit frontal regions (16). Meta-analyses confirm relative posterior cortical hyperactivity during visual and auditory processing tasks and frontal hypoactivity for the same tasks (17). EPF reframes perceptual differences as features rather than deficits and is among the most empirically supported autism cognition models. EPF is closely related to, but more carefully grounded than, the controversial "intense world theory" of the Markrams, which the autistic community and some basic scientists have criticised for over-extrapolating from a single rat model.

### 1.5 Cerebellar contributions

Tsai and colleagues (18) showed that selectively deleting *Tsc1* in cerebellar Purkinje cells produces autism-relevant behaviours (social deficit, repetitive grooming, vocalisation changes) in mice, with reduced Purkinje cell numbers and stress markers. Subsequent work (19) demonstrated that optogenetic activation of right Crus I rescues behavioural deficits in multiple mouse models, establishing cerebellum as a causal node, not just a correlate. Human imaging converges on right Crus I/II and lobules VI/VII abnormalities (20, 21). Lobules VI and VII show atypically increased sensorimotor cerebral coupling and reduced supramodal cerebral coupling. Reduced grey matter in right Crus I, vermis VI/VIII and lobule IX correlates with worse communication scores (20).

### 1.6 EU-AIMS LEAP

The EU-AIMS Longitudinal European Autism Project (LEAP) is the largest multi-modal European stratification cohort (~800 participants spanning childhood to adulthood), with longitudinal MRI, EEG, eye-tracking, blood, and behavioural data (22). LEAP-derived 2025 publications have reported atypical resting-state EEG graph metrics linked to social cognition, multimodal neural signatures of face processing, and locus coeruleus tonic upregulation linked to auditory information processing (23). LEAP provides one of the few cohorts where multi-modal coordinate spaces can be empirically validated.

### 1.7 ENIGMA-ASD

The ENIGMA-ASD working group (1571 ASD vs 1651 controls, 49 sites, ages 2 to 64) found that autism is associated with greater cortical thickness in frontal regions, lower thickness in temporal regions, lower volumes in several subcortical structures, and reduced leftward asymmetry of mean cortical thickness (24). Effects are largest in adolescence, consistent with altered cortical maturation rather than a static lesion. ENIGMA-ASD effect sizes are modest at the group level (Cohen's d typically 0.1 to 0.3), reinforcing the case for stratification.

### 1.8 Sensory subtypes

Dunn's sensory processing framework distinguishes over-responsivity, under-responsivity, and sensory seeking. ASD youth with sensory over-responsivity show reduced amygdala-OFC discrimination between social and nonsocial stimuli and exaggerated sensory-motor responses to social stimuli (25). A 2025 transdiagnostic study confirmed that sensory over-responsivity has replicable behavioural and neural correlates across ASD, anxiety, and OCD, supporting a transdiagnostic sensory biotype (26).

## 2. EEG and MEG biotypes (primary expansion)

### 2.1 Gamma-band oscillations and the parvalbumin link

Gamma oscillations (30 to 80 Hz) depend on rhythmic perisomatic inhibition by fast-spiking parvalbumin-positive (PV) interneurons. Sohal and colleagues established that driving PV cells generates gamma rhythmicity and inhibiting PV cells suppresses it (27). Postmortem and imaging work documents decreased PV interneuron density and PV+ chandelier cell counts in autism across multiple cortical areas (28, 29). Functionally, adults with autism show reduced amplitude and phase locking of induced gamma during perceptual organisation, with frontal-posterior gamma desynchrony (30). Children and adolescents show reduced resting gamma power lateralised to the right hemisphere (31). Gamma deficits constitute one of the best replicated EEG biotype anchors and converge with the broader excitation-inhibition imbalance frame.

### 2.2 Mismatch negativity (MMN)

A 2025 systematic review and meta-analysis of MMN in autism (32) confirmed reduced MMN amplitudes in autistic children and adolescents, especially in multifeature paradigms. MMN reductions correlate with impaired adaptive functioning (33). Critically, MMN reduction is not autism-specific; it is a transdiagnostic vulnerability marker that also indexes schizophrenia, dyslexia, and aging. For coordinate-space purposes, MMN is best treated as a dimensional axis, not a diagnostic feature.

### 2.3 Prepulse inhibition and P50 sensory gating

Findings are mixed. Some studies report reduced PPI in high-functioning autistic adults (34), but pediatric studies often find no PPI or P50 sensory gating differences when controls are well matched (35). The mixed picture probably reflects substantial within-spectrum heterogeneity. PPI may identify a subgroup with prominent sensorimotor gating deficits rather than serve as a population-level marker.

### 2.4 Auditory steady-state response (40 Hz ASSR)

The 40 Hz auditory steady-state response (ASSR) is a pharmacodynamic biomarker of cortical NMDA-PV circuits (36). A 2026 meta-analysis aggregating schizophrenia, bipolar disorder, and ASD studies confirmed reduced 40 Hz ASSR power across all three conditions (37). In autism, reductions are most consistent in left auditory cortex and correlate with language ability (38). A SHANK3 partial duplication case report (39) and broader genetic-syndromic work support 40 Hz ASSR as a translational biomarker bridging genetic subtypes (notably SHANK3) to cortical gamma physiology. The ASSR is testable in young children and intellectually disabled adults who cannot complete cognitive tasks, making it operationally valuable for an adaptive headset.

### 2.5 Aperiodic 1/f slope (excitation-inhibition proxy)

Donoghue, Voytek and colleagues (40) operationalised the aperiodic (1/f) component of EEG power spectra and proposed it as a noninvasive proxy of cortical excitation-inhibition (E/I) ratio: steeper slopes indicate relative inhibition; flatter slopes indicate relative excitation. A 2022 study (41) reported a globally elevated E/I ratio (flatter aperiodic slope) in autistic children with below-average intelligence. A 2025 task-related study (42) found that autistic adults show steeper aperiodic slopes during active task vs passive viewing, whereas neurotypicals do not. A 2024 infant longitudinal study (43) reported that the change in aperiodic activity over the first year of life predicts later autism diagnosis and language outcome at 18 months among siblings with family history. Fragile X syndrome shows divergent aperiodic and alpha dynamics (44), supporting aperiodic slope as a within-spectrum stratifier rather than a single-direction marker.

### 2.6 Resting alpha peak frequency

Alpha peak frequency (PAF) increases with brain maturation, shifting from theta to alpha range across childhood. PAF is altered in autism in a manner that scales with cognitive function across the spectrum: lower PAF tracks lower IQ within autistic samples (45). A large cross-sectional study (n=411, 212 with autism) confirmed atypical occipital alpha power and PAF developmental trajectories (46). Fragile X syndrome shows specific PAF and theta band signatures distinct from idiopathic autism (47), again supporting PAF as a stratification variable.

### 2.7 OPM-MEG (optically pumped magnetometer MEG)

Optically pumped magnetometer MEG is operationally important for pediatric autism imaging because it is wearable (no cryogenic helmet), motion-tolerant, head-size-adaptive, and substantially less aversive than conventional MEG or MRI for sensory-sensitive children (48, 49). 2024 and 2025 work (48, 50) demonstrated wireless OPM-MEG and pushed the technology toward newborn imaging. For Cytognosis purposes, OPM-MEG and high-density EEG together cover most of the gamma, ASSR, MMN, and aperiodic-slope biomarkers above and are increasingly compatible with adaptive paradigms.

### 2.8 Sleep EEG (spindles, slow waves)

Autistic children show reduced sleep spindle density in frontal and central regions, reduced spindle-slow-oscillation coupling, and atypical anteroposterior slow-wave gradients (51, 52, 53). 15q duplication syndrome and other genetic-syndromic forms of autism show even more pronounced spindle abnormalities (54). Machine-learning models using sleep spindle metrics, aperiodic slope, REM percentage, and spindle-slow-oscillation coupling discriminate autistic from typically developing children with reasonable cross-cohort generalisation (52).

### 2.9 Predictive coding biomarkers

Autism is frequently framed as a disorder of precision-weighted predictive inference: overly precise sensory observations combined with weak top-down priors (55). MEG and EEG indices of prediction error (MMN, P3a) and sensory precision (steady-state phase locking, gamma) provide convergent biomarkers. A non-human primate ASD model (56) showed erroneous predictive coding across multiple hierarchical levels, and a 2023 study (57) reported that predictive coding neural signatures track individuals along an autism-schizophrenia continuum, consistent with shared genetic architecture.

### 2.10 Eye-tracking-EEG co-registered biotypes

Joint EEG and eye-tracking recording is now technically tractable and is increasingly applied to autism (58, 59). Graph-convolutional networks operating on joint EEG and eye-tracking features detect autism with accuracy higher than either modality alone (58). Pupillometry adds an autonomic axis: gaze following and pupil dilation are promising early diagnostic markers in toddlers (60). For a coordinate headset, eye-tracking and pupillometry are low-cost additions that materially raise biotype resolution.

## 3. Molecular and cellular biotypes

### 3.1 mTOR pathway (PTEN, TSC1/2, NF1)

PTEN, TSC1/TSC2, and NF1 are negative regulators of PI3K-mTOR. Heterozygous mutations substantially elevate autism risk and produce identifiable autism-related Mendelian syndromes (tuberous sclerosis, neurofibromatosis 1, PTEN hamartoma tumor syndrome) (61, 62). Sahin and colleagues at Boston Children's have led much of the translational work. The mTOR-pathway biotype provides a molecularly tractable subgroup with clear circuit-level signatures (altered dendritic spines, E/I shifts) and pharmacological probes (rapamycin, everolimus).

### 3.2 SHANK1, SHANK2, SHANK3 (synaptopathies)

Shank scaffolding proteins organise postsynaptic densities of excitatory glutamatergic synapses. SHANK3 mutations cause Phelan-McDermid syndrome and a substantial fraction of severe idiopathic autism (63). Shank3B null mice show repetitive grooming, social deficits, vocalisation differences, reduced open-field activity, and enhanced gamma-band oscillatory activity indicative of enhanced inhibitory tone (64). Shank2 rat models show striatal mGluR1 hyperactivation, hyperactivity, and repetitive behaviour (65).

### 3.3 NLGN, NRXN, CNTNAP2 cell-adhesion biotypes

Neuroligin-neurexin transsynaptic complexes specify excitatory vs inhibitory synapses (NLGN1 favours excitatory, NLGN2 favours inhibitory). Mutations in NLGN3, NLGN4, NRXN1, and CNTNAP2 (the contactin-associated protein 2) are recurrent in autism (66). CNTNAP2 knockouts show core autism behavioural domains and prominent cortical migration defects.

### 3.4 GABAergic interneuron loss (parvalbumin, somatostatin)

Postmortem human autism cortex shows reduced parvalbumin interneuron and chandelier cell counts, and reduced somatostatin interneuron density in some studies (28, 29). Many genetic and immune autism models converge on PV interneuron deficits, and this convergence underwrites the broader E/I imbalance hypothesis.

### 3.5 Excitation-inhibition imbalance as a core biotype

Rubenstein and Merzenich's 2003 E/I imbalance hypothesis (67) proposed elevated cortical excitation-to-inhibition ratio as a core feature of at least a subset of autism. Two decades of work supports the framework but qualifies it. Different autism subtypes show different E/I directions (some with elevated E/I, some with elevated I/E), and E/I imbalance is region- and circuit-specific rather than global (68, 69). Aperiodic slope, gamma oscillations, ASSR, and MRS GABA/glutamate provide convergent E/I biomarkers; their joint pattern may identify subgroups.

### 3.6 MRS GABA and glutamate

Magnetic resonance spectroscopy (MRS) GABA and glutamate measurements in autism are mixed across studies. Some report reduced GABA+ in sensorimotor cortex, others find elevated Glx (glutamate+glutamine) with unchanged GABA, others find no group differences (70, 71). Edden, Puts and colleagues have demonstrated that GABA+ in sensorimotor cortex tracks tactile discrimination behaviour both in autistic and typically developing samples, consistent with a dimensional rather than categorical framing (72). MRS is feasible in clinical MRI scanners and adds a molecular axis to the coordinate space.

### 3.7 Mitochondrial subtypes

Frye and Rossignol have argued for a mitochondrial autism subgroup (perhaps 5 to 30% of cases) characterised by elevated lactate or pyruvate, ultrastructural mitochondrial abnormalities, and developmental regression often triggered by inflammation (73, 74). A 2024 meta-analysis (75) confirmed several reproducible biochemical markers. Mitochondrial subgroups may be relevant for personalised intervention (mitochondrial cocktails) and for understanding regressive subtypes.

### 3.8 BDNF and TrkB

Serum BDNF in autism is variably reported as elevated, reduced, or unchanged, with study heterogeneity reflecting age, severity, and assay differences (76, 77). TrkB-PI3K-AKT1 signalling is reduced in idiopathic autism brain tissue (77). BDNF and TrkB are relevant for plasticity-targeted interventions including psychedelics and ketamine, where BDNF release is part of the mechanism of action.

## 4. Genetic and genomic biotypes

### 4.1 SFARI gene categorization

The SFARI Gene database (Q1 2025 release) lists 1136 scored genes and 94 uncategorised candidates (78). The scoring is high-confidence (Score 1), strong candidate (Score 2), suggestive evidence (Score 3), with an additional syndromic category for genes associated with autism-co-occurring syndromes. The Tier S designation marks the strongest-evidence syndromic genes (e.g. SHANK3, FMR1, MECP2, TSC1, TSC2). The total of over 1000 genes underscores extreme genetic heterogeneity.

### 4.2 Copy number variants

Recurrent autism-associated CNVs include 16p11.2 (most frequent recurrent de novo CNV in autism), 22q11.2 (deletion = velocardiofacial syndrome with overlap to schizophrenia), 15q11-13 (overlap with Angelman, Prader-Willi, Dup15q), 7q11.23 (where the reciprocal deletion produces Williams syndrome with its hypersocial phenotype, and duplications produce autism), and 17q12 (79, 80). The 7q11.23 duplication-deletion polarity provides a striking natural experiment for the sociability axis. CNV penetrance is variable and incomplete.

### 4.3 PGC ASD GWAS

Grove and colleagues (81) published the first well-powered ASD GWAS (n=18,381 cases, 27,969 controls) in Nature Genetics 2019, identifying five genome-wide-significant loci. Polygenic risk for autism partially overlaps with risk for ADHD, schizophrenia, major depressive disorder, and educational attainment. Cross-disorder polygenic analyses confirm shared genetic architecture; some loci act in opposite directions for ASD and ADHD, supporting a polygenic form of pleiotropy (82).

### 4.4 X-linked autism subtypes

X-linked autism includes FMR1 (Fragile X syndrome, the most common single-gene cause of autism, accounting for ~2% of male autism cases) (83), MECP2 (Rett syndrome, X-linked dominant, lethal in most males) (84), CDKL5, IQSEC2, and others. Fragile X has its own well-developed EEG biotype with distinct alpha and aperiodic signatures (44, 47), making it a useful within-spectrum landmark.

### 4.5 De novo vs inherited

De novo loss-of-function and missense variants in constrained genes account for a substantial share of severe autism, especially with co-occurring intellectual disability (85). Inherited common variants account for the bulk of polygenic risk in less severely affected probands. The de novo-inherited split partly maps to severity and to cognitive ability.

## 5. Immune and inflammatory biotypes

### 5.1 Maternal immune activation

Maternal immune activation (MIA) models (rodent and non-human primate) reliably produce autism-relevant offspring phenotypes (86, 87). Replication is robust for behavioural endpoints but more variable for cellular outcomes such as microglial activation, with some preclinical findings failing to replicate. Epidemiological work links maternal infections, autoimmune disease, and elevated maternal cytokines during pregnancy to elevated autism risk (88).

### 5.2 Microglial activation and TSPO PET

Postmortem and TSPO PET studies show evidence of microglial activation in subsets of autistic individuals (88, 89). MIA-exposed rodents show increased TSPO ligand binding in some studies but not others, reflecting the heterogeneity of the model. For human translation, TSPO PET is invasive and expensive; serum cytokine profiling is more scalable.

### 5.3 Cytokine profiles

Meta-analyses identify elevated peripheral IL-6, IL-1β, IL-12p70, MIF, eotaxin-1, MCP-1, IL-8, IL-7, IL-2, TNF-α, IL-17, and IL-4 in autistic vs control samples, with study-level heterogeneity (90, 91). A 2024 study (92) identified TNF-α, IL-6, and IL-8 (CXCL8) as a candidate immune subtype panel. Cytokine profiles may identify an immune autism subtype responsive to anti-inflammatory adjuncts.

### 5.4 Autoimmunity overlap

Maternal autoimmunity (lupus, antiphospholipid syndrome) and maternal anti-fetal-brain antibodies are linked to a subset of autism cases (88). Family history of autoimmunity is more prevalent in autistic vs control families.

## 6. GI and microbiome biotypes

### 6.1 Hsiao, Mazmanian and the gut-brain axis

Hsiao, Mazmanian and colleagues established that gut microbiota modulate autism-relevant behaviours in mouse models, including the MIA model, and that specific bacterial strains (e.g. *Bacteroides fragilis*) can rescue behavioural and intestinal phenotypes (93, 94). The work motivated multiple human microbiome studies.

### 6.2 GI comorbidity rates

GI symptoms (constipation, diarrhoea, abdominal pain) co-occur in approximately 30 to 50% of autistic children and correlate with autism severity ratings (95). The GI-behaviour link is bidirectional and not fully explained.

### 6.3 Microbiota transfer therapy

Kang, Krajmalnik-Brown and colleagues (96, 97) reported an open-label Microbiota Transfer Therapy (MTT) trial in 18 autistic children with GI symptoms, showing approximately 47% reduction in autism symptoms and sustained GI improvement at 2-year follow-up. Results are intriguing but require placebo-controlled replication; open-label improvements in autism are well documented across many interventions. MTT identifies a candidate gut-axis biotype for further investigation, particularly in regressive and GI-symptomatic subgroups.

## 7. Hormonal and endocrine biotypes

### 7.1 Oxytocin and vasopressin

The oxytocin and vasopressin systems regulate social behaviour and have been studied as autism therapeutic targets for over a decade. The SOARS-B trial (n=277 autistic children and adolescents, 24-week intranasal oxytocin, Sikich et al., NEJM 2021) (98) showed no significant primary or secondary outcome benefit and no subgroup effect. Balovaptan, a vasopressin V1a receptor antagonist, showed promising Vineland-II improvements in phase 2 (VANILLA trial) (99) but failed in phase 3 (V1aduct), leading Roche to discontinue the autism programme (100). The oxytocin-vasopressin story is a cautionary tale about responder heterogeneity and the limits of inferring subtype response from group means; if there is a responder subgroup, current biomarkers do not yet identify it.

### 7.2 Fetal testosterone (extreme male brain)

Baron-Cohen and colleagues proposed the extreme male brain hypothesis: autistic cognition reflects exaggerated "male" traits (systemising over empathising), with fetal testosterone as a candidate driver (101). The hypothesis has been critiqued by autistic researchers and rebutted by several large studies that find no link between adult testosterone and reduced cognitive empathy (102). Amniotic testosterone is itself a noisy proxy of fetal brain steroid exposure. A 2014 study (103) reported elevated fetal steroidogenic activity in autism, but the broader framework remains contested. We treat the EMB hypothesis as one possible developmental modifier rather than a primary biotype.

## 8. Single-cell and postmortem biotypes

### 8.1 PsychENCODE and the Velmeshev/Sestan atlas

Velmeshev, Schirmer, Sestan, Kriegstein and colleagues (104) used single-nucleus RNA sequencing on autistic and control postmortem cortex and identified cell-type-specific transcriptomic changes. Synaptic signalling genes in upper-layer (L2/3) cortico-cortical projection neurons and microglia activation-state genes correlated with autism severity. A follow-up by the Sestan group (105) reported convergent cell-type signatures across multiple ASD samples, again implicating L2/3 neurons and microglia.

### 8.2 Cortical L2/3 deep-layer pyramidal cells

The L2/3 projection neuron is the dominant convergent target. L2/3 neurons mediate cortico-cortical communication essential for sensory integration and social cognition. A 2025 study (106) identified a human-accelerated L2/3 neuron subtype, raising the possibility that autism-elevated prevalence in humans reflects vulnerability of an evolutionarily recent cell type.

### 8.3 Astrocytes and oligodendrocytes

Recent single-cell studies (107, 108) implicate distinct glial reactive states in autism, particularly oligodendrocyte maturation defects and astrocyte activation. Glial biotypes are an emerging dimension and overlap with mitochondrial and inflammatory subtypes.

### 8.4 2024 to 2026 single-cell findings

Recent work (108, 109) emphasises that autism involves interhemispheric and callosal-projecting neurons, superficial interneurons, and reactive glial states. Single-cell atlases now provide cell-type-specific gene programs that may eventually link genetic subtypes (e.g., SHANK3, CHD8) to circuit-level signatures detectable noninvasively.

## 9. Treatment-stratified biotypes

### 9.1 SSRIs for repetitive behaviours and anxiety

The STAART citalopram trial (110) showed no group benefit for repetitive behaviour in autistic children. Fluoxetine adult trials (111) and meta-analyses (112) show modest improvements in some adult subgroups but not children. A 2024 systematic review (113) concluded that evidence is insufficient to recommend SSRIs for core autism symptoms but supports their use for comorbid anxiety and depression in some subgroups. SSRIs likely identify a responder subgroup but the biomarker is not yet established.

### 9.2 Risperidone and aripiprazole for irritability

The only two FDA-approved autism medications, risperidone and aripiprazole, target irritability (114). Response rates are moderate; weight gain and metabolic side effects are common. The BAART study (Biomarkers in Autism of Aripiprazole and Risperidone Treatment) reports that IL-5 and other cytokine markers differ between responders and non-responders, suggesting an immune-stratified responder biotype (115).

### 9.3 Balovaptan and oxytocin subgroups

As above, SOARS-B and V1aduct failed. Post-hoc subgroup analyses have not identified consistent responder biotypes. Trial-based biomarker discovery in autism remains a known unsolved problem.

### 9.4 Rapamycin and everolimus in TSC autism

Rapamycin reverses social deficits in TSC mouse models, but only if given before a developmental window closes (62). The 12-month everolimus pediatric trial in TSC (n=32, ages 4 to 17) (116) showed no significant benefit on full-scale IQ, autism behaviours, or other neuropsychological outcomes. Translation has been disappointing despite strong preclinical rationale, possibly because of timing (treatment after critical periods) or downstream pathway redundancy.

### 9.5 EIBI responder profiles

Early Intensive Behavioral Intervention (EIBI, 20 to 40 hours per week for 1 to 3 years) shows large gains in some children. Higher intake IQ, younger age, and early social engagement (joint attention, imitation) predict stronger response (117, 118). The autistic community has critiqued some EIBI protocols (especially historical ABA) as compliance-focused and harmful; contemporary practice increasingly emphasises play-based, naturalistic developmental approaches such as Early Start Denver Model.

### 9.6 TMS in autism

Trials of rTMS over DLPFC (low-frequency for repetitive behaviours and irritability; high-frequency for social functioning) show modest effects in open-label and small randomised trials (119, 120). Bilateral DLPFC protocols (high-frequency left, low-frequency right) have shown improvements in eye fixation and core scores (120). Evidence is limited; large sham-controlled pediatric trials are now running.

### 9.7 Psilocybin in autism

The PSILAUT trial (King's College London, Compass Pathfinder, completed August 2024) used low-dose psilocybin to probe serotonergic function in autistic vs non-autistic adults (121). It is an experimental medicine study, not an efficacy trial. Preclinical work shows psilocybin rescues sociability in some autism mouse models (122). Adult trials of full-dose psilocybin in autism are absent, though Fonzo and colleagues at Stanford are investigating psychedelics in adjacent populations (depression, PTSD). Psychedelic autism trials should proceed cautiously given heightened sensory and anxiety risks and the autistic community's mixed views.

## 10. Developmental trajectory and age-stratified biotypes

### 10.1 Regressive vs non-regressive

About 25 to 30% of autistic children show regression of language and social skills between 15 and 30 months after apparently typical early development. Regressive autism is no longer treated as a categorical subtype but as a trajectory variant. Genetic and inflammatory triggers are over-represented in regressive subgroups (123).

### 10.2 Optimal outcome trajectory

Fein and colleagues (124, 125) demonstrated that 10 to 25% of children initially diagnosed with autism may later not meet diagnostic criteria, a phenomenon termed "optimal outcome." Optimal-outcome individuals show typical socialization and communication scores in adulthood but retain subtle differences. Early predictors include higher IQ, milder social deficits at diagnosis, and intensive early intervention. Whether optimal outcome reflects "loss" of autism or successful camouflage and adaptation is debated.

### 10.3 Adult-diagnosed autism

Adult-diagnosed autism, especially in women and gender-diverse adults, is increasingly recognised. The Female Phenotype Theory holds that diagnostic criteria were calibrated on boys and that autistic girls present with more internalising symptoms, better surface social functioning, and stronger camouflaging or masking (126, 127). Masking predicts delayed diagnosis and increased risk of burnout, anxiety, depression, and suicidality. Biomarkers for masked or compensated autism are largely undeveloped.

### 10.4 Lifespan studies

Autistic adults are at substantially elevated risk for anxiety, depression, GI disease, and early mortality. Long-term cohort studies (e.g., the AIMS-2-TRIALS follow-up of LEAP participants) are beginning to characterise the trajectories. Adaptive support needs change across the lifespan; a static "biotype at diagnosis" is insufficient.

## 11. Synthesis: defensible biotypes and brain regions for the headset target list

Across the modalities reviewed, four to six biotype anchors are sufficiently replicated to merit inclusion in an adaptive multi-modal coordinate space for autism. Each biotype is defined by convergent evidence across at least two modalities and links to specific brain regions or networks accessible to the planned spectroscopy headset.

### Biotype A. PV interneuron / E-I imbalance / gamma deficit biotype

Convergent evidence: reduced PV interneuron counts postmortem, reduced ASSR power at 40 Hz, altered aperiodic 1/f slope, reduced induced gamma during perceptual tasks, MRS GABA reductions in subsets. Regions: auditory cortex (Heschl's gyrus, planum temporale), DLPFC, sensorimotor cortex. Bridges to schizophrenia and intellectual disability via shared PV biology. References: 27 to 30, 37 to 42, 67 to 69.

### Biotype B. Social brain hypoactivation biotype

Convergent evidence: reduced STS, FFA, right IFG and amygdala activation to social stimuli; reduced effective connectivity in the social brain network; reduced eye-tracking gaze to social regions; reduced MMN to socially meaningful sounds in some studies. Regions: right STS, FFA, right IFG (mirror neuron / language), TPJ, mPFC. References: 13 to 15, 25, 58 to 60.

### Biotype C. Cerebellar-cortical biotype

Convergent evidence: structural and functional abnormalities in right Crus I/II and lobules VI/VII; correlations with language and communication; causal mouse work via Purkinje cell Tsc1 deletion. Regions: cerebellar lobules VI, VII, Crus I/II; cerebellar dentate to thalamic and cortical targets. References: 18 to 21.

### Biotype D. Sensory perception / EPF biotype

Convergent evidence: enhanced posterior cortical activation on perceptual tasks; sensory over-responsivity neural signatures in amygdala and OFC; salience network alterations from infancy; cortical-thickness subtype 4 from Zabihi-Lombardo work centred on sensorimotor and visual cortex. Regions: primary visual cortex, primary somatosensory and auditory cortex, anterior insula, OFC. References: 4, 16, 17, 25, 26.

### Biotype E. mTOR / synaptopathy / molecular biotype

Convergent evidence: causal Mendelian genes (PTEN, TSC1, TSC2, SHANK3, NLGN3/4, NRXN1, CNTNAP2, FMR1, MECP2); altered dendritic spine density; characteristic EEG signatures in single-gene autisms; partial pharmacological reversibility. Regions: cortex-wide, with specific patterns (e.g. Fragile X aperiodic and alpha; SHANK3 ASSR). References: 39, 44, 47, 61 to 66, 83, 84.

### Biotype F. Default mode and developmental trajectory biotype

Convergent evidence: long-range DMN under-connectivity with local hyperconnectivity in childhood; altered DMN maturation; Bethlehem and Lombardo normative deviation profiles distinguishing maturational subtypes; sleep spindle alterations. Regions: medial PFC, posterior cingulate cortex, angular gyrus / TPJ. References: 4, 5, 6 to 10, 51 to 53.

### Suggested anchor regions to add to the existing eight

The existing eight Cytognosis anchor regions (DLPFC, dACC, vmPFC/OFC, anterior insula, sgACC, superior temporal, posterior cingulate, ventral striatum/amygdala via cortical effectors) already cover several autism-relevant nodes. Recommended additions for autism:

1. **Superior temporal sulcus (STS), right-lateralised.** Hub of the social brain network. Reproducible hypoactivation across face and biological-motion paradigms.
2. **Fusiform face area (FFA), right ventral temporal cortex.** Face processing core node. Accessible via fNIRS over lateral occipitotemporal scalp and via MEG.
3. **Inferior frontal gyrus, pars opercularis (right and left).** Mirror-neuron and language node with reduced activation during action and emotion observation in autism.
4. **Cerebellum, right Crus I/II and lobules VI/VII.** Cerebellar coverage is technically demanding for fNIRS but achievable with thin-skull pediatric scalp configurations and routine for OPM-MEG. The strong causal mouse literature and convergent human imaging justify inclusion.

These four additions extend the headset coverage from frontal-limbic-striatal psychiatric circuits into the social brain, language, and cerebellar circuits central to autism while remaining compatible with the existing transdiagnostic anchors.

## 200-word synthesis summary

Across modalities, six biotypes have sufficient replication to anchor an autism coordinate-space implementation. Biotype A (PV interneuron / E-I imbalance / gamma deficit) integrates postmortem PV losses, reduced 40 Hz ASSR, altered aperiodic 1/f slope, and MRS GABA findings, implicating auditory cortex, DLPFC, and sensorimotor cortex. Biotype B (social brain hypoactivation) groups STS, FFA, right IFG, amygdala, TPJ, and mPFC signatures from fMRI, EEG event-related, and eye-tracking studies. Biotype C (cerebellar-cortical) covers right Crus I/II and lobules VI/VII abnormalities, supported by causal Purkinje cell mouse work. Biotype D (sensory perception / EPF) reflects posterior cortical hyperactivity, sensory over-responsivity, and salience network alterations from infancy, implicating primary sensory cortices, anterior insula, and OFC. Biotype E (mTOR / synaptopathy) groups the molecularly characterised single-gene autisms (PTEN, TSC1/2, SHANK3, FMR1, MECP2, NLGN, NRXN, CNTNAP2) with their distinctive EEG signatures. Biotype F (DMN / developmental trajectory) captures Bethlehem-Lombardo normative deviation profiles, long-range under-connectivity with local hyperconnectivity, and sleep spindle alterations in mPFC, PCC, and TPJ. Recommended additional headset anchors: right STS, FFA, right inferior frontal gyrus, and right cerebellar Crus I/II.
