Genomic factor loading (Nature 2025): **Substance-use factor** (shared with opioid, nicotine, and cannabis use disorders). The substance-use factor is the cleanest of the five Grotzinger factors in clinical terms because its four constituents share both reward-circuit substrate and a metabolic/pharmacogenetic layer that the other factors lack. Grotzinger, Werme, Peyrot, ... Smoller, Mapping the genetic landscape across 14 psychiatric disorders, Nature 2025 (doi:10.1038/s41586-025-09820-3, 1,056,201 cases; 5 genomic factors explaining ~66 percent of genetic variance; 238 pleiotropic loci). Factor-specific pathways for the substance-use factor are more specific than the broad transcriptional-regulation signal shared across all 14 disorders.

Alcohol use disorder is biologically distinctive among substance use disorders in three respects that recur throughout this document. First, AUD carries the strongest and most mechanistically clean pharmacogenetic story of any psychiatric diagnosis: the alcohol-metabolizing genes ADH1B and ALDH2 are protective by directly changing the aversive load of drinking, an effect that is anatomy-independent and ancestry-stratified. Second, AUD has the most developed neuroinflammatory case of any SUD, built largely by Crews and colleagues on TLR4/HMGB1/microglia signaling. Third, alcohol produces a regionally specific cerebellar atrophy signature that is disproportionate to other substances. Throughout, replication status is explicit and contested findings are flagged.

## MICRO scale (molecular / genetic / cellular / immune) vs GO-harmonized

The shared signal across all 14 disorders in Grotzinger et al. 2025 was enriched for broad transcriptional regulation; the substance-use-factor-specific biology that AUD loads on points to reward dopaminergic and metabolic axes. AUD-specific molecular biotyping centers on six axes: alcohol metabolism (the strongest and most distinctive), GABA-A potentiation (alcohol's primary pharmacology), glutamatergic/NMDA withdrawal hyperexcitability, dopaminergic reward with opioid (OPRM1) modulation, serotonergic 5-HT3 signaling, and a prominent neuroinflammatory/oxidative axis. Alcohol uniquely decreases striatal BDNF, the opposite direction to several other drugs of abuse, which is itself a candidate biotype-defining feature.

**GWAS anchor.** Zhou et al. 2023 (Nat Med) performed a multi-ancestry GWAS of problematic alcohol use (PAU) in 1,079,947 individuals, identifying 110 independent risk variants in within- and cross-ancestry analyses and explicitly implicating OPRM1 as a druggable locus pointing to naltrexone. Kranzler et al. 2019 (Million Veteran Program, Nat Commun) confirmed ADH1B as the strongest signal for both AUD and AUDIT-C across ancestries and added cross-ancestry transferability. Walters et al. 2018 (PGC, Nat Neurosci, doi:10.1038/s41593-018-0275-1) anchored ADH1B in alcohol dependence and reported genetic correlation with multiple psychiatric disorders. SNP-heritability is on the order of 10 percent on the liability scale, below twin estimates (~50 percent). The single most distinctive feature of AUD genetics is the protective alcohol-metabolism layer (ADH1B, ALDH2): these variants reduce risk by changing the aversive consequence of drinking rather than via brain reward circuitry, and ALDH2 rs671 is the largest-effect common protective variant in psychiatry, but it is essentially East-Asian-specific. Polygenic risk scores are too noisy to define a biotype alone, but the metabolic variants (ADH1B/ALDH2) function nearly as Mendelian protective markers in the populations carrying them.

## MESO scale (connectomic) vs Allen Atlas nodes + edges

Reference: Allen Human Reference Atlas 3D 2020 (141 anatomical regions). Functional labels and Brodmann areas are given in parentheses; the Allen anatomical container is named first. Many AUD findings are reported for functional networks (reward circuit, salience network, executive control) not 1:1 with Allen anatomical parcels. AUD maps onto the Volkow-Koob three-node scaffold (ventral/dorsal striatum, prefrontal control, extended amygdala plus insula), with two alcohol-distinctive additions: a disproportionate cerebellar atrophy signature, and a heritable, abstinence-stable EEG signature (reduced P3, elevated resting beta) recovered by COGA in unaffected relatives.

**Replication caveat (flagged).** Robust, cross-consortium nodes/edges: ventral-striatal cue reactivity, OFC/dlPFC hypofunction with reduced D2/D3 binding, anterior-insula craving coupling, cerebellar atrophy, and the COGA P3 endophenotype (the single most replicated electrophysiological vulnerability marker in psychiatry). The ventral-to-dorsal striatal habit transition rests largely on rodent causal work from the Cambridge (Everitt/Belin) group; human evidence is directional. Connectome-based predictive modeling of AUD craving (Garrison et al. 2023, transdiagnostic) generalizes across alcohol, cocaine, and food craving but uses modest samples without prospective external validation; treat as state-of-art directional evidence.

## MACRO scale (phenotype) vs SNOMED CT / HPO

AUD phenotype dimensions map to the DSM-5 criteria (impaired control, social impairment, risky use, pharmacological criteria of tolerance and withdrawal) and to the Koob-Volkow three-stage cycle. Historical typologies (Cloninger Type 1/Type 2, Babor Type A/Type B) capture an early-onset/externalizing-vs-late-onset/anxiety-driven split that maps onto serotonergic vs stress-circuit biotypes. HPO coverage of addiction-specific phenotypes is sparse, so several HPO cells are marked approx/needs curation and mapped to the nearest superordinate concept.

## Interventional studies (incl. neuromodulation, DBS, neuroplastogens)

The AUD pharmacopeia maps cleanly onto the molecular biotypes: naltrexone (opioid/dopaminergic reward, with the OPRM1 A118G pharmacogenetic story), acamprosate (glutamatergic normalization), disulfiram (aldehyde-dehydrogenase aversion, mirroring the ALDH2 genotype), ondansetron (5-HT3, serotonergically driven early-onset subtype), topiramate and baclofen (GABA/glutamate), and emerging neuromodulation (TMS for craving, DBS for refractory cases) and a neuroplastogen (psilocybin).

## Most defensible biotypes (cross-scale synthesis)

Five biotypes integrate cleanly across the three scales and map onto Allen anatomical anchors a Cytognosis-style spectroscopy headset could target. Three share substrate with other SUDs (cross-substance); two are alcohol-distinctive.

**Biotype A: Hyperreactive incentive-salience (reward-circuit).** Elevated ventral-striatal and OFC reactivity to alcohol cues with relatively intact non-drug reward, opioid-modulated dopamine release. MICRO: dopaminergic reward with OPRM1 modulation, decreased striatal BDNF. MESO anchors: striatum ventral (NAc), frontal cortex orbital part (OFC), anterior insula. MACRO: craving, binge/intoxication stage. Treatment lever: naltrexone (the fMRI cue-reactivity and OPRM1 pharmacogenetic story). Confidence MEDIUM-HIGH.

**Biotype B: Glutamatergic withdrawal-hyperexcitability.** Chronic NMDA upregulation and GABA-A downregulation producing withdrawal hyperexcitability, seizures, elevated resting EEG beta, high baseline glutamate. MICRO: glutamatergic/NMDA, GABA-A. MESO anchors: cortex/hippocampus (E-up in withdrawal), scalp EEG (elevated beta). MACRO: tolerance, withdrawal, delirium tremens. Treatment lever: acamprosate (glutamate-responder biomarker), topiramate (GRIK1). Confidence MEDIUM-HIGH.

**Biotype C: Stress-driven / extended-amygdala (negative-affect).** Elevated amygdala-BNST reactivity, high CRF tone, anxiety-driven late-onset (Cloninger Type 1/Babor A). MICRO: CRF/stress (downstream). MESO anchors: amygdala/extended amygdala (BNST), vmPFC, anterior insula. MACRO: withdrawal/negative-affect stage, late-onset anxiety subtype. Treatment lever: baclofen, stress-circuit modulators, possibly ondansetron in the serotonergic early-onset variant. Confidence MEDIUM.

**Biotype D (alcohol-distinctive): Neuroinflammatory / TLR4-microglia.** Elevated central and peripheral inflammatory markers (HMGB1, TLR4, NLRP3, IL-1beta, TNF-alpha, IL-6), microglial activation, oxidative stress, gut-permeability component. This is the most developed neuroinflammatory case of any SUD. MICRO: neuroinflammatory + oxidative axes. MESO anchors: hippocampus, anterior insula (interoceptive readout), vmPFC. MACRO: cognitive impairment, severity. Treatment lever: ibudilast and other anti-inflammatories (modest signals). Confidence MEDIUM-HIGH for the biology, LOW-MEDIUM for stratified treatment.

**Biotype E (alcohol-distinctive): Cerebellar/thiamine-vulnerable structural.** Disproportionate cerebellar (anterior lobe, vermis) and mediodorsal-thalamic atrophy, the largest brain-age gap among SUDs, thiamine-sensitive. MICRO: oxidative/mitochondrial, thiamine. MESO anchors: cerebellum, thalamus (mediodorsal), hippocampus. MACRO: ataxia, Wernicke-Korsakoff, cognitive impairment. Treatment lever: abstinence + thiamine; structural monitoring is the main lever. Confidence HIGH (structural), prevention-oriented.

A sixth, pre-clinical axis deserves mention: the **COGA P3/GABRA2 vulnerability endophenotype** (reduced P300 amplitude and elevated resting beta in unaffected relatives) is the most replicated electrophysiological risk marker in psychiatry and is detectable before any drinking, making it the strongest candidate for pre-onset, EEG-based risk stratification.

**Genomic factor statement.** AUD loads on the **substance-use factor** (Grotzinger et al. 2025), shared with opioid, nicotine, and cannabis use disorders. Alcohol is distinguished within that factor by its protective metabolic layer (ADH1B, ALDH2, with ALDH2 rs671 the largest-effect common protective variant, East-Asian-relevant), its GABA-A-centered primary pharmacology, its disproportionate neuroinflammatory and cerebellar signatures, and the alcohol-specific direction of striatal BDNF change (decrease).
