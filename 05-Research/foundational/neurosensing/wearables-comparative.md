# Comparative Review of Noninvasive Wearable Brain-Sensing Technologies

*Prepared for Cytognosis Foundation X-Labs Phase 0 proposal (Marie-Nelly / Cytognosis), May 2026.*

## Scope

This review compares three families of fully noninvasive wearable brain-sensing technologies that have reached either commercial or advanced research-prototype maturity by 2026: electroencephalography (EEG), optically pumped magnetometer magnetoencephalography (OPM-MEG), and functional near-infrared spectroscopy / diffuse optical methods (fNIRS / DOT). Each is evaluated on signal class, spatial and temporal resolution, depth, form factor, cost, leading vendors, and clinical / research applications. Implantable systems, fMRI, PET, and conventional cryogenic MEG are explicitly out of scope, since the proposal targets wearable use outside the scanner.

---

## 1. Electroencephalography (EEG)

### Signal and neural correlate
EEG measures voltage fluctuations on the scalp generated by synchronized post-synaptic currents in large populations of (mainly pyramidal) cortical neurons. The signal is dominated by sources whose dipole orientation is normal to the scalp; deep and radial sources are heavily attenuated by the volume-conduction problem (Michel and Murray 2012, doi:10.1016/j.neuroimage.2011.10.030).

### Spatial resolution
Spatial resolution is fundamentally limited by volume conduction and by the small number of independent observation points. Typical research-grade dense arrays use 64 to 256 channels; localization accuracy with state-of-the-art source reconstruction lands in the 1 to 3 cm range for superficial cortical sources and degrades substantially with depth (Hassan and Wendling 2018, doi:10.1109/MSP.2018.2810708). Consumer dry-electrode systems use 4 to 32 channels and provide much coarser localization, on the order of a lobe.

### Temporal resolution
Sub-millisecond, set only by the analog-to-digital sampling rate (commonly 250 to 2000 Hz). This is the best of any noninvasive modality and is the principal reason EEG is the workhorse for event-related potentials and brain-computer interfaces (BCI).

### Depth
Cortical only, and heavily biased toward gyral crowns. Deep sources (hippocampus, amygdala, thalamus, brainstem) contribute very weakly to scalp EEG; recovering them requires aggressive source modeling with priors and is widely regarded as unreliable for routine use (Seeber et al. 2019, doi:10.1038/s41467-019-08725-w report deep-source recoverability with caveats).

### Form factor, motion robustness, setup
Wet (gel) electrodes give the cleanest signal but take 20 to 45 minutes to apply for high-density caps and require post-session cleaning. Dry-electrode and semi-dry systems (e.g., Wearable Sensing DSI, CGX Quick, mBrainTrain, OpenBCI Cyton, Emotiv EPOC X, Muse, Neurosity Crown) drop setup to under 5 minutes at the cost of higher impedances and motion artifact. Active dry electrodes have narrowed the gap (Lopez-Gordo et al. 2014, doi:10.3390/s140712847).

### Cost
Research wet systems run $20k to $100k. Mid-range research dry systems are $5k to $30k. Consumer dry systems are $200 to $1500. Per-subject cost is essentially the price of disposable gel (a few dollars) for wet systems and zero for dry.

### Current leaders
- Research: BrainProducts, ANT Neuro, EGI/Magstim, Compumedics, Wearable Sensing.
- Wearable/dry: Cognixion, Neurable, CGX, mBrainTrain, OpenBCI, Bitbrain.
- Consumer: Muse (InteraXon), Emotiv, Neurosity, Flowtime.

### Major applications
Routine clinical: epilepsy diagnosis, sleep staging, intraoperative monitoring, brain death assessment. Research: cognitive ERPs, BCI control, neurofeedback for ADHD and anxiety (limited efficacy evidence). FDA-cleared digital diagnostics include the BrainScope Ahead family for concussion (low-channel dry) and several EEG-derived depression / ADHD biomarkers with mixed adoption.

### Key 2024 to 2026 advances
- Ear-EEG and in-ear sleep staging matured to consumer scale (Ko et al. 2024, doi:10.1109/JBHI.2024.3367540).
- EEG foundation models (LaBraM, BIOT, BENDR, NeuroLM) pretrained on tens of thousands of hours of multi-site recordings began to show transfer learning gains for clinical classification (Jiang et al. 2024, ICLR; Yang et al. 2024, doi:10.48550/arXiv.2304.06474).
- Closed-loop EEG-driven transcranial alternating current stimulation (tACS) entered larger psychiatric trials.

### Trade-offs and gaps
EEG offers the best temporal resolution and the lowest cost, but its spatial resolution and depth are intrinsically limited by volume conduction. It cannot meaningfully target subcortical regions without invasive electrodes. Its signal is not molecularly specific; it indexes synchronized synaptic activity only.

## 2. Optically Pumped Magnetometer MEG (OPM-MEG)

### Signal and neural correlate
OPM-MEG measures the magnetic field produced by intracellular dendritic currents in cortical pyramidal neurons. Unlike scalp EEG, magnetic fields are not distorted by skull and scalp conductivity, giving cleaner spatial reconstruction (Boto et al. 2018, doi:10.1038/nature26147). OPM sensors are alkali-vapor (typically 87-Rb) cells read out by a laser; they replace SQUID detectors and the liquid-helium dewar that defined legacy MEG.

### Spatial resolution
Better than EEG and approaching cryogenic MEG. Because OPM sensors sit directly on the scalp (a 6 to 10 mm sensor-to-cortex distance versus 25 to 40 mm for cryogenic MEG), signal amplitude is roughly 4 to 5 times higher and spatial resolution improves accordingly. Reported localization for sensorimotor evoked fields lands at roughly 4 to 8 mm with whole-head arrays of 64 to 128 sensors (Brookes et al. 2022, doi:10.1016/j.tins.2022.05.008).

### Temporal resolution
Sub-millisecond, comparable to EEG. Sensor bandwidth typically extends to about 100 to 150 Hz; new helium-vapor OPMs extend higher.

### Depth
Cortical with reasonable sensitivity, and the subject of substantial debate for subcortical sources. Simulation and a handful of empirical studies suggest hippocampal and cerebellar signals are recoverable under favorable noise conditions (Tierney et al. 2021, doi:10.1016/j.neuroimage.2020.117548; Barry et al. 2019, doi:10.1016/j.neuroimage.2019.04.013), but routine subcortical localization remains a single-lab or small-consortium finding. A 2025 simulation paper modeled laminar specificity but did not yet demonstrate it in vivo (Imaging Neuroscience, doi:10.1162/imag_a_00538).

### Form factor, motion robustness, setup
A 3D-printed or fabric helmet holds 32 to 128 OPM modules. Setup is 10 to 20 minutes. The subject can move and even walk, but only inside a magnetically shielded room (MSR) or with active shielding (matrix coils) that compensates the residual ambient field. The MSR requirement is the dominant deployment barrier. Recent work demonstrated walking and head-turning inside compact MSRs.

### Cost
Per sensor approximately $5k to $10k. A whole-head 64-sensor system runs $0.5M to $1.5M; the MSR adds $200k to $1M; matrix-coil active shielding adds more. Total system cost is therefore in the $1M to $3M range, an order of magnitude cheaper than cryogenic MEG but well above EEG or fNIRS. Per-subject cost is negligible after capital outlay.

### Current leaders
- Sensor vendors: QuSpin (US, Boulder), Mag4Health (helium OPM), FieldLine, Twinleaf.
- System integrators: Cerca Magnetics (UK spinout of the Nottingham group), Sandia, Hitachi prototypes.
- Research labs: Nottingham (Brookes, Bowtell), UCL (Tierney, Barnes), Sandia, NIH, Oxford OHBA (installed 2025).

### Major applications
Pediatric epilepsy localization (movement-tolerance is the key advantage over cryogenic MEG), dementia and Parkinson disease oscillatory biomarkers, developmental neuroscience from infants up. Wearable OPM-MEG has been used for evoked-field studies during free movement, including walking and ball games (Holmes et al. 2023, doi:10.1162/imag_a_00006).

### Key 2024 to 2026 advances
- Whole-head helium OPM systems with miniaturized electronics demonstrated in 2025 (PMC12006120).
- Pediatric whole-head arrays for early human life (Imaging Neuroscience 2025, doi:10.1162/imag_a_00489).
- Larger commercial installations at Oxford OHBA (June 2025) and other psychiatric / dementia centers.

### Trade-offs and gaps
Excellent temporal resolution with much better spatial fidelity than EEG, but it still requires a shielded room, so it is not yet a free-living wearable. Its signal, like EEG, is electrical and not molecularly specific. Subcortical sensitivity is improving but unproven at single-subject reliability. Capital cost remains high.

## 3. Functional Near-Infrared Spectroscopy / Diffuse Optical Tomography (fNIRS / DOT)

### Signal and neural correlate
fNIRS shines near-infrared light (typically 690 to 905 nm) into the scalp and measures back-scattered light at one or more source-detector separations. Differential absorption at two or more wavelengths is converted (via the modified Beer-Lambert law or via a forward model for tomography) into concentrations of oxygenated and deoxygenated hemoglobin. The signal is the hemodynamic response, the same neurovascular coupling that produces the fMRI BOLD signal, with comparable temporal lag of 4 to 8 seconds. Broadband NIRS additionally resolves the redox state of cytochrome-c-oxidase (CCO), a direct mitochondrial readout (Bale et al. 2016, doi:10.1117/1.JBO.21.9.091307).

### Spatial resolution
Standard sparse fNIRS uses about 30 mm source-detector separations and resolves brain activity at roughly 2 to 3 cm spatial scale. High-density DOT (HD-DOT) with about 13 mm spacing and multi-distance measurements achieves 13 to 16 mm spatial resolution, half that of fMRI (Eggebrecht et al. 2014, doi:10.1038/nphoton.2014.107). Ultra-high-density DOT prototypes at 6.5 mm spacing have improved spatial resolution a further 30 to 50% and improved decoding (Sci Rep 2025, doi:10.1038/s41598-025-85858-7).

### Temporal resolution
Sampling at 10 to 100 Hz is straightforward, but the temporal resolution of the underlying biological signal is set by the hemodynamic response, roughly 0.5 to 1 Hz effective bandwidth for stimulus-evoked work. The putative fast neuronal optical signal (EROS) would, if real, provide sub-100 ms resolution; see the deep-dive document for the replication picture.

### Depth
Standard CW-fNIRS reaches the outer cortex, with practical sensitivity at 1.5 to 2.5 cm beneath the scalp. Time-domain (TD-fNIRS) and frequency-domain (FD-fNIRS) systems extract photon time-of-flight, which gates light by depth and improves cortical specificity. TD-DOT pushes the depth envelope toward 3 to 4 cm under favorable conditions, with longer wavelengths (greater than 800 nm) helping. Truly subcortical readout in adults remains out of reach for routine fNIRS; the SOTA limit is approximately the top 3 to 4 cm. Diffuse correlation spectroscopy (DCS) at 1064 nm, time-gated, and massively parallelized speckle methods (SCOS, iDWS) are extending the depth envelope for blood-flow signals (Cheng et al. 2024, doi:10.1038/s41467-024-46537-9; Robinson et al. 2024, doi:10.1117/1.NPh.11.S1.S11505).

### Form factor, motion robustness, setup
Wearable caps weigh 200 to 500 g, set up in 5 to 15 minutes, and tolerate motion much better than EEG because they are not voltage-sensitive. They work in normal lighting (with dark fabric over the array), at home, and outdoors. Hair coverage is the dominant nuisance: dense black hair scatters too much light and remains the principal subject-screening criterion.

### Cost
Research CW systems: $30k to $200k. TD-fNIRS research systems: $200k to $1M+. Consumer systems (Mendi, Brite, FocusCalm): $200 to $500. HD-DOT prototypes built in-house cost similar to high-end research systems. Per-subject cost is near zero.

### Current leaders
- Research-grade systems: NIRx (US/Germany, recently joining forces with Artinis), Artinis (Netherlands), Shimadzu (Japan), Hitachi (Japan, legacy ETG-7100), Gowerlabs (UK, multi-distance HD), Cortivision (Poland, wearable), Rogue Research, Biopac.
- TD-fNIRS: PIONIRS, Kernel Flow (LA, deprecated Flux OPM line in favor of Flow), HORIBA modules used in academic builds.
- Consumer: Mendi (Sweden), Brite (Norway, Iceland), FocusCalm.
- Therapeutic adjacent: Niraxx Light Therapeutics (Boston, photobiomodulation wearables, not fNIRS sensing per se).
- Hybrid optical / ultrasound: Openwater (Mary Lou Jepsen, SF), HALO Neuroscience (defunct, not fNIRS).
- HD-DOT research: Washington University (Culver lab), Boston University (Boas), MGH Martinos, UCL (Tachtsidis).

### Major applications
- Pediatric language and developmental studies (the historical anchor application).
- Stroke rehabilitation, where motion tolerance matters.
- Psychiatry: depression and schizophrenia biomarkers using prefrontal verbal-fluency or working-memory paradigms; Japan's PMDA-approved fNIRS-assisted differential diagnosis of mood disorders is the most prominent clinical use of fNIRS in psychiatry to date (Takizawa et al. 2014, doi:10.1016/j.neuroimage.2013.05.126).
- Cybin / Kernel psychedelic-therapy biomarker work (Cybin Phase 1 with ketamine, 2023; publications on Flow1 in psychedelic dosing).

### Key 2024 to 2026 advances
- Whole-head TD-DOT systems reaching cortical functional maps (Imaging Neuroscience 2024, doi:10.1162/imag_a_00475).
- Ultra-high-density DOT arrays at 6.5 mm spacing (Sci Rep 2025).
- Speckle Contrast Optical Spectroscopy / Tomography (SCOS / SCOT) for cerebral blood flow with parallelized cameras (Commun Biol 2025, doi:10.1038/s42003-025-08915-x).
- Time-gated 1064 nm DCS demonstrating depth-resolved adult cortical blood flow with low-frequency oscillation analysis (medRxiv 2025, 10.1101/2025.08.31.25334647).
- Kernel Flow's TD-fNIRS platform applied in psychedelic biomarker work; Kernel discontinued its Flux OPM product line and refocused on Flow.
- Openwater raised additional capital in 2024 (~$54M reported), miniaturized stroke-detection visors, and ran investigator-initiated trials at UPenn and Brown (151 patients in LVO stroke detection); leadership transitioned to Aaron Timm in 2025.
- Foundation models for fNIRS data (interpretable transformer trained on raw fNIRS, arXiv 2506.22476, 2025).

### Trade-offs and gaps
Hemodynamic temporal resolution is slow. Spatial resolution is in the centimeter range for sparse fNIRS, sub-2 cm for HD-DOT. Depth is the single biggest limitation: routine sensitivity to deep regions remains absent. The signal, however, is molecularly specific (HbO2, HbR, total Hb, water, lipid, cytochrome-c-oxidase) and tunable by wavelength selection, which is why the platform is uniquely suited to a "zoom-in" personalized strategy.

## Side-by-side comparison

| Property | EEG | OPM-MEG | fNIRS / DOT |
|---|---|---|---|
| Signal | Scalp voltage from synchronized postsynaptic currents | Magnetic field from dendritic currents | Hemoglobin (HbO2/HbR), CCO; some blood-flow (DCS, SCOS) |
| Neural correlate | Direct, electrical | Direct, electrical | Indirect (neurovascular, neurometabolic) |
| Best spatial resolution | 1 to 3 cm (256-ch source-modeled) | 4 to 8 mm (whole-head OPM) | 6.5 to 16 mm (HD-DOT prototypes) |
| Temporal resolution | < 1 ms | < 1 ms | ~0.5 to 1 Hz biological (hemodynamic); fast optical signal contested |
| Practical depth | Cortical, biased to gyri | Cortical; subcortical contested | Outer cortex (1.5 to 2.5 cm CW); 3 to 4 cm (TD-DOT, DCS at 1064 nm) |
| Molecular specificity | None | None | HbO2, HbR, CCO, water, lipid |
| Motion robustness | Poor to moderate | Moderate (must stay in shield) | Good |
| Free-living use | Possible with dry caps | Not yet (needs MSR) | Possible (the strongest modality here) |
| Setup time | 5 to 45 min | 10 to 20 min | 5 to 15 min |
| System cost | $0.2k to $100k | $1M to $3M | $0.3k to $1M |
| Consumer products today | Yes (Muse, Emotiv, Neurosity) | No | Yes (Mendi, Brite) |
| FDA / regulatory footprint | Multiple cleared digital diagnostics | None as wearable | PMDA-approved (Japan) for mood-disorder differential diagnosis; investigational elsewhere |

## Why fNIRS-class spectroscopy is the most promising substrate for a personalized wearable platform

EEG and OPM-MEG measure electrical activity directly and quickly, but they have two structural limits that prevent them from becoming a personalized adaptive health-monitoring platform. First, neither carries molecular information: they cannot distinguish whether prefrontal hypoactivity reflects a metabolic deficit, a vascular problem, an inflammatory state, or a neurotransmitter imbalance. Second, OPM-MEG still depends on a magnetically shielded environment and EEG cannot reach deep brain regions even with the best source models. The signals they provide are powerful, but they are not enough to support the kind of "GPS for health" that Cytognosis envisions.

Diffuse optical spectroscopy, by contrast, is a chemistry instrument worn on the head. It already measures multiple distinct quantities (HbO2, HbR, total Hb, water, lipid, cytochrome-c-oxidase) using nothing more than the choice of wavelengths and timing, and recent work has added cerebral blood flow (DCS, SCOS, iDWS) and is moving toward true depth selectivity (TD-DOT, time-gated 1064 nm DCS, ultrasound-modulated tomography, wavefront-shaping prototypes). The form factor is the most permissive of the three: a fabric cap that sets up in minutes, tolerates motion, and costs in the hundreds to low thousands of dollars for a research-grade device. The signal can be made adaptive by changing wavelengths, source-detector geometry, and timing on the fly, which is the substrate the proposal needs for a coarse-to-fine "zoom-in" architecture. EEG can read the moment-to-moment brain electrical state and OPM-MEG can localize cortical sources, but only fNIRS-class spectroscopy can in principle answer "what molecule, where, how much, and how is it changing" from a wearable cap. That combination of molecular specificity, depth tunability via wavelength and timing, and a deployable form factor makes spectroscopy the right substrate on which to build a personalized, adaptive sensing platform.
