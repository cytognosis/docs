# Noninvasive Neuromodulation: Status, Personalization, and the Sensing Gap

Research brief prepared for the Cytognosis Foundation + Hervé Marie-Nelly NSF X-Labs Phase 0 proposal.
Last updated 25 May 2026.

## Executive frame

Noninvasive neuromodulation has matured from a fringe practice into a billion-dollar clinical field over the past decade. Four families of devices now hold US clearances or strong European authorization for psychiatric and neurological indications: transcranial magnetic stimulation (TMS), transcranial electrical stimulation (tES), focused ultrasound (FUS/LIFU), and transcutaneous vagus nerve stimulation (tVNS). Across all four families, response rates in real-world depression hover near 30 to 50 percent and remission rates near 20 to 35 percent (1, 2). Stanford's SAINT/SNT protocol, which uses functional-connectivity-guided individualized targeting and an accelerated theta-burst schedule, achieved 79 percent remission in a randomized sham-controlled trial of 29 patients (3) and 90 percent in the earlier open-label cohort of 22 patients (4). That result remains the single most striking demonstration that the field's response ceiling is set primarily by where stimulation lands, not by the dose or duration delivered.

The implication for the X-Labs proposal: the largest tractable performance gap in noninvasive neuromodulation is target personalization, and the technology now most missing from the loop is a wearable, continuous, individualized brain-state sensor that can survive outside an MRI scanner. A spectroscopy-class headset that maps each user to a personal mental-health coordinate is exactly the sensor that current modulation systems lack.

The rest of this brief reviews each modality, then synthesizes the sensing-paired landscape and what it means for the combined Cytognosis + Marie-Nelly platform.

## 1. Transcranial magnetic stimulation (TMS)

### Mechanism
TMS uses a rapidly changing magnetic field generated by a figure-of-eight or H-coil to induce eddy currents in the cortex, depolarizing pyramidal neurons and synaptic terminals at depths of roughly 1.5 to 3 cm. Repetitive TMS (rTMS) at high frequency (10 to 20 Hz) is excitatory; low-frequency rTMS (1 Hz) is inhibitory. Theta-burst stimulation (TBS) delivers 50-Hz triplets at 5 Hz; intermittent TBS (iTBS) is excitatory, continuous TBS (cTBS) is inhibitory (5).

### Spatial and depth specificity
Standard figure-of-eight coils stimulate a cortical patch of about 2 to 3 cm in diameter at depths up to roughly 2 cm. Brainsway's H-coils (H1, H4, H7) reach deeper structures of approximately 3 to 4 cm at the cost of focality (6). No TMS coil reaches subcortical structures with focal precision, and field strength falls off sharply with depth.

### Best psychiatric indications and current evidence (2024-2026)
TMS is FDA-cleared for major depressive disorder (MDD), obsessive-compulsive disorder (OCD), smoking cessation, anxious depression, and adolescent MDD. In treatment-resistant depression, the BrainsWay H1 coil's pivotal trial reported a 38 percent response rate sustained for at least four weeks, and real-world OCD response with the H7 coil reached 52.4 percent across 22 sites (7). Pooled meta-analyses of standard left-DLPFC high-frequency rTMS in MDD report response rates of approximately 46 percent and remission rates of approximately 28 percent (1).

The signal example for personalization is Stanford SAINT/SNT. In the open-label SAINT trial of 22 patients with treatment-resistant depression, 90.5 percent achieved remission within five days (4). The follow-up double-blind randomized sham-controlled SNT trial of 29 patients reported 79 percent remission in the active arm versus 13 percent in the sham arm (3). Sustained efficacy in repeated treatment was reported in 2023 (8). These results have not yet been independently replicated outside Stanford in a large multi-site trial as of May 2026, and the cohort sizes remain small. The field's confidence in individualized targeting therefore rests on a single landmark protocol plus mechanistic plausibility, not on broad replication.

Accelerated protocols without individualized neuroimaging have also been tested. A 2025 multi-site sham-controlled trial in São Paulo of accelerated 5-day iTBS reported response and remission rates of 52 and 34 percent in the active arm (9), substantially below SAINT's numbers, consistent with the hypothesis that targeting drives much of the difference. A 2024 head-to-head trial of accelerated iTBS as a substitute for ECT showed comparable response in moderate-severity patients (10), and accelerated iTBS has now been tested in bipolar depression with positive but small-sample results (11).

### FDA / regulatory status
- 2008: NeuroStar (Neuronetics) cleared for MDD
- 2013: Brainsway Deep TMS cleared for MDD
- 2018: Brainsway Deep TMS cleared for OCD
- 2018-2019: iTBS cleared for MDD on multiple platforms (MagVenture, Magstim, NeuroStar)
- 2020: Brainsway Deep TMS cleared for smoking cessation
- 2021: Brainsway Deep TMS cleared for anxious depression
- 2022: Magnus Medical SAINT cleared (510(k)) for treatment-resistant depression with individualized neuronavigation
- 2024: NeuroStar cleared for adolescent MDD; SAINT commercial launch by Magnus Medical
- 2025: Brainsway accelerated 6-day protocol cleared; Brainsway adolescent indication cleared (12-14)

### Personalization status
TMS is the modality where individualization has progressed furthest. Three approaches dominate:

1. Scalp-based heuristics ("5 cm rule," Beam F3): used in standard practice, ignore anatomy.
2. Structural MRI neuronavigation: standard at SAINT-capable sites, aligns the coil to an anatomically defined target.
3. Functional-connectivity-guided individualized targeting: identifies the patch of left dorsolateral prefrontal cortex (dlPFC) most anticorrelated with each individual's subgenual anterior cingulate cortex (sgACC), the operational principle behind SAINT/SNT and behind Magnus Medical's commercial system (15). Variants now include dorsal-attention-network anticorrelation and default-mode-network correlation targets, with comparable efficacy signals in TBI-associated depression (16).

The personalization step requires an rsfMRI scan and currently costs roughly $1,000 to $3,000 per patient on top of the treatment course. This is the bottleneck that a wearable optical sensor could address.

### Closed-loop / sensing-paired implementations
Brain-state-dependent TMS, pioneered by Christoph Zrenner and Ulf Ziemann in Tübingen, triggers TMS pulses based on the real-time phase of the sensorimotor mu rhythm in EEG. The original demonstration showed larger motor-evoked potentials when pulses arrived at the mu trough rather than the peak (17). Subsequent work has extended phase-locked TMS to motor cortex plasticity and to stroke rehabilitation (18). The Massimini and Casarotto group at Milan has used TMS-EEG together to compute the Perturbational Complexity Index (PCI), now used to gauge cortical complexity and disorders of consciousness, and 2024 work has extended PCI dissociations to minimally conscious states (19, 20). EEG-locked TMS for depression remains primarily an academic technique in 2026; no commercial FDA-cleared closed-loop TMS for psychiatry exists.

### Key companies and labs
- **Companies**: Neuronetics (NeuroStar), BrainsWay (Deep TMS H-coils), MagVenture, Magstim, Nexstim (navigated TMS), Magnus Medical (SAINT)
- **Labs**: Nolan Williams and Eleanor Cole at Stanford (SAINT/SNT); Michael Fox at Harvard/Brigham (sgACC connectivity targeting) (21); Zrenner and Ziemann at Tübingen (brain-state-dependent TMS); Massimini and Casarotto at Milan (TMS-EEG PCI); Nahas at MUSC; Daskalakis and Blumberger at UCSD/CAMH; Hummel at EPFL.

## 2. Transcranial electrical stimulation (tES)

### Mechanism
tES delivers low-amplitude currents (typically 1 to 4 mA) through scalp electrodes. The current is too weak to depolarize neurons directly but shifts resting membrane potentials closer to or farther from spike threshold, biasing endogenous firing (22).
- **tDCS**: direct current, polarity-dependent (anode generally excitatory, cathode inhibitory).
- **tACS**: alternating current, used to entrain endogenous oscillations.
- **tRNS**: random noise across a frequency band; mechanism less settled, possibly via stochastic resonance.
- **tTIS (temporal interference)**: two high-frequency carriers (e.g., 2 kHz and 2.01 kHz) intersect in the brain, producing a low-frequency envelope (10 Hz) at the intersection. The envelope can drive neuronal activity at depth while overlying cortex sees only the inaudibly fast carriers (23).

### Spatial and depth specificity
Conventional tDCS spreads diffusely across the brain. HD-tDCS uses 4 by 1 or denser ring montages to focalize current at the price of magnitude. Depth penetration with standard tDCS reaches only superficial cortex. tTIS is the first noninvasive electrical technique that demonstrates focal modulation of deep structures: Wessel, Beanato, and Hummel (Nature Neuroscience 2023) showed that theta-burst tTIS focally engaged the human striatum without modulating overlying cortex and improved motor skill learning in older adults (24, 25). A follow-up Nature Human Behaviour paper in 2024 showed striatal tTIS can disrupt reinforcement learning of motor skills, demonstrating bidirectional control (26).

### Best psychiatric indications and current evidence
- **tDCS for MDD**: The largest recent evidence is the EMPOWER trial (174 participants, fully remote home-based tDCS, 10 weeks). Active home tDCS produced significantly greater symptom reduction than sham, with 58 percent achieving remission by the end of treatment (Woodham, Loo et al., Nature Medicine 2025) (27). This is the registration data behind Flow Neuroscience's December 2025 US clearance.
- **tACS at 40 Hz for Alzheimer's and MCI**: Several 2024-2025 RCTs show target engagement and cognitive trajectory-level benefit (28); not yet approved.
- **tRNS for MDD**: A randomized sham-controlled trial of 4-week tRNS for depression found no significant differences from sham on mood outcomes (29). Evidence for schizophrenia negative symptoms is more positive but limited.
- **tTIS for psychiatry**: A 2025 feasibility study of tTIS for bipolar depression reported safety and provisional efficacy (Molecular Psychiatry 2025) (30). A 2025 Movement Disorders paper showed tTIS of the subthalamic nucleus reduced beta activity in Parkinson's disease (31). Systematic review of human tTIS now includes 820 participants across 20 studies through 2025 (32).

### FDA / regulatory status
- **Flow Neuroscience FL-100**: BSI and CE-marked since 2019 in the EU/UK; NICE-recommended in NHS pathways. FDA approval granted December 2025 as the first at-home tDCS device for moderate-to-severe MDD; US availability planned for mid-2026 (33).
- **Soterix Medical**: IDE for home tDCS trials; not yet approved.
- **Neuroelectrics Starstim**: research-use; some EU clinical authorizations.
- **tTIS, tACS, tRNS**: no FDA-cleared psychiatric indications as of May 2026.

### Personalization status
Individualized electric-field (E-field) modeling has moved from research to common practice. SimNIBS (Saturnino, Puonti, Thielscher, et al.) and ROAST (Bikson et al.) take an individual T1 MRI, segment tissue compartments, and compute the spatial distribution of E-field for a given montage (34). Most published trials still use one-size-fits-all montages (e.g., F3-F4 anode-cathode for MDD); only a minority of studies adjust electrode placement or current amplitude based on individual modeling. tTIS adds another personalization dimension: the carrier frequencies, electrode positions, and current ratios can be jointly optimized to focus the beat envelope on a chosen subcortical target. Recent biorxiv preprints argue that individualized tTIS optimization is necessary to reach claimed targets in real heads (35).

### Closed-loop / sensing-paired implementations
Closed-loop tACS that adapts to ongoing EEG phase is now demonstrated in laboratory settings, with Wagner and colleagues at Boston and several European groups publishing phase-locked tACS protocols. Commercial closed-loop tES is not yet available. The principal sensing problem with tES is that the stimulation itself produces large electrical artifacts that contaminate the EEG channels needed for state estimation, an issue that fNIRS-based sensing would sidestep entirely because optical signals are immune to the electrical artifact.

### Key companies and labs
- **Companies**: Flow Neuroscience (Sweden), Neuroelectrics (Spain), Soterix Medical (US), Halo Neuroscience (Halo Sport; defunct since 2020), Sooma (Finland), Ybrain (Korea), Innosphere (tTIS, Switzerland)
- **Labs**: Bikson at CCNY; Saturnino and Thielscher at DRCMR Copenhagen; Hummel and Wessel at EPFL; Grossman at Imperial College; Boyden at MIT; Loo at UNSW; Brunoni at São Paulo

## 3. Focused ultrasound (FUS / LIFU)

### Mechanism
FUS focuses acoustic energy through the skull to a small (millimeter-scale) volume in the brain. Two regimes matter:
- **High-intensity (HIFU)**: thermal ablation at the focus (Insightec ExAblate Neuro).
- **Low-intensity (LIFU, also LIFUP)**: mechanical, nondestructive modulation through ion-channel gating, capacitive membrane effects, and neuromodulation through unclear molecular mediators. Hypothesized substrates include TRP, Piezo, and mechanosensitive K+ channels (36). Sonogenetics (Chalasani, Boyden) uses engineered ion channels for ultrasound-specific actuation but is preclinical.
- **BBB opening with microbubbles**: ultrasound oscillates injected microbubbles to transiently open tight junctions in capillary endothelium, allowing drug penetration (Carthera SonoCloud).

### Spatial and depth specificity
This is FUS's structural advantage. With MR guidance and aberration correction for skull heterogeneity, FUS can reach a focal spot of a few cubic millimeters anywhere in the brain, including deep targets like the thalamus, subthalamic nucleus, nucleus accumbens, and amygdala. Depth resolution and focality are roughly an order of magnitude better than any noninvasive electrical or magnetic method.

### Best psychiatric and neurological indications and current evidence
- **MRgFUS thalamotomy (Insightec ExAblate)**: FDA-approved for essential tremor (2016), tremor-dominant Parkinson's disease (2018, expanded 2021), and staged bilateral PD (2023) (37). Not approved for OCD as of May 2026; multi-site trial for capsulotomy in refractory OCD is ongoing (NCT06131502), with long-term safety and efficacy data for OCD and MDD from a Korean cohort reported in 2024 (38, 39).
- **LIFU for depression**: A 2024 double-blind sham-controlled RCT of LIFU to left dlPFC over six sessions in MDD showed greater MADRS reduction than sham and increased rsfMRI connectivity between sgACC and medial PFC (40). Smaller open-label work targets the sgACC directly. The University of Arizona reported in 2025 that Openwater's Open-LIFU 2.0 device produced substantial depression-symptom improvement after under two hours of total treatment time targeting anterior medial PFC and the default mode network (41).
- **LIFU for substance use disorder**: Mahoney and colleagues at West Virginia University used LIFU targeting the bilateral nucleus accumbens for severe SUD with sustained craving reductions in open-label work (42, 43), and initiated a randomized double-blind sham-controlled trial in March 2024.
- **LIFU for anhedonic depression**: UCLA's LIFUP trial in anhedonic depression completed in April 2024 (44).
- **BBB opening**: Carthera's SonoCloud-9 for recurrent glioblastoma (Phase 3 SONOBIRD launched February 2024) showed 5.9-fold increases in carboplatin brain concentration and a one-year survival rate of 58 percent in an early cohort (45). Carthera and others are also investigating BBB opening for Alzheimer's.

The clinical evidence for psychiatric LIFU rests on a handful of small trials with heterogeneous targets and protocols; the effect sizes are encouraging but the field has not yet seen a definitive multi-site RCT.

### FDA / regulatory status
- **MRgFUS (Insightec)**: cleared for ET, tremor-dominant PD, staged bilateral PD.
- **Carthera SonoCloud**: investigational (implantable, requires craniotomy).
- **LIFU for psychiatry**: investigational; multiple IDE studies active in 2026.

### Personalization status
FUS targeting is inherently personalized at the level of anatomy because of the need for individual MR planning and skull aberration correction. The choice of target, however, is still group-level: most LIFU depression trials use the same anatomical target (left dlPFC, sgACC, or anterior medial PFC) across all participants. Functional-connectivity-guided individualized FUS targeting, analogous to SAINT, is the obvious next step but is not yet standard.

### Closed-loop / sensing-paired implementations
Closed-loop FUS is in early development. MR thermometry already provides closed-loop dosimetry for HIFU ablations. For LIFU, closed-loop sensing of physiological response within a single session has been demonstrated using simultaneous EEG and fMRI but is not yet packaged. Openwater's roadmap and TIESA collaboration (announced January 2026, anticipated patient enrollment early 2026) target AI-driven and closed-loop FUS neuromodulation of the default mode network for depression, anxiety, and trauma-related symptoms (46).

### Key companies and labs
- **Companies**: Insightec (MRgFUS ExAblate Neuro, Israel/US); Carthera (SonoCloud, France); Openwater (Open-LIFU, Mary Lou Jepsen, US); BrainSonix (LIFUP, US); Profound Medical; NaviFUS (Taiwan)
- **Labs**: Kim Butts Pauly at Stanford; Wynn Legon at Virginia Tech; Jerrold Tyler and Jay Sanguinetti at U Arizona / Sanmai Technologies; Yoo at Harvard; Mahoney at WVU; Beric and Mahoney's groups for SUD; Bystritsky at UCLA

## 4. Transcutaneous vagus nerve stimulation (tVNS)

### Mechanism
The vagus nerve carries roughly 80 percent afferent fibers from peripheral organs to the nucleus tractus solitarius, with projections to the locus coeruleus, dorsal raphe, and amygdala. Stimulating vagal afferents engages noradrenergic and serotonergic ascending systems and the inflammatory reflex, both relevant to mood, anxiety, and pain (47). Two noninvasive routes:
- **Auricular tVNS (taVNS)**: surface electrodes on the cymba conchae or tragus stimulate the auricular branch of the vagus.
- **Cervical tVNS (nVNS)**: handheld device against the lateral neck (electroCore Gammacore).

### Spatial and depth specificity
tVNS does not target a brain region directly; it targets a peripheral nerve and recruits brain-wide modulatory systems. There is no real "spatial specificity" in cortical terms. Effects on brain regions are indirect and slow.

### Best psychiatric indications and current evidence
- **Migraine and cluster headache**: Gammacore (electroCore) is FDA-cleared for acute and preventive treatment of migraine, episodic cluster headache, paroxysmal hemicrania, and hemicrania continua (cleared 2024) (48).
- **Depression**: Implantable VNS has long-standing FDA approval for treatment-resistant depression; noninvasive taVNS shows positive but small-effect-size evidence in meta-analyses (49). The iWAVE 2025 open-label pilot used accelerated taVNS in inpatients with depression and anxiety and reported safety and reductions in both symptom domains (50).
- **PTSD and anxiety**: Mostly preclinical and small pilot studies; recent murine work shows taVNS reduces anxiety-like behavior through anterior cingulate glutamatergic neurons (51).
- **Epilepsy**: Implantable VNS is FDA-approved; noninvasive use is investigational.

### FDA / regulatory status
- **Gammacore (electroCore)**: FDA-cleared for migraine and cluster headache indications.
- **Nurosym (Parasym)**: CE-marked in Europe; FDA Non-Significant Risk designation; 60+ trials.
- **Pulsetto**: consumer wellness device, no FDA or CE psychiatric clearance.

### Personalization status
Minimal. Most protocols set frequency at 20 to 25 Hz and amplitude at perceptual threshold. Some labs personalize using pupillometry or heart-rate-variability (HRV) responses as proxies for vagal engagement, but this is not standard. Closed-loop protocols paired to respiratory phase or to HRV peaks are emerging in research (52).

### Closed-loop / sensing-paired implementations
Respiration-gated and HRV-locked tVNS are the most developed closed-loop variants. Real-time EEG-locked or fMRI-guided taVNS is not yet standard.

### Key companies and labs
- **Companies**: electroCore (Gammacore), Parasym (Nurosym), Pulsetto, LivaNova (implantable VNS), SetPoint Medical (implantable VNS for autoimmune), Spark Biomedical (taVNS for opioid withdrawal, FDA-cleared)
- **Labs**: Bremner at Emory (PTSD taVNS); Frangos and Komisaruk; George at MUSC

## 5. Cross-cutting: sensing-paired neuromodulation

### The personalization gap
Standard left-DLPFC TMS for MDD achieves remission in roughly 28 percent of patients (1). Individualized functional-connectivity targeting in the SAINT protocol pushed remission to 79 percent in a 29-patient randomized sham-controlled trial (3). Even discounting the magnitude of the effect to account for small-sample variance and lack of multi-site replication, the principle that individualized targeting matters more than dose has now been demonstrated repeatedly in functional connectivity work. The Fox group's retrospective analyses showed that more negative DLPFC-sgACC functional connectivity at the stimulated site predicts better antidepressant response (53). Both lines of evidence converge on the same conclusion: where you stimulate dominates the outcome, and "where" is patient-specific.

### Why "everyone gets the same target" is breaking
For two decades, scalp-based heuristics (the 5-cm rule, the F3 EEG landmark) placed every depression patient's TMS coil over roughly the same scalp location. Anatomical and functional variability in the DLPFC, in coil-cortex distance, and in DLPFC-sgACC connectivity patterns means the actual cortical target differs by 1 to 2 cm across patients, sometimes more (16, 21). The "right" target for a patient with an anhedonic biotype may differ from the right target for a patient with an anxious-distress biotype (Drysdale 2017 originally proposed four such biotypes from rsfMRI patterns, though the replication picture remains mixed (54, 55)).

### What a sensor-paired system needs to do
A useful closed-loop noninvasive modulator must accomplish four things:
1. **Sense brain state**: continuously estimate a relevant neural feature (oscillatory phase, hemodynamic activation, connectivity profile, or biotype assignment).
2. **Infer optimal target**: map the sensed state to a personalized stimulation target and parameters.
3. **Modulate**: deliver the stimulation (TMS, tES, FUS, or VNS).
4. **Re-sense and update**: verify the modulation's effect on the targeted feature and adjust on the next iteration.

Today, the only modality that comes close to all four is invasive DBS with sensing, notably the Medtronic Percept RC neurostimulator with BrainSense technology, which received FDA approval in January 2024 for sensing and February 2025 for full adaptive DBS in Parkinson's disease (56). NeuroPace's RNS for epilepsy has provided responsive cortical stimulation for nearly a decade. Scangos and colleagues at UCSF demonstrated a single-patient closed-loop intracranial system for treatment-resistant depression with a personalized amygdala gamma biomarker (Nature Medicine 2021) (57); this is the conceptual proof-of-principle that the same loop applied noninvasively would be transformative.

### MRI-guided personalized targets
The Stanford SAINT model uses an offline rsfMRI scan to identify each patient's DLPFC target as the spot maximally anticorrelated with sgACC. Magnus Medical commercialized this approach with FDA 510(k) clearance in 2022 and commercial launch in 2024 (58). The bottleneck is access to and cost of MRI. Replacing the offline MRI with a wearable, in-clinic, optical or hybrid sensor that estimates the same functional networks is the obvious next step. The sgACC itself is too deep for fNIRS, but DLPFC activity, default-mode-network and frontoparietal-control-network signatures, and connectivity-network coordinates are within reach of multi-channel fNIRS, EEG, or EEG-fNIRS hybrids.

### EEG-guided TMS: brain-state-dependent stimulation
The Bergmann, Zrenner, and Ziemann groups developed real-time TMS triggered by the phase of ongoing oscillations (17, 18, 59). The clinical extension to depression is at proof-of-principle stage; one open question is whether the optimal phase target is itself patient-specific. Closed-loop EEG-TMS systems using Bayesian optimization to identify each patient's preferred phase have been published (60), as has reinforcement-learning-based closed-loop EEG-TMS (61). A 2025 study from the same general school reported that pre-stimulus mu power, not phase, predicts motor evoked potentials, complicating the picture (62). The replication tension on phase-specific effects illustrates the value of patient-by-patient calibration with a personal sensor.

### fNIRS-guided TMS and tES
fNIRS provides cortical hemodynamic activity with reasonable depth into superficial cortex, no electromagnetic interference with TMS, and a portable form factor. Reviews from 2019 onward describe concurrent fNIRS-TMS measurements (63), and a randomized study used fNIRS to navigate rTMS for motor hemiplegia (64). A 2024 meta-analysis aggregated fNIRS-measured cortical hemodynamic responses to rTMS across studies (65). The literature is comparatively small. No commercial fNIRS-guided TMS or tES product exists in 2026. This is precisely the gap a Cytoscope-class wearable would fill.

### MEG-guided targeting
MEG provides better spatial localization of oscillatory sources than EEG and has been used to define individualized TMS targets in research, but it requires a magnetically shielded room and a $2 to 4 million system. Optically pumped magnetometer (OPM) arrays are reducing the cost and footprint and may eventually become portable, but they are not yet a wearable closed-loop sensor.

### Implantable closed-loop systems and what's transferable
- **NeuroPace RNS**: detects ictal patterns in cortex, delivers responsive cortical stimulation, FDA-approved for epilepsy since 2013.
- **Medtronic Percept RC with BrainSense Adaptive DBS**: FDA-approved February 2025; senses local field potentials (LFPs) through the same electrodes that deliver stimulation, adjusts stimulation in real time. Indicated currently for Parkinson's; OCD and depression remain investigational (56, 66).
- **Scangos UCSF closed-loop DBS for TRD**: amygdala gamma biomarker triggered ventral capsule stimulation in a single patient with sustained antidepressant response (57). PRESIDIO and other multi-patient trials are ongoing.
- **Translation to noninvasive**: the architecture is identical (sense, infer state, stimulate, re-sense). The substitution is sensor and effector: noninvasive optical or electrical sensing instead of LFPs through implanted leads; noninvasive TMS, tES, or FUS instead of intracranial stimulation. The conceptual blueprint is already published.

### Recent 2024-2026 closed-loop psychiatry literature
- Zrenner and Ziemann, Closed-Loop Brain Stimulation (Biological Psychiatry 2024) (67): primary review of state-dependent stimulation principles.
- Provenza et al., closed-loop neurostimulation for OCD (Nature Medicine 2021, extended through 2024 with additional patients) demonstrated personalized symptom-state biomarkers triggering DBS.
- 2025 AJP review, "Personalizing Brain Stimulation for Psychiatric Disorders: From Circuits to Closed-Loop Control" (68): the field's most recent synthesis.
- 2025 EEG-Biomarkers-for-NIBS review (69): canonical EEG biomarker pipeline for individualized noninvasive stimulation.

## 6. Implications for the Cytognosis + Hervé Marie-Nelly proposal

### The missing piece is a wearable, individualized brain-state sensor
Across all four modalities, the limit on effectiveness in psychiatric indications is not power, dose, or device sophistication. It is uncertainty about where, when, and at what state to stimulate each patient. The single demonstration that closed that gap most convincingly, SAINT, required an offline rsfMRI scan that costs roughly $1,000 to $3,000 per patient and is only available at well-equipped centers. The field has spent the past three years trying to find ways to deliver SAINT-like personalization without an MRI scanner: with EEG biomarkers, fNIRS-guided coil placement, individualized E-field modeling from anatomical scans, and pupillometry or HRV proxies. None of these has yet replaced the rsfMRI. A wearable spectroscopy headset that estimates each user's connectivity-network coordinates, network state, and effective stimulation target would directly substitute for the MRI personalization step at orders-of-magnitude lower cost and with continuous longitudinal sampling that an MRI cannot match.

### How biotype-based zoom translates to personalized targets
The companion biotype documents being prepared for this proposal (for depression, anxiety, psychosis, PTSD, and SUD) identify candidate transdiagnostic biotypes defined on multi-modal features (connectivity, autonomic, behavioral). Each biotype maps to a hypothesized circuit target. Examples informed by the published literature:
- Anhedonic/reward-deficit biotype across MDD and SUD: target ventromedial prefrontal cortex, ventral striatum, nucleus accumbens (LIFU advantage given depth).
- Anxious/distress biotype across MDD, GAD, PTSD: target amygdala-DLPFC connectivity; potentially insula-anterior cingulate via tTIS.
- Cognitive-control deficit biotype across psychosis, MDD with executive dysfunction: target dorsolateral and dorsomedial prefrontal cortex; standard TMS targets.
- Threat hypervigilance biotype in PTSD: target amygdala downregulation via fMRI-guided LIFU or via top-down DLPFC TMS.

A Cytoscope-class headset that maps a patient to a biotype provides the "what" of personalization, while individual functional sensing identifies the "where." Pairing the two with TMS, tES, or LIFU operationalizes the SAINT principle for every biotype, not just for anhedonic-anticorrelated-DLPFC depression.

### The combined sensing + modulation roadmap
For the X-Labs Phase 0 proposal, the relevant architecture has three layers:
1. **Sensing layer (Cytoscope)**: a multi-channel optical (fNIRS) plus electrical (EEG) plus autonomic headset that estimates a per-user mental-health coordinate, including network-state labels and connectivity features that map to candidate stimulation targets.
2. **Inference layer (Cytonome)**: an AI model that maps the sensor's coordinate to (a) a biotype, (b) the optimal stimulation modality for that biotype, and (c) the per-user stimulation target.
3. **Modulation layer**: an integrated or paired noninvasive modulator. For Phase 0 demonstration purposes, this could begin with tES (the most portable and lowest-risk), with a planned roadmap to LIFU (the deepest reach) and to existing FDA-cleared TMS systems via interoperability.

The combined system addresses the central problem in noninvasive neuromodulation today: nobody knows which region to modulate in which person. A wearable, continuous sensor produces the answer.
